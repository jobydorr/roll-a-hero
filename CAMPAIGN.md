## 1. The Story

*The whole thing, short. Everything after this is detail.*

There was a world, and something began eating it from the edge. Not an army — an *appetite*. It came in from the rim of everything, and what it touched stopped being. So the greatest mapmaker who ever lived did the only thing he could think of: he took the world in his hands and **folded it**, the way you would fold a sheet of paper to bury a stain in the middle. The wild lands folded out into sealed pockets. The far places folded close together. And the thing at the edge folded *inward*, wrapped in layer after layer of world, until it was too deep to reach anybody. It cost cities. It cost towns, and roads, and people who were never found. **And it worked.**

He did not do it alone. His own order of mapmakers — his **Surveyors** — folded the world at his side, and they knew exactly what it was for. It was meant to be **temporary**: a wall built out of the world's own body, bought with blood, to buy time to find a way to kill the thing. Nobody ever went looking. Because up out of the wound came **starsalt** — glowing, and warm, and seemingly endless — and it lit the lamps and drove the mills and made the folded world richer than the wide one had ever been. And because the folded world was, for most people, on most days, *nicer*. Close. Safe. Warm.

Then the Foldwright learned two things. The Fold was **failing**: the thing inside had never stopped chewing, and every lamp burned made the walls between it and daylight a little thinner. And the woman he loved had been **swallowed into the creases** in the Folding, and was still in there. He went to his Surveyors and told them the design was flawed, that the world had to be unfolded and re-made, whatever it cost. **They said no.** They liked the world they had. So they took his order, his tower, his seal, and his name — and they put him into the creases with his own hands' work, and they told the world he had folded it out of greed and a hunger to be a god, and that they, brave and sorrowing, had **risen up** and wrested the terrible machine from him, and now hold it in trust. *For you.*

They still fly his sigil. They still speak his name every single day, because a tyrant in the dark is the most useful thing a ruler can own: every fence is his fault, every sealed road, every monster that claws out of a failing crease. And so the world has its villain — and the villain has spent a lifetime searching the folds for a woman — and the thing in the middle is four walls from daylight.

## 2. The World

### The folded world — and it is *not* simply better

Everything is close now. Your grandmother is a day's walk, not a season's journey. Trade is cheap, the roads are short, nobody starves crossing a wilderness. The lamps burn starsalt, and they never go out. Every monster and murderous wild place is sealed away in the **banished zones**, where it cannot touch you.

Most people, asked, would tell you the world is better. **They are not fools, and they are not simply wrong.**

But it is not better in every way, and everybody knows it:
- Cities were **crushed together** in the Folding, and towns went into the creases and never came back. Every family has a name they don't say.
- The world's edges are **grey and dead** — the seams — and the greyness is spreading.
- The monsters get out. **More every year.**
- And the road you walk on can be **sealed at a word**, by people who don't have to explain themselves.

*It is a smaller, safer, warmer, poorer, more frightened world, and it is running out — and that trade is exactly what the campaign is about.*

### How the Fold actually works

**Take a sheet of paper. Something terrible is eating in from one edge. Fold the sheet.** The bad edge ends up buried in the middle, with layer upon layer of world wrapped around it.

That's the Fold. It didn't build a wall — **it used the world's own body as padding.** The **banished zones are the layers.** The **Devouring** is in the **innermost crease**, and it never stopped chewing.

Every year it eats through a little more. That is why the creases are failing, why the breaches are getting worse, and why **no lamp ban and no fence will ever save anybody.**

*The players get this picture in Act Three. You should have it from session one, because it silently determines everything.*

### Starsalt — the "limitless" power

Sold by the Surveyors as **unlimited**. It is not.

It is **the world's own living essence**, pressed to the surface by the Fold. Lying in the ground it is still *part of the world*. **Dig it out and spend it, and that piece of the world is gone forever.**

**The seams are the mines.** The great grey dead zones at the world's edges — fenced, guarded, visible from a mile off — are where starsalt is cut. Everyone can see them. Everyone has been told the grey is *"the Foldwright's blight,"* seeping out of the creases.

> **The Act One reveal is not "there is a dead zone." It is "the dead zone is the mine."** The ugly fact sits in plain sight with a villain's name already attached to it — the same trick as the monsters.

And the party learns it **with their own hands.** They can't buy refined Surveyor starsalt, so they cut it **raw from a seam, with their fingers** — and the ground dies under their hands and stays dead. A grey handprint they leave behind, every single time they want a direction. Nobody has to explain starsalt to them. *They did it.*

**Why burning it matters, and why stopping isn't enough.** The world's strength is what holds the layers against the thing chewing through them, and starsalt *is* that strength. Burn it and the layers thin faster. So yes — every lamp in the world is helping the Devouring. But **stopping would not save anyone**: it is eating regardless, and a lamp ban buys years, not a future. *If a player proposes rationing — and a sharp one will — the answer is: good idea, it helps, it is not enough, and nobody with the power to do it will. Four heroes cannot ration a civilization.*

### Breaches

Monsters getting out. A crease gives; something with teeth walks into a wheat field. **Getting worse every year**, and everybody has an explanation:

- **The Surveyors:** *"The Foldwright's malice. More fences. More wrinkles. More obedience."* Then they ride in and heroically kill the thing — **which is precisely how they earn and keep their authority.**
- **The Stillguard:** *"The Foldwright's malice! Join us and fight him!"*
- **The truth:** the thing in the middle is eating its way out, and every lamp in the world is helping it.

Nobody says the true thing. *Breaches are your Act One clock, your monster budget, and your dramatic irony, all at once.*

### The banished zones — the layers

Sealed pockets holding the world's wild places. **Each portal leads into one, and each sits deeper than the last.** The party's journey goes *inward*; the dread builds geographically; **the final zone is where it lives.**

Some zones are monsters. Some hold **lost towns and lost people**, swallowed in the Folding and still in there. *That's where the heart is.* → these become `location` docs.

### Wrinkles

The Fold is a *living* thing. Whoever holds it can pinch a fresh crease anywhere — seal a road, quarantine a village, strand a party on the wrong side of a valley.

**Wrinkles cost a great deal of starsalt.** Big ones are events, not routine. *This is the limit on Surveyor power — otherwise they'd simply box the party in every session — and a party that learns the price can bait them into overspending.*

### Portals and the Unfold Rule

Every folded region has a hidden **portal**. To **unlock** a region, the party must open its portal and travel through it. **No region unfolds until every region is unlocked** — then the world unfolds all at once, because a folded sheet tears if you pull one crease while the others are set.

**Portals cannot be destroyed** — they *are* the creases. The Surveyors can hide them, fence them, and wrinkle the roads around them, but not unmake them. *That's why they never simply removed the problem.*

## 3. The Factions

### The Surveyors — the establishment

**They were his own order.** The Surveyors were the Foldwright's mapmakers — the people who folded the world at his side and knew exactly what it was for. That is why they carry his sigil, sit in his tower, and speak his name daily. They did not defeat a stranger. **They deposed their own master.**

Today they survey, fence, wrinkle, and chart what you are allowed to see. They own the starsalt mines. And they kill the things that come through breaches, in public, in good armor.

*Public line:* **"We took the Foldwright's machine from him. We hold it for you. Stay away from the edges. Buy your starsalt here."**

What the leadership tells itself:
- *"Unfolding means every monster loose at once, and cold houses, and no light."* — **True**, and the heroes have to actually answer it.
- *"We hold back the breaches. Without us, people die this week."* — **Also true.**
- *"We'll find another way before it matters."* — They stopped looking generations ago.

### The Stillguard — the "resistance"

Rallies, recruiters, banners: *"The Fold is failing! The Foldwright is a monster! Join us!"*

They never bring anything down. The portals are "lost." It is "not the right moment." They are "gathering strength."

### The truth about both

**They are one organization.** Same leadership, same purse, same starsalt into the same pockets. The Stillguard is a machine for **absorbing dissent** — anyone who senses something is wrong gets recruited, handed a mission designed to fail, monitored, and neutralized.

*Their name is the tell: they don't guard the world, they guard the* **still***ness. And notice what this buys the establishment — it does not* suppress *the alarm about the failing world, it* owns *the alarm. Secrets leak; a public, permanently-parked worry does not. Anyone who notices the breaches worsening already has somewhere to put that fear: "yes, terrible — the Stillguard are handling it."*

**Not all of them are rotten.** The rank and file are sincere. The Surveyor who kills a breach-troll to save a village *is* a hero and has no idea what he is part of. The rot is at the top. *That gives us sympathetic enemies, future allies, and a genuinely upsetting reveal for the kids: the good guys were the bad guys — but not all of them.*

## 4. The Cast

### The Foldwright

**What everyone believes:** a mapmaker who wanted to be a god. He built a machine that could fold the world and used it the moment it was finished — for limitless power, for safety, and above all to **own** the world by making himself master of distance itself. The Surveyors rose up and wrested it from him. He lurks in the creases still, and every breach is his spite leaking through.

**What is true:** he folded the world to shield it from the Devouring. It was a stopgap. When he learned the Fold was failing — and that the woman he loved was lost inside it — **he tried to undo it, and his own order refused him.** They deposed him, trapped him in his own creases, took his name and his sigil, and buried the reason.

**Why he needs the heroes** — four nobodies, when he is the most powerful mapmaker who ever lived:
- **He cannot walk in the world.** His is the most hated face alive; they would kill him and be cheered for it.
- **He is trapped in the creases.** He can move *within* the folds and slip briefly through weak places, but he cannot open a portal from the inside.
- **The Compass Spirit will not serve him.** He made it; it fled him. It woke for *these heroes*, and chose them, and that is the one thing he can neither order nor buy. He can only follow.

**Why he is both help and hindrance:** he *wants the portals opened* — she is in there, and every opened portal is a place he can finally search. He *dreads the final Unfold* — **it** is in there too. He genuinely wants both. *It isn't a trick; it's a man torn in half, and it's why the party cannot read him for two acts.*

**And he might still choose her over the world.** He nearly did once. In Act Three the heroes may have to talk him down — *that's a scene, not the finale.*

*How to run him: brief, and gone. He appears in the creases beyond a portal, never at the seams where the party harvests. He warns in* **complete sentences the party will misread** *— "You don't know what's on the other side." (They'll hear: the monsters.) He never trails off and never voices his real fear. A blank space is a signpost.*

### The Compass Spirit

A shy little creature — a piece of the world's living connective tissue, the thing that once held distant places together. It curls up small, like a firefly-fox. Fed, it unfurls into a **glowing compass rose** projected in the air or on the ground, pointing toward the next portal.

- **It drinks starsalt**, which is its own substance. Every direction costs a grey handprint.
- **Its directions are riddles**, never "go north." A symbol, a fractured image, a rhyme: *"the tower that drinks the sun," "where two rivers forget each other."*
- **The Foldwright made it, and it will not serve him.** It fled its maker and slept a lifetime rather than answer.

*This is the campaign's moral engine, and it's carried entirely by the players' own hands.*

### Still to cast

The **Surveyor leader** (who has laughed at the founders' sealed warning his whole life), the **Foldwright's lost love**, and the **sincere Stillguard recruiter** who brings the heroes in and will eventually have to face what she's part of. → §8.

## 5. The Threat

### The Devouring

In the innermost crease. Still eating. **Nobody living believes in it** — not the public, not the Stillguard, and **not the Surveyors**, though their founders knew and wrote it down.

*Generations on, the current leadership inherited the rule without the reason. The Devouring is a line in a founding document that reads like superstition — the sort of thing they assume their own predecessors invented as propaganda, because it's what* they *would have done. So nobody in power acts as though it exists, and that is what keeps the reveal sealed. Bank this payoff: in Act Three the party puts the proof in front of the Surveyor leader and* **he goes white**, *having laughed at that warning his entire life.*

**It is not a banished-zone monster.** The zones hold things with teeth. This is something else. *Name and nature TBD — but it must end up describable and fightable. This table will have spent twenty sessions swinging swords; the finale cannot be an abstraction or a conversation.*

### The banished-zone monsters

Ordinary, familiar, fightable: trolls, wyrms, wild magic, things with teeth. **This is the encounter engine** — the portals lead into the zones, and the zones are the dungeons. → `creature` docs.

## 6. The Arc

### The mission — why unfolding is not madness

**The Fold is coming down regardless. It is being eaten. Nothing stops that.**

*Do nothing,* and it snaps on its own: every banished zone bursting at once, into a world with no warning, no walls, no army in place, and no living memory of how to survive any of it.

*Unfold on purpose,* and you go through the portals **one at a time**, and in every zone you **get the lost people out, learn the land and what lives in it, find the old ways of fighting the things there, and mark the door.** Then the world comes undone into a world that **knows what is coming.**

> *"The wall is going to fall. We can be standing there ready — or asleep in bed."* **Say this out loud at the table.**

*This is the answer to the campaign's hardest question, it's what every region is* for*, and it must be in front of the players by early Act Two and never allowed to go fuzzy. The party isn't ticking boxes — each zone they open is a zone that won't be a massacre.*

### The opening hook — ten minutes, no exposition

1. **A breach.** Something came out of a crease and wrecked something they love. **Fight it in the first ten minutes.** No history lesson: a monster, a village, a reason to be angry.
2. **The Stillguard recruit them.** Join the resistance, find a portal, strike a blow at the man who broke the world.
3. **The mission was designed to fail** — like every Stillguard mission. Come back empty, feel useful, stay in the pen.
4. **The Compass Spirit wakes up.** Nobody planned that. It chooses them, off-script, and the pointless mission suddenly *works*.
5. **Their own side tries to shut them down.** *That's the first turn, and it's earned.*

### The three acts

**Act One — "unlimited" was a lie, and our own side is in on it.**
Breaches worsen, this season, where they live. The grey dead zones are **the mines**, not a curse. The resistance is a **leash**.
*Act One's turn is the betrayal and the mechanism — NOT "the world is dying." The Stillguard shout that at every rally; the party heard it on day one. It cannot be a revelation.*

**Act Two — the villain was trying to save us, and his own order deposed him.**
He didn't fold the world to rule it. He folded it to shield it, his Surveyors *helped him*, and when he tried to undo it they took everything from him and blamed him for it. He is alive. He is in there.
**And here the party learns the mission.** Closing question: *what was he shielding us from?*

**Act Three — the Devouring, and the man who would let it in.**
It's real, it's in the middle, it's been eating all along, and the founders' sealed warning was true. And the Foldwright **might still unfold right now, unprepared, for her.**
The Fold comes down. The only question was ever whether the world would be standing there ready.

## 7. Running It

### The load-bearing rules

Break one and something downstream quietly collapses.

1. **The banished zones hold ordinary monsters.** The **Devouring is categorically different** and is never hinted at as one of them.
2. **Every Devouring seed must have a satisfying wrong answer available.** Never leave a *blank* — a gap is a signpost. If the Compass Spirit is afraid, it's afraid **in a monster's lair**, so they think it fears the monster.
3. **Nobody living believes in the Devouring** — not the public, not the Stillguard, not the Surveyors.
4. **The Foldwright never explains himself** until Act Three.
5. **Starsalt is destroyed by being taken out of the ground and spent.** In the ground it is still the world. *This is what makes the finale a real sacrifice.*
6. **Wrinkles cost starsalt, heavily.**

### Why the propaganda holds

The stated story is that the Foldwright folded the world for three things: **unlimited power**, **permanent safety**, and **control** — the last being his crime, seized the moment his machine was finished.

**Two of the three are true, and the third completely answers "why did he do it?"** Nobody is kept in the dark about the danger — **the danger is advertised.** So when a player asks *"if we unfold, don't the monsters come back?"* the answer is **yes, obviously, everyone knows that, it's on the leaflet.** Asked, answered, closed.

> **The Devouring hides behind a satisfying answer, not behind a blank space. This is the most important structural fact in the campaign.**

### The paper map — the best prop we have

A real folded paper map with a **seal** (paper clip, wax dot, ribbon) on every crease. **Unlocking a region = taking that fold's seal off.** The map stays folded. The kids watch the checklist shrink and never once ask *"wait, is it unfolded now?"* **Last seal off = the finale.** Demonstrate the Unfold Rule by pulling one crease open while the others are set, and letting the paper tear.

### Tone and guardrails

Family-friendly with a **light edge, leaning family**. Spooky villains, real stakes, gentle dark themes — betrayal, loss, a comfortable lie. Cheeky humor that winks at the adults.

- **No permanent character death.** Heroes get knocked out, captured, or rescued — never killed off.
- **No explicit gore.** Nothing genuinely inappropriate for a nine-year-old.
- **No long exposition.** The kids get hooked fast; the story reveals itself through *doing*.
- **Not just battles.** Every leg wants a fight, a puzzle (the Compass riddles carry most of this load), and one moment where somebody has to decide something uncomfortable.

### Every faction is partly right — keep it that way

The **townsfolk** love the folded world and aren't fools; it *is* safer and warmer. The **rank-and-file Surveyors** genuinely save lives at every breach. The **rank-and-file Stillguard** sincerely want to save the world, and have been used. The **Surveyor leadership** is right about the cost of unfolding and wrong about nearly everything else. The **Foldwright** saved the world, and then grief nearly made him unsave it — *his jailers were not entirely wrong to stop him.* And the **heroes** are right that the Fold must come down — but the cost is real, and it will be paid by people with names.

## 8. Open Questions

| # | Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **What IS the Devouring?** Name and nature. | Needs a **face and a fight** by Act Three. Highest priority. |
| 2 | **How many layers/regions?** | Sets campaign length. Probably 5–7, ordered outermost → innermost, alternating monster zones and lost-town zones. |
| 3 | **The Foldwright's lost love** — name, and can she be saved? | No-permanent-death + *"if there's a loss, build the save"* → probably yes, and it should cost something. |
| 4 | **The Compass Spirit's name.** | Candidates: *Pip*, *Tesse*. |
| 5 | **The Foldwright's real name**, and the name of his order. | The Surveyors carry his sigil — it should mean something. |
| 6 | **The Surveyor leader**, and the Stillguard recruiter who brings the heroes in. | Two `npc` docs the opening needs. |
| 7 | **What happens to the lamps afterward?** | The finale asks a world to give up its power source. A hopeful answer is available — decide whether to take it. |
