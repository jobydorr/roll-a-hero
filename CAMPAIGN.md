# Campaign One — *The Folded World*

*The campaign bible. What is true, what the world is told, and what the heroes find out — in order.*

*Foundation laid 2026-07-11; revised twice on 2026-07-14 — the second time to invert the two rebellions, which is the revision this document now reflects. Prose follows `STYLE.md`; §1 is the reference implementation. Before changing anything, read §7, The Load-Bearing Rules — several of them exist to keep the Act Three revelation from leaking, and breaking one will quietly break the campaign.*

*One warning belongs at the front. **The players are not in this document.** There are four heroes with no stakes, no hooks, and no wounds, and until that is corrected we have a world rather than a campaign. It is the first open question in §8 and it outranks everything else there.*

## How This Document Is Organized

The sections are arranged to feed the DM OS rather than to duplicate it. §1 is the whole story in brief and explains everything that follows. §2, The World, supplies the `location` documents. §3, The Factions, and §4, The Cast, supply the `npc` documents, and the factions also determine the shape of most `encounter` documents. §5, The Threat, supplies the `creature` documents. §6, The Arc, supplies the `beat` and `scene` documents. §7, Running It, holds the tone, the guardrails, the props, the table vocabulary, and the rules that must not break. §8 records what remains undecided, and §9 is the glossary.

## 1. The Story

### The Calamity

Long ago, something began to consume the world. It did not invade from any direction, because it did not come from anywhere; it emerged from between — through the gaps in things, the spaces inside of spaces — and whatever it touched simply ceased to exist. It could not be fought in any conventional sense. Armies could not march on it, and no border could be defended, because it had none. Against an enemy made of absence, every power in the world was helpless.

### The Folding

The answer came from the most powerful wizard who ever lived, a man capable of reaching past the surface of the world and taking hold of the fabric beneath it. His reasoning was simple: if the enemy lived in the spaces between things, the only possible defense was to leave it no space. So he took the world in his hands and folded it, and folded it again, and continued folding until nowhere remained for the thing to be. The wild lands were folded away into sealed pockets. The far places were folded close together. The entity itself — everything of it that had already come through — was swept inward and pinned at the center of the Fold, the one point that no folding can close, because nothing can be folded down to nothing. There is always a center.

The working succeeded, and its price was paid in a currency nobody had thought to count. Entire cities went into the creases, along with towns, roads, harbors, and every person who lived in them. And because a memory is nothing more than a thread between a person and a place, and the folding creased those threads shut along with everything else, the world did not merely lose those cities. It lost the knowledge that it had ever had them. The names went. The roads that led there went. The songs went, or lost the verse that mattered. The grief went, mostly.

Because a fold that tight cannot hold itself, the wizard — known as the Foldwright — built an instrument to maintain it: the Armature, a geometric construct of nested rings and sliding cells, like a labyrinth that can be turned in the hands. The Armature is both a map of the Fold and the mechanism that governs it, which means that whoever holds the Armature holds the shape of the world.

### The Clean Folding

What the world remembers is a rescue that cost nothing.

The Folding was clean. The cities are all still there, the maps are complete, and the roads all go where roads have always gone. People did vanish in those years, everyone agrees, but that was the monsters, in the bad time before the Fold was drawn tight — and this is the half of the story that is true, which is precisely what makes the rest of it believable. The folded world genuinely is a paradise, the old horrors genuinely are sealed away, and a person who has lived their whole life in safety has no particular reason to interrogate the price of it, least of all a price they cannot remember paying.

The theft was not perfect, and the campaign lives in the imperfection. Here and there a person is left holding a thread with nothing on the end of it. A woman lays four places at a table for three children and cannot say why she does it. A man keeps a map in his attic that shows a good road to a city no one has heard of, and he cannot bring himself to burn it. A song has a verse about a harbor that does not exist, and everybody sings it anyway, because the tune is old.

These people are not believed. The world has a word for them and it is not a kind one. They are also, without exception, the only people alive who are right, and they are the entire recruiting pool of the resistance.

### The Surveyors

The Foldwright did not perform the working alone. His own order, the Surveyors, had spent their lives measuring what lies between one place and another, and they folded the world at his side with full knowledge of what it was for. The Fold was intended to be temporary — a way to buy time until the entity could be destroyed, after which the world would be unfolded and allowed to breathe again.

No one ever pursued that answer, because of what the folded world had given them.

Distance is the raw material of all power. Armies move or they do not; grain reaches the city or it rots; a message arrives in time or a year too late. In the folded world, the Surveyors control the movement of every soldier, every sack of wheat, and every word — and they can close any route with a pinch. No rebellion can ever gather against them, because no two angry towns can reach each other.

The Fold produced a second gift as well. Out of the compressed fabric of the world bleeds a substance called sunsalt, and there is nothing it cannot do. It lights and heats, but it also mends what should not mend, drives what should not move, and serves as the fuel by which reality itself is bent. Total command of distance, combined with a substance capable of anything, does not merely make a man rich — it makes him a god. This is what corrupted the Surveyors: the folded world could satisfy any ambition a person might hold, and so an emergency measure quietly became a permanent arrangement. It is the oldest pattern of power — a crisis justifies extraordinary control, and the control outlives the crisis.

### The Break

The Folding took its price from the Foldwright as well, and he paid it out of himself. He emerged from the working hollowed — a fraction of the man who went in, his own measure spent into the creases along with the lost cities. It cost him something worse than power. His wife, his children, and his closest allies were swallowed into the folds and never came out. He had folded the world around his own family.

He is not, quite, the only man who remembers. The working stripped the memory of the lost places out of the world, and it did not strip it out of the people who were holding the fabric while it happened, so his order came out of the Folding remembering everything, and wrote down what they had done, and sealed the record. But the Surveyors lost nobody they loved. He is therefore the only man alive who mourns an entire vanished geography, and there is no reason to believe that his exemption was mercy.

Grieving, and weakened for the first time in his life, he went to the Surveyors and told them the truth: the design was flawed, the thing at the center was still eating, the Fold was failing, and the world would have to be unfolded and remade, whatever the cost.

They refused. They were gods now, and they had never seen him weak before.

When the Surveyors came to kill him, he fled into the one place they could not follow — the Snarl, the deep interior of the creases, where nothing can navigate — and he took the Armature with him.

### The Standoff

That flight created a stalemate that has now held for a lifetime, and it rests on four locked conditions. The Surveyors cannot kill the Foldwright, because without the Armature the Fold tears, and the world tears with it. They cannot find him, because nothing can chart the Snarl. He cannot come out, because the moment he did, they would take the Armature from him. And he cannot repair the failing design himself, because he is hollow.

So he sits in the dark and works the Armature with the last of his strength, holding the world together — while the world curses his name for every tremor that comes when his grip slips.

### The Invented Tyrant

The Surveyors told the world nothing. As far as anyone knows, the Foldwright still rules. He is the unseen god-king, the man who folded the world and holds it in his fist; no one has laid eyes on him in a lifetime, and no one expects to. The Surveyors present themselves as nothing more than his ministry: they administer his Fold, they ration his sunsalt, they carry out his will, and they are ever so sorry about the fences. Every cruelty in the world belongs to him — every sealed road, every monster loosed from a failing crease, every tremor that cracks a wall.

But the Surveyors still need the Armature, and they cannot enter the Snarl to take it. So they built a rebellion.

They call it Green Fields, and its genius is that it does not attack the Fold at all. Green Fields loves the Fold. The Fold is why children play at the treeline, and only a lunatic would give that up. What Green Fields cannot abide is that the whole of it sits in the fist of one man nobody has ever seen, who seals a road when it suits him and answers to nobody, so that a person must beg a tyrant's leave to visit his own sister. Break the fist, keep the Fold, and the world could belong to everyone. It is a decent, moderate, popular position, and the people who hold it mean every word.

It also happens to require somebody to go into the folds and find the man, which is the one thing the Surveyors cannot do for themselves, and which is the entire reason the movement exists.

The rank and file have never heard of the Armature. They are hunting a tyrant. Their masters are hunting what he carries, and they will know it has surfaced on the day some cell comes back talking about a machine. Green Fields has never won anything and never will, because winning was never the point.

They invented a tyrant to serve and a rebellion to hunt him, and they own them both. Only they know the throne is empty.

There is one thing in the world they did not build, and it is small, and it is growing. It is discussed in §3 under the name the heroes will learn it by, which is Red Flag.

### The World as It Stands

And so the world has its villain: a hollowed old man in the dark, holding the world up with one hand and searching the folds for his family with the other — while the thing at the center sits a few walls from daylight.

## 2. The World

### The Folded World

The world is close, it is safe, and it is rich.

A grandmother lives a day's walk away rather than a season's journey, and the roads between are short and empty of threat. The folding tamed the wild, and the truly dark things — the old horrors that once ate whole villages — were banished outright, sealed into pockets from which they cannot reach anyone. Children play at the treeline. Doors go unbarred at night.

The world is also prospering as it never has. Sunsalt lights and heats and mends and drives, it does what nothing else has ever done, and every year some workshop finds a new trade to put it to. No generation has lived this well.

Most people, asked, would say the world is better than it was, and they are neither fools nor wrong. Nor do they believe they paid anything for it, because as far as the world remembers, the Folding was clean. What complaints exist are complaints about the present rather than the past, and there are three of them. The ground shakes: foldquakes crack walls, stop clocks, and drop bridges into rivers, and they come more often now than they did, though the Surveyors call it settling and insist it is nothing to fear. Where sunsalt is drawn out of the world the land goes gray and does not recover, and the fenced gray is a little wider every year. And any road a person walks can be sealed at a word by a tyrant nobody has ever seen.

All three grievances are real, all three are permitted, and all three point at the Foldwright. That is not an accident.

This is the trade the campaign is about: a close, safe, prosperous, shuddering world that has been robbed and does not know it, and something badly wrong underneath the floor.

### The Visible Fold

The folding is not hidden. It is the sky.

Look up from any street in the world and, past a certain height, the land comes back — hazy, inverted, immense, the underside of some other country hanging where the clouds ought to thin out. Light comes through it the way light comes through weather, and nobody has ever thought this strange, because nobody alive has seen a sky without it. Children are taught the shapes overhead the way children elsewhere are taught constellations.

In the older cities the fold above hangs close, and one can make out roofs, and roads, and the hands of a clock. Every city of any size therefore has a town above: a place its people have looked at their entire lives, whose silhouette they know by heart, and which they can never reach. Where the two hang closest they can very nearly see one another, and it has occurred to nobody to wave.

This does a great deal of work for the propaganda, and it should be understood as the propaganda's foundation rather than as decoration. The creases everyone can see are neat, orderly, and shallow — which is to say they look exactly like the tidy folded sheet the Surveyors print on every pamphlet. The diagram is therefore not merely a lie the world has been told; it is a lie the world has been shown, every day, since birth. And that is why the Snarl, when the party finally stands inside it, does not land as new information. It lands as betrayal.

### What the Folding Took

Three things went into the creases, and only the first is obvious.

It took places. Cities, towns, roads, harbors, whole provinces, and the people who were living in them at the time, none of whom were warned and none of whom came back.

It took memory, and the image to use for this is a thread. A memory is a thread running from a person to a place, which is to say it is a kind of distance, and the folding creased the world's distances shut without troubling to ask what was tied to them. The threads went into the creases with everything else. This is why the world does not mourn: there is no conspiracy of silence about the lost cities, because there is no silence and there are no lost cities, only a complete map, an unbroken history, and a population that would be baffled to be told otherwise. And it is exactly what the haunted are. They are the people left holding one end of a thread that goes into the fold and does not come back, and who cannot say what is on the other end of it, and cannot put it down.

And it took ideas, which is the strangest of the three and the one to spend most carefully. Some things the world knew are simply not known anymore, and nobody can miss them, because a person cannot notice the absence of a thought they have never had. The Foldwright's own art is the clearest case. Nobody living but him can fold anything or explain how it was ever done, and the Surveyors who pinch wrinkles into the world do it the way a child copies letters in a language nobody speaks — the shapes are right, the hand is steady, and the meaning is gone. They know they lost the craft, because they alone remember having had it, and they have built a doctrine out of pretending it was always this way.

Against all of this stands one exception, and the campaign runs on it. The Compass Spirit is the world's memory of how the places used to touch, which means it is the only thing left that remembers — and it can give a memory back. That is discussed in §4, and it is the party's evidence.

### What Everyone Believes the Fold Is

The Surveyors print the same diagram on every pamphlet. The world is a sheet of paper, folded over on itself to wall the darkness out — a barrier, with the danger safely on the far side of it.

The diagram is teachable, memorable, corroborated by the sky, and wrong. There is no far side. There never was. Nobody alive understands what the Fold actually is: not the public, not either rebellion, and not the Surveyors themselves, who administer a machine they cannot read and long ago mistook their own diagram for the thing it described. The only people who ever knew were the ones who performed the Folding, and they are dead, deposed, or lost inside it.

### What the Fold Actually Did

The Devouring did not come from anywhere, and so it could not be shut out. It comes through the gaps in things, the spaces inside of spaces, and nothing can be walled out that is already present between every two grains of sand. The Foldwright's answer was therefore not to build a barrier but to eliminate the vacancy: he compressed the world until there was nowhere left for the thing to be.

For a child at the table, the image that carries this instantly is bugs in a blanket. You cannot kill them, but you can wad the blanket so tightly that there is nowhere left for them to sit — except one lump in the middle.

That lump is the center, and it is the flaw in the design. Nothing can be folded down to nothing; some space always survives at the core. Everything of the entity that had already entered the world was swept inward and pinned there, and it has been eating outward ever since. It eats because eating makes room, and room is the only thing it needs in order to live.

### The Snarl

The interior of the Fold is not a stack of neat layers. Crumple the paper and then force the wad into a box until it cinches tight, and the result is closer to the truth: a dense, convoluted, doubled and redoubled labyrinth packed into a shape, with the texture of a brain squeezed into a geometry. There are chambers within chambers, passages that fold back through themselves, and rooms larger inside than the space that appears to contain them.

The banished zones are the cells of this structure, and each cell is itself densely folded, which gives them their character: a forest that loops back on itself, a town in which the same street occurs three times, a mountain whose interior is larger than its exterior. Depth still means proximity to the center, but the route inward is not a line and cannot be reasoned out from the outside.

Two things in the world can navigate the Snarl, and no third. The first is the Armature, which is a map of the Fold as well as its mechanism, and which tells the man who holds it where the cells lie without telling him what is inside any of them — so he can walk the labyrinth for a lifetime and never find a door he is able to open from within. The second is the Compass Spirit, which is the world's connective tissue and the only thing that still remembers how the separated places used to touch. Everyone else who has gone in has simply wandered until they stopped. That fact also does quiet work for Green Fields, whose expeditions into the folds were designed to fail and would have failed regardless.

### The Cinch

The world is folded as tightly as folding goes. Past a certain density no further crease will take, and the Foldwright called that limit the cinch. He folded the world deliberately to it, because reaching the cinch was the entire point: no slack, no gaps, no room.

Four consequences follow, and each of them matters.

Nobody can fold their way out of this, because there is no room left to fold into. This forecloses the obvious question — why not simply fold it again — with a hard physical answer rather than an evasion.

The Surveyors pinch wrinkles into an over-packed structure they cannot read, at the point of its greatest strain, and they believe themselves to be careful.

The Devouring eats, and eating produces slack, and slack is space. A breach is therefore not a wall giving way. It is the thing at the center making room for itself, and something with teeth wandering out through the gap it left.

And the Fold holds together at all only because one hollowed old man is still working the Armature. Foldquakes are his grip slipping, and they will worsen as he weakens. They will also ease as the heroes restore what he lost, which means the party can read its own progress in the steadiness of the ground without anyone ever explaining the connection.

The cruelty at the heart of the arrangement is that the lamps and the thing at the center are doing the same work from opposite ends. Every measure of sunsalt burned is a little more room handed to it, and humanity is helping the entity one warm evening at a time.

### Sunsalt

Sunsalt is rationed by the Surveyors in the Foldwright's name and sold as limitless. It is neither limitless nor a gift.

It does very nearly anything. It lights and heats and mends, it drives what should not move and holds up what should have fallen, and it is the fuel by which reality itself is bent — every wrinkle the Surveyors pinch is paid for in sunsalt. This is not lamp oil, and understanding that is necessary to understand why the Surveyors will never willingly let go of it.

What sunsalt actually is, is the living essence of the world, bled to the surface by the compression. While it remains in the ground it is still part of the world. Taken out and spent, that piece of the world is gone permanently, and the vacancy it leaves belongs to the thing at the center.

It is not dug but drawn. Where the folding bit deepest the fabric shows through — a seam — and from a seam the world's essence can be bled up and left to crystallize, in the manner of brine in a salt pan. The workings are called the Draws: broad glowing flats, fenced and guarded and visible from a mile away, with Surveyor crews scraping the crust. Around every worked Draw the land is gray and dead, and the gray is spreading. Everyone can see this, and everyone has been told that it is the Foldwright's blight, seeping up out of the creases.

The Act One revelation is therefore not that a dead zone exists, which is common knowledge, but that the dead zone is the Draw. The ugly fact has been sitting in plain sight the whole time with a villain's name already attached to it, which is the same misdirection the campaign uses for the monsters.

The party will learn this with their own hands. Refined Surveyor sunsalt is beyond their reach, so they bleed it raw from a seam with their fingers, and the ground dies beneath their hands and stays dead. They leave a gray handprint behind every time they want a direction from the Compass Spirit, and another every time they want a memory back. Nobody will need to explain sunsalt to them, because they will have done it themselves.

A player will eventually propose the obvious remedy, which is to burn less. The honest answer is that rationing would help, that it would not save anyone, and that it is not on the menu in any case. The entity is making its own room regardless, so restraint buys years rather than a future; and a civilization runs on this substance, the Surveyors are gods because of it, and four heroes cannot ration a world. Red Flag reached the same conclusion by a harder road and drew the only remaining inference, which is that the seams exist because the world is folded, so the burning stops for good on the day the world is unfolded and not one day sooner. That is why the faction that hates sunsalt most is also the faction that wants every door opened, and it is why the party's own harvesting is forgivable to them: a handful of gray handprints now, to end the Draws forever, is an argument they have already had with themselves and won. It is also exactly the kind of argument that turns out to be wrong.

### Breaches

A crease gives, and something with teeth walks into a wheat field. Breaches are worsening every year, and everyone has an explanation for them.

The Surveyors say that the Foldwright permits them, that they plead with him on the world's behalf, and that with more fences and more wrinkles and more obedience they will plead again. Then they ride out and kill the thing in public, wearing good armor, which is precisely how they earn and keep their authority. It allows them to appear as the reluctant administrators softening a tyrant's rule, and it is a very good racket.

Green Fields says that the breaches are the tyrant's negligence, that a man who can seal a road at breakfast could seal a crease if he cared to, and that the world should stop asking him nicely.

The truth is that the entity is making room for itself, and that every lamp in the world is helping it.

Because breaches are visible, local, urgent, and fightable, they do three jobs at once: they supply the campaign's early pressure, they supply its monsters, and they supply its central irony, since the people blaming the Foldwright are themselves feeding the thing that causes them.

### The Banished Zones

The banished zones are sealed pockets holding the world's wild places. Each portal opens into one, and each sits deeper than the last. Some contain monsters. Some contain lost towns and lost people, swallowed in the Folding and still inside. And some contain what the Foldwright lost, which is discussed in §4.

### Wrinkles

The Fold is a living thing, and a fresh crease can be pinched into it anywhere — sealing a road, quarantining a village, stranding a party on the wrong side of a valley. This is the entire basis of Surveyor power. No army marches, no cargo arrives, and no rebellion gathers without their leave.

Pinching a wrinkle does not require the Armature, and the distinction matters. A wrinkle is a crude local crease made by hand at ruinous cost in sunsalt, and the Surveyors can make one anywhere they please. The Armature is the instrument that holds the whole shape together, and they do not have it. So they can fold a road shut and they cannot stop the ground shaking, which is the exact condition of a man who can slam a door in a burning house.

Because the Surveyors wrinkle in the Foldwright's name, a road slamming shut in front of the heroes will read to them as the tyrant hunting them personally. He is not. He is helping them, and the misreading is available to run for two full acts.

Wrinkles cost a great deal of sunsalt, which is what limits their use. Large ones are events rather than routine, and a party that learns the price can bait the Surveyors into overspending.

### Portals and the Unfold Rule

Every folded region has a hidden portal, and a portal is not a door. It is a place where the crease shows, which is to say a place where two things that should line up do not: a garden wall whose stones do not match across a single course, a stream that runs into a hillside and never comes out the other side, a stair with one step too many that nobody in the house has ever counted. The world is full of such places and everybody has walked past one, because a thing that does not line up is not frightening, it is merely shoddy. The Compass Spirit's riddles are riddles precisely because this is what it has to describe.

To unlock a region the party must open its portal and travel through it, and no region can unfold until every region has been unlocked, at which point the world unfolds all at once. The reason is mechanical: a folded sheet tears if one crease is pulled open while the others remain set.

Portals cannot be destroyed, because they are the creases themselves. The Surveyors can hide them, fence them, and wrinkle the roads around them, but they cannot unmake them, which is why the problem has never simply been removed.

## 3. The Factions

### How All Three Are Built

Three organizations matter in this world, and all three are built the same way, which is the most useful single fact to hold while running them. Each has a leadership that knows what the organization is actually for, and a rank and file that carries out its orders while believing something else entirely. The gap between those two layers is not incidental decoration. It is where every betrayal in this campaign lives, and every sympathetic enemy, and every ally the party will ever recruit.

The practical consequence is that "what does this faction want" is a question with two answers every time, and it should never be given one. It also means that almost everyone the heroes fight is decent, and almost everyone who gives an order is not.

### The Surveyors

The Surveyors are the empire, and they have never needed to call themselves one. They were the Foldwright's own order. They had spent their lives measuring what lies between one place and another, they folded the world at his side, and they knew exactly what it was for. They did not defeat a stranger and seize his invention; they deposed their own master and then continued to rule in his name.

What their leadership holds is not a throne but distance itself: every army's march, every harvest's journey, every message between two angry towns, closable with a pinch. Together with sunsalt, which does very nearly anything, this makes them less a government than a pantheon. They are not officials who grew greedy. They are gods who were asked to stop being gods and declined.

What they lack is the Armature, and the shape of their want is precise. With it they could end the deadlock in an afternoon: still the ground, kill the man at last, and hold the world outright and permanently rather than through a hundred-year fiction. Without it they can seal a road and they cannot stop the shaking, which is the exact condition of a man who can slam a door in a burning house. They have been hunting that machine for a lifetime. Everything they do points at it.

They also lack the craft. The knowledge of how the world was folded went into the creases along with the cities, so the order that governs the Fold cannot read it, cannot repair it, and pinches its wrinkles by rote — a priesthood performing a rite whose grammar it has forgotten. Because they were holding the fabric when it closed, they are the only people in the world who remember having once known how, which means they are not innocently ignorant but knowingly diminished, and they have built a doctrine out of pretending it was always this way. It is the second-worst thing that ever happened to them and they will not discuss it.

Their rank and file are the law. They wear good armor, they keep the roads, they ride out and kill the thing in the wheat field in public, and they believe in the god-king as completely as anyone alive. They are also the men who arrest Green Fields organizers as anarchists and terrorists, and they are not wrong to think them dangerous, because a movement that wants to loosen the world's only defense is dangerous — it is simply that the movement is their own employer's. The Surveyor who saves a village on Tuesday and breaks up a rally on Thursday is a hero twice in his own mind and an instrument twice in fact.

In public the order presents itself as nothing more than a ministry. They administer the Fold, they ration the sunsalt, they carry out the tyrant's will, and they are ever so sorry about the fences. Their standing line is that the Foldwright wills it, that they only serve, that the world should take its ration and keep off the seams, and that they will plead with him again on the world's behalf.

They keep him on the throne rather than ruling openly for three mutually reinforcing reasons. His name carries absolute authority, because nobody would kneel to a committee of surveyors while everybody kneels to the man who folded the world. He absorbs every cruelty, so that each fence, each sealed road, and each spreading gray mile belongs to him rather than to them. And he gives the world's anger somewhere to go, which is the function of Green Fields.

The leadership justifies itself with arguments, two of which are true. Unfolding would release everything in the zones at once and leave the world cold and dark, and the heroes will have to answer that honestly. The Surveyors do hold back the breaches, and without them people would die this week. They also tell themselves that another solution will be found before it matters, which they have been saying for generations and have long since stopped pursuing. Beneath all three sits the argument they never say aloud, which is that unfolding would require them to become men again.

### Green Fields

Green Fields is the rebellion, and it is the establishment's finest work.

Its platform is not that the Fold was a crime. That is the crucial thing about it, and it is what makes the whole arrangement stable. Green Fields loves the Fold. The Fold is the reason children play at the treeline and doors go unbarred, and no sane person wants that undone. What Green Fields cannot accept is that the whole of it — every road, every harvest, every word between two towns — should sit in the fist of one man nobody has ever seen, who fences what he likes, seals what he likes, and answers to nobody. The world should not have to ask a tyrant's leave to walk to the next village. Break the fist, and the Fold could belong to everyone.

Every word of that is a decent, moderate, deeply reasonable position, and the people who hold it are sincere. They are also, in the eyes of the law that hunts them, anarchists and terrorists agitating to loosen the world's only defense — and the law is not being cynical when it says so.

The organization's leadership is the Surveyor leadership. Not an ally of it, not a wing of it; the same room, the same purse, the same sunsalt flowing into the same pockets. They built the rebellion because they needed the one thing they could not do themselves, which is send people into the folds after the Foldwright. So Green Fields preaches that the tyrant hides inside his own work and rules the world from a place no honest man can reach, and that he must be found and his grip broken — and it points its bravest at the creases and calls the result a liberation movement.

The rank and file have never heard of the Armature. They are hunting a man. Their masters are hunting what he carries, and they will know it has surfaced on the day some cell comes back talking about a machine.

The cruelty in it goes further than the deception, and it should be played rather than merely noted. A rebellion with no martyrs is not believable, so the leadership lets its own sincere members be arrested, hunted, and killed by its own law enforcement, and then it grieves them in public and recruits on the strength of the grief. Both halves of that transaction are staffed by honest people. Nobody in the square knows that the raid and the rally were ordered by the same hand.

Green Fields also competes for the haunted, and it gets to them first. This is the quietest cruelty in the campaign. A woman who lays four places for three children is told that her wound is the tyrant's doing, that the fences and the fear and the empty chair all come from the same fist, and that helping to break it will make her whole. It is a lie of omission and it is close enough to true to hold: the tyrant did not take her fourth child, the Fold did, and the Fold is the thing Green Fields exists to preserve. She will spend years fighting for the thing that robbed her, and she will be brave about it.

### What Is True of Both

The Surveyors and Green Fields are one organization wearing two coats, and only the leadership knows it. They invented a tyrant to serve and a rebellion to hunt him, and they own them both. Only they know the throne is empty.

The arrangement does three jobs at once, which is why it is such an efficient machine. It searches the Snarl. It absorbs every angry person in the world and marches them in a circle. And it owns the alarm, which is the subtlest of the three: the establishment does not suppress fear about the failing world, it broadcasts that fear itself, through the mouths of people who mean it. Secrets leak, but a publicly acknowledged and permanently parked worry does not. Anyone who notices the shakes worsening already has somewhere to put the feeling, which is that Green Fields are on it.

The corruption is entirely at the top of both, which is what gives this campaign sympathetic enemies, plausible future allies, and a reveal that lands as upsetting rather than clever. The good guys were the bad guys, but not all of them, and not the ones the party will have come to love.

### Red Flag

Red Flag is the only real thing in the world, and it is small, and it is growing.

It began where a lie of that size always begins to come apart, which is with somebody who could count. Hettie Sarn ran the Cinder Draw for thirty-one years and personally scraped more sunsalt out of the world than any living person. She is not a mystic and she does not make speeches. She keeps a ledger — the gray ledger, drawn year by year, showing the exact rate at which the dead land has advanced across ground she used to farm — and her method of recruitment is to open it, turn it around, and wait. She is the world's foremost authority on the damage because she did the damage, which means she cannot be dismissed as an idealist, cannot be bought, and is the one person in the world who would most like to be proven wrong.

What the ledger showed her is the thread the whole thing unravels from. The gray is not spreading outward from the creases, as everyone has been told for a lifetime. It is spreading outward from the workings. The blight has a fence around it, and a payroll, and a schedule. And once a person has seen that the establishment is manufacturing the disaster it blames on the tyrant, it takes very little to see that it is manufacturing the opposition too.

So Red Flag knows what nobody else outside a sealed room knows: that the Surveyors and Green Fields are one hand in two gloves, that the sunsalt everyone calls a gift is the world being sold back to itself by the lampful, and that the creases have doors in them. Their conclusion follows from all three and it is not moderate. The Fold is not a home that needs better management. It is a wound that has been kept open for a lifetime because keeping it open is profitable, and the seams exist only because the world is folded, so there is exactly one way to stop the burning forever, which is to unfold the world and let it be what it was. Open every door. Set it right. Give it back.

They live by that in the plainest way available. An Unlit house — their own word for themselves before Red Flag was ever painted on a wall — burns no sunsalt, so at night its window is the dark one on the street. In its place they keep a tallow candle: a real, honest, ordinary flame of the sort the world has almost forgotten how to make. It is not a signal so much as an argument. Everyone can see the dark window, nobody has worked out what it means, and the Surveyors, who cannot imagine an enemy that refuses the gift rather than demanding more of it, have never thought to look for one.

Their rank and file are the haunted, which is the difference between them and Green Fields stated in one line. Green Fields tells the woman with the empty chair that a tyrant did this to her. Red Flag tells her the truth, which is worse and which she has been waiting her whole life to hear: nothing did this to you on purpose, the world was folded and your child was inside it, and the only way you will ever see her again is if we open every door there is. Red Flag does not need to persuade the haunted. It needs to survive long enough to reach them, because Green Fields is reaching them first.

Their people are also, in significant part, Green Fields defectors — which is why Green Fields loses cells it cannot account for, and why its rank and file mourn friends who are three hundred miles away in a dark house with a candle in the window, unable to send word without hanging everyone they left behind. The leadership is content to let them be mourned as martyrs, because a martyr is worth more than a member.

**What Red Flag does not know, and this is the whole of their tragedy.** They do not know that opening every door at once looses everything in the zones onto a world with no warning and no army in position. They do not know that the man they are trying to depose was trying to do the same thing and was overthrown for it. And they do not know about the Devouring, because nobody does. They are right about the crime, right about the criminals, right about the cure in the long run, and catastrophically wrong about the method — and they will learn all of that from the heroes, in the field, one door at a time, which is what turns them from a slogan into a real faction over twenty sessions.

Their arc is therefore the party's arc, and it should be run as one. Open every door → *these zones are full of things and we are about to hand them a world* → find a way to unfold that does not end in a massacre → **the machine.** When they learn the Armature exists, everything else stops mattering to them, and Red Flag becomes the third hand reaching for it.

**Why they remain dangerous, and why the finale is theirs.** Red Flag is the party's ally, honestly and warmly, for two acts. Then the Devouring turns up at the center of everything, and Red Flag does not flinch, because they have been right about every single thing for their entire existence and it has cost them everybody they love. *We have come this far. We are not stopping at the last door because there is something behind it.* Their zeal is the same shape as the Foldwright's love for his family, and it points the same way, and in Act Three the heroes may find that the people who recruited them are the ones who have to be talked down.

## 4. The Cast

### The Foldwright

The world believes that the Foldwright rules. He is the unseen god-king, the wizard who wanted to be a god, who folded the world for limitless power and permanent safety and above all to own it by making himself the master of distance. No one has seen him in a lifetime and no one expects to. Every fence is his hand and every breach is his indifference, and he is at once the most powerful and the most hated man alive.

The truth is that he folded the world to leave the Devouring no room, as a stopgap he always intended to undo. The working hollowed him, spending his own measure into the creases along with the lost cities, and it swallowed his wife, his children, and his closest allies, so that he had folded the world around his own family. When he told his order that the design was failing and would have to be undone, they refused, and they came to kill him.

He is also, in the only sense that matters, the sole mourner of an entire vanished geography. The Folding stripped the memory of the lost places out of the world and did not strip it out of the people who performed it, so the Surveyors remember the cities perfectly well and keep the record under seal; they simply do not care, having lost nothing they valued. He grieves streets that nobody else has ever heard of, and a wife and children whose existence no living person outside his own order will concede. Whatever else is true of the man, that is his condition, and it explains more about him than any of his choices do.

He is not a prisoner. He is a fugitive. He fled into the Snarl because it was the one place they could not follow, and he took the Armature with him, and it is the only reason he is still breathing: kill him and the Fold tears, so until they hold that machine they dare not touch him. He has spent every day since working it, steadying a world that curses his name, with the last of what he has left. The foldquakes are his hands shaking.

There is no one on the throne, and there has not been for generations.

He needs the heroes, four nobodies, despite having been the most powerful wizard who ever lived, and for reasons that are worth keeping straight. He is hollowed, and is no longer the man the world fears. He cannot leave the Snarl, because stepping out would cost him the Armature and end everything, and although he can move within the folds and slip briefly through weak places, he cannot open a portal from the inside — and in any case he cannot spare the strength to try, since every ounce he has is holding the ground still. He cannot walk in the world, because his is the most hated face alive and the one nobody would recognize, which is somehow worse. And the Compass Spirit, which he made himself, will not serve him. It fled its maker and slept a lifetime rather than answer him, and it woke for these heroes and chose them, which is the one thing he can neither command nor purchase. He can only follow.

He is both help and hindrance, and the contradiction is honest rather than a trick. He wants the portals opened, because his family is inside and every opened portal is somewhere he can finally search. He dreads the final Unfold, because the entity is inside as well. He wants both things sincerely, and this is why the party will be unable to read him for two acts. He may also, in the end, still choose his family over the world, as he nearly did once — which is what got him deposed, and which means his jailers were not entirely wrong to stop him. Talking him down in Act Three is a scene worth playing, but it is not the finale.

He should be run briefly and then removed. He appears in the creases beyond a portal and never at the seams where the party harvests. He warns in complete sentences that the party will misread — *"You don't know what's on the other side"* will be heard as a warning about monsters — and he never trails off, because an unfinished sentence is a signpost and a complete misleading one is a disguise. He never voices his real fear, because his fear is the reveal.

He could, of course, simply tell them who he is. They would try to kill him.

### What He Lost, and Where It Is

Three things went into the Snarl with the world: his family, meaning his wife and his children; his closest allies, the Surveyors who stayed loyal and were folded away for it; and his measure, the piece of himself he spent on the working, whose absence left him weak enough to be overthrown.

These are scattered through the banished zones, one to a cell — a child in this one, a loyal Surveyor in that one, a shard of his measure deeper in. The party's journey inward is therefore also the reassembly of the Foldwright, and by the finale the man the whole world fears will be strong enough to matter in the fight, because the heroes will have rebuilt him without understanding that they were doing it.

A shard of his measure needs to be a thing a child can pick up, and the word gives us the image. To take a man's measure is to weigh him, so what he spent is weight, and what is left of him is light — a hollow man, in the plainest sense the word has. A shard is therefore a small dull object, plain enough to be mistaken for a paperweight, which is impossibly heavy for its size and which nothing in the world will make lighter. It does nothing. It cannot be spent, sold, or used, and it is worth carrying anyway, and every one the party finds makes their packs harder to lift. The last time they see one it is in his hands, and the man they have been following stands differently.

This structure earns its place three times over. It gives every zone an emotional payload beyond the mechanical business of unlocking a crease. It answers the otherwise fatal question of how a hollowed fugitive could possibly be useful in Act Three. And it arms the real conflict of the endgame, which is that his priorities and the world's priorities are not the same, and the heroes have spent twenty sessions serving both without being told.

It carries a serious risk, which is recorded here rather than hidden. If every cell holds only *his* losses, then the campaign's emotional spine belongs to a non-player character and the table spends twenty sessions fetching an old man's family. The correction is in §8, and it is the highest priority open question in this document: each cell must hold a piece of the Foldwright **and** a piece of a player's story.

### The Compass Spirit

The Compass Spirit is a shy creature, a fragment of the world's living connective tissue, the thing that once held distant places in relation to one another. It curls up to about the size of a sleeping cat, with rather less of a cat's confidence and rather more of its opinions, and it gives off roughly the light of a firefly, which is to say enough to find it by and not enough to read by. Fed, it unfurls into a glowing compass rose thrown into the air or across the ground, indicating the way to the next portal.

It is the only thing in the world that can navigate the Snarl, because it is the only thing that still remembers how the places used to touch. Without it the Fold is an unmappable labyrinth, and this is what allows four nobodies to accomplish what armies could not.

It does a second thing, and the second thing is the campaign's evidence. A memory is a relation between a person and a place, and relation is precisely the substance this creature is made of, so what the Folding creased shut the Compass Spirit can briefly open. Fed at a seam and set down where a lost thing used to be, it gives a memory back — a street, a harbor, a face, an afternoon — to whoever is standing there. The heroes will therefore carry around with them a handful of memories that no other living person possesses, which is the proof Red Flag needs most and which is worth exactly nothing to anyone who was not standing there when it happened. Learning that a true thing cannot be handed to somebody is one of the harder lessons this campaign has to teach, and it should be taught by letting them try.

It drinks sunsalt, which is its own substance, and so every direction and every memory costs a gray handprint left in the world. Red Flag has counted handprints of exactly that kind, in a ledger, for thirty years.

Its directions are riddles rather than bearings — *the tower that drinks the sun*, or *where two rivers forget each other* — which is not an affectation. In a place with no straight lines a bearing would be useless; the spirit can only describe what a place is like, never which way it lies.

The Foldwright made it, and it will not serve him.

### Wenna Ash — the recruiter

Wenna Ash lays four places at her table and has three children. She has done it for as long as she can remember, she cannot explain it, and for most of her life the people around her have been gentle with her in the way that people are gentle with the unwell.

Green Fields found her, and gave her the only explanation anybody has ever offered, which is that a world where one man's fist closes on everything is a world where people go wrong in ways they cannot name, and that when the fist is broken she will be whole. She has believed that for six years. It is not true. It is not quite a lie either, and it is close enough to the shape of her wound to fit, which is why it has held.

She is a true believer, and she is nowhere near the top of anything. She has never met the people who give the orders, she has never heard of the Armature, and she could not tell the heroes what she is really for because she does not know. Everything she says to them she means. When the betrayal lands it will not land on her; it will land through her, and she will be as ruined by it as they are — and she should be on the page when it happens, which is why she must be a person to them long before then.

The cruelest fact in her file is that she is one of the haunted, which means she belongs to Red Flag and always did. The people who took her fourth child are the people she works for, and she is brave for them, and she recruits others like herself. Getting her out is a payoff worth holding until late.

One caution on running her. Wenna does not know what was taken, only that something is missing; she has a fourth place setting and no theory, and she must never be permitted to explain the memory-theft, because she has never heard of it. Green Fields does not preach that the Fold stole anything. That is Red Flag's line, and she has not met them yet.

### Tobin Rell — the clerk who was sent to watch them

The Surveyors send a man with the party, and the man is not a spy in any sense a nine-year-old would recognize. Tobin Rell is a junior clerk of the Registry — the Surveyor office that records what crosses a road, what comes out of a Draw, and who was standing nearby — chosen because he is cheap, because he is expendable, and above all because he can write a legible report. He has never been in a fight. He is not brave, he is not deft, and he will spend the first half of the campaign being protected by children.

What he is, is a very good clerk. He writes down what he sees, accurately, every night, because that is what he was told to do and he has never in his life done a job badly. That habit — and not courage, and not conscience — is what destroys the people who hired him, because a man who records the truth faithfully for a year ends up holding a year of the truth.

He is not Red Flag when the campaign begins. He is a man who has begun to doubt, who has been quietly approached once, and who has not said yes. His doubt has a name, and the name is a friend of his who went into the folds with a Green Fields cell and did not come back, and whom Tobin has been dutifully mourning for two years. The friend is not dead. He walked, and he is alive, in a dark house, three hundred miles away, with a candle in the window.

He is also not Wenna's man, and the party should be allowed to work that out. Wenna did not ask for him and does not question him, because a movement that has always had handlers does not notice having one. He was assigned from above her by people she has never met, and he reports to them and not to her. The first time the heroes see Tobin file something Wenna has not been told about is the first loose thread in this campaign, and it is available in the first three sessions.

The party's evidence is what pushes Tobin over, and the moment that finishes him is not an argument, it is being taken to see his friend. That scene does three jobs at once and is the reason he exists: it proves the rebellion is a puppet, it hands the heroes the only real faction in the world, and it does both by way of a man finding out that the person he has grieved for two years is alive and could not write. Red Flag does not recruit the heroes. It inherits them, through him.

Afterward he takes his year of notes and goes, because Red Flag needs what is in them and because he is finally, for the first time in his life, doing a job for somebody he chose. The party loses their most useless companion and gains their only honest witness, and if it has been played well they will miss him badly.

Two notes on running him. He should be a genuine burden in a fight for a long time, because a party that has to keep an adult alive will grow attached to him whether they mean to or not, and because the inversion is funny and the children will enjoy it. And he must not be load-bearing: Red Flag exists without him, and if he dies in a bad fight in session six, Hettie Sarn finds the party some other way.

### Still to Cast

The campaign still needs the Surveyor leader, who has spent his life laughing at his own order's sealed warning, and it still needs the Foldwright's wife and children, who are the whole of what he is walking toward. See §8.

## 5. The Threat

### Two Tiers, and Never Confuse Them

The campaign has two kinds of monster and the whole architecture depends on keeping them apart.

**Tier two** is everything in the banished zones: trolls, wyrms, wild magic, things with teeth. Ordinary, familiar, satisfying to fight, and the reason unfolding carelessly is a massacre rather than a liberation. These are the campaign's encounter engine, because the portals lead into the zones and the zones are the dungeons.

**Tier one** is the Devouring, and there is only one of it. It is not a large tier-two monster and it is not the king of them. It is a different category of thing, it was the entire reason the world was folded, and it must never be hinted at as one of the others.

The reason to name this split plainly is that the party will spend two acts solving a tier-two problem — *how do we open the doors without loosing all of that at once* — and the answer they arrive at, which is the machine, is a genuinely good answer to the wrong question. Tier one does not care about the machine.

### The Banished-Zone Monsters

Tier two is also the decoy that protects the reveal, and this is the most important structural fact in the campaign. Everyone knows the Fold walls off monsters and everyone knows unfolding lets them out, so when a player asks what happens if the world comes apart, the answer is available, satisfying, and entirely true: all of that gets out. The question is asked, answered, and closed, and nobody digs any further.

The Devouring hides behind a satisfying answer rather than behind a blank space.

### The Devouring

The Devouring sits at the center of the Snarl, and it is still eating. Nobody living believes in it — not the public, not Green Fields, not Red Flag, and not the Surveyor leadership, although their founders knew of it and wrote it down before sealing the record.

The reason for this is generational rather than conspiratorial. The present leadership inherited the rule without the reason. The Devouring survives in a founding document as a line that reads like superstition, and they assume their own predecessors invented it as propaganda, because inventing it as propaganda is exactly what they themselves would have done. Nobody in power acts as though the entity exists, and that is what keeps the reveal sealed for two acts. It also banks a considerable payoff, because when the party finally puts the proof in front of the Surveyor leader, he will go white — having spent his entire life laughing at a warning that was true.

Its name and nature remain undecided, deliberately, but one constraint on that decision is already fixed. It must end up describable and fightable. This table will have spent twenty sessions swinging swords, and the finale cannot be an abstraction or a conversation.

## 6. The Arc

### The Opening — the Town Above

The heroes are in a city, for four reasons of their own that are not yet written, and they are crossing a square.

A man is shouting in it. He has a box to stand on and a voice that has been used too hard, and what he is shouting is not treason, or not quite. He is saying that the Fold is the best thing that ever happened to anybody, that his own grandmother walks to market through country that used to eat travellers, and that not one word of that gives one man the right to seal a road at breakfast because it pleases him. Ask his leave to visit your sister. Ask his leave to sell your grain. There is a fist around the world and nobody has ever seen the hand it belongs to. Break the fist, keep the Fold, and the whole thing could belong to all of us. Half the square agrees with him and keeps walking, because he says it every week; the other half has somewhere to be; and there are two Surveyors at the edge of it, not intervening, writing down faces.

Above him, as above every city, hangs the town above: the inverted underside of some other country, close enough here to make out roofs, and a road, and a clock tower with hands on it that the children of this city have watched all their lives and have never once been able to reach.

Then the ground moves.

It is a foldquake, and it is a bad one, and what it does is shake the tower loose from the sky. A city's worth of masonry comes down out of the ceiling of the world, end over end, taking three seconds to arrive, and the square becomes the worst thing that has ever happened in it. That is the first thing the heroes see. The second thing is that something came down with the tower — that the crease opened as it tore, and things with teeth came through the gap, and they have landed in a crowded square and are hungry.

The heroes fight, which is the point, and the fight is inside ten minutes.

Afterward, in the dust, with the clock face lying broken in the road and its hands still moving, a woman comes up to them who has been in the square the whole time and who did not run. Her name is Wenna Ash, she has been with Green Fields for six years, and she has just watched four strangers do in ninety seconds what her entire movement has failed to do in a lifetime, which is stand between the fist and somebody's grandmother. She does not give them a speech. She tells them that the tower did not fall, it was *dropped*, in the sense that everything in this world happens because one man permits it and nothing happens that he forbids; that the shaking is worse every year and the fences go up and never come down; that there are people who intend to do something about it and cannot get near him because he is hiding inside the folds themselves; and that what they need, and have needed for years, is somebody who can go in there and find him.

Every word she believes. Most of it is true. And the two Surveyors at the edge of the square have written down four new faces and will file them by evening, which is how the heroes acquire a handler.

Why each of the four heroes happens to be in that city on that morning is not yet written, because that is the seam where the players' own material goes, and it is being held open until we have it. See §8.

### How the Mission Escalates

The party's task never changes and its meaning changes six times, which is what makes it playable for twenty sessions by a nine-year-old. The operational verb is always the same — *find the next door, go through it, and bring back what is on the other side* — and it acquires a new significance at each rung.

1. **Go in and find him.** The tyrant is hiding inside his own work and no honest man can reach him. This is the whole of the brief Green Fields gives them, and Wenna means it.
2. **The portals, and the Unfold Rule.** Inside, the party discovers that the regions are locked, that each has a door, and that no crease can be pulled open while the others are still set. The mission acquires a shape: all of them, or none of them.
3. **The Fold cannot hold itself.** Deeper in it becomes plain that something is actively holding all of this shut, that the holding is being done by an instrument rather than by a will, and that an instrument can be taken. Nobody told them this. The mission acquires an object.
4. **The machine has a keeper.** He is the man they were sent to find, he is in the dark ahead of them rather than on any throne, and the ground stops shaking for as long as they are near him. The mission acquires a target and a face — and when the party sends word of the machine back to the people who hired them, the answer comes far too quickly and asks about nothing else.
5. **Act Two.** He is not a tyrant, there is nobody on the throne, and the people who sent them want the machine for themselves.
6. **Act Three.** There is something at the center, it has been eating the whole time, and the last door is a decision rather than a dungeon.

Two things follow from this and both are load-bearing. The machine is *discovered* rather than briefed, so the Armature must not be named in Act One. And the heroes have time to become people who could plausibly take it, which means the campaign has a structural stake in leveling.

### Why Unfolding Is Not Madness

The Fold is coming down regardless, because something is eating it, and nothing available to anyone will stop that. The choice facing the heroes is therefore not whether the world comes apart but how.

If they do nothing, it snaps on its own. Every banished zone bursts at once into a world with no warning, no walls, no army in position, and no living memory of how to survive any of it.

If they unfold it deliberately, they go through the portals one at a time, and in each zone they get the lost people out, learn the land and what lives in it, find the old ways of fighting the things there, and mark the door. When the world finally comes apart, it comes apart into a world that knows what is coming.

Stated for the table, and it should be said aloud: the wall is going to fall, and we can be standing there ready or asleep in bed.

This is the answer to the campaign's hardest question, and it needs to be in front of the players by early Act Two and never allowed to go vague. It is also what every region is *for*. The party is not ticking boxes; each zone they open is a zone that will not be a massacre.

It is not, however, the end of the argument, and it should not be allowed to become one. The counter is held by ordinary people and by the sincere half of Green Fields, and it is not stupid: the Fold works, it has worked for a lifetime, children play at the treeline because of it, and four strangers with a magic firefly propose to take that away on the strength of something they saw behind a door nobody else can open. Every word of that is fair. The heroes are asking a world to give up the only safety it has ever known, on their word, and the world does not owe them the benefit of the doubt.

### The Three Acts

**Act One.** *The rebellion is a puppet, and the Fold is the crime.* Breaches and foldquakes worsen, this season, where the heroes live. The gray turns out to be the Draws rather than a blight. Their own handler turns out to be filing reports their recruiter never sees. And the movement they joined is revealed as an instrument of the people it claims to fight — which means the heroes are not, and never were, fighting the tyrant. They were bird-dogging him.

The turn of Act One is Tobin's friend, alive in a dark house, and everything that follows from a man being mourned by the organization that knows perfectly well where he is. It delivers the betrayal and hands the party Red Flag in the same scene. The second half of the turn is Red Flag's own claim, which is larger and which the heroes will spend the act testing: the Fold did not merely cost something, it robbed the world of the memory of what it cost, and the answer is not a better grip on the fist but no fist and no Fold at all.

**Act Two.** *The tyrant has been a fugitive for a lifetime.* There is nobody on the throne and has not been for generations; every decree issued in his name was theirs. The man the heroes were recruited to disarm folded the world in order to save it, was hollowed doing so, lost his family inside it, tried to undo it, and was overthrown by his own order for saying so. Here the party learns the mission, and the act closes on the question of what he was shielding them from.

**Act Three.** *The Devouring, and the man who would let it in.* It is real, it is at the center, it has been eating the entire time, and the founders' sealed warning was true. And the Foldwright — restored, with his family a single door away — may still choose to unfold immediately and unprepared in order to reach them.

### The Act One Clock

The breaches and the foldquakes are worsening this season, in the place where the heroes live. The pressure is visible, urgent, fightable, and blamed by everyone on the wrong man. The world-scale truth is what the heroes learn; the monster in the wheat field is what makes them go.

## 7. Running It

### The Load-Bearing Rules

Several of the rules below exist to keep the Act Three reveal from leaking, and breaking one of them will quietly break something downstream.

1. The banished zones hold ordinary monsters. The Devouring is categorically different and is never hinted at as one of them.
2. Every seed planted for the Devouring must have a satisfying wrong answer available. Never leave a blank, because a gap is a signpost. If the Compass Spirit is afraid, it should be afraid in a monster's lair, so that the party concludes it fears the monster.
3. Nobody living believes in the Devouring, including the Surveyor leadership and Red Flag.
4. The world believes the Folding was clean. This is the foundation the whole propaganda rests on, and it is corroborated every day by a sky full of neat, shallow, orderly creases.
5. The Folding took the memory of what it took, and it took it from the world and not from the people who performed the working. The Surveyors therefore remember everything and keep the record sealed, which is how a conspiracy this old still knows what it is concealing; the Foldwright remembers everything and is the only one who mourns; and the haunted are the people the theft botched. Nobody explains the mechanism, at the table or in the fiction, because nobody in the world understands it. State the fact and show the evidence.
6. The world believes the Foldwright rules. Only the leadership of the two arms knows he was deposed, and everything the Surveyors do, they do in his name.
7. Nobody knows what the Fold really is. Everyone, including the Surveyors, believes the folded-sheet diagram, and the visible sky appears to prove it. The Snarl is a revelation, and it must land from the inside by being walked through; no character on the outside may ever describe it correctly.
8. The Fold is at the cinch, and the cinch was the point. The Devouring comes from between rather than from outside, so it cannot be walled out, only crowded out. Nobody can fold their way out of anything, because there is no room.
9. The Armature is not named in Act One, and neither Green Fields' rank and file nor Red Flag has ever heard of it. Wenna's brief is to go in and find the man; the machine is a discovery the party earns at the third rung of §6, and its power as a goal depends entirely on their having found it themselves. Only the leadership of the two owned arms knows it exists, which is why their sudden interest when the party reports it is the loudest clue in Act Two.
10. The Foldwright never explains himself until Act Three.
11. Sunsalt is destroyed by being taken out of the ground and spent. In the ground it is still part of the world, which is what makes the finale a genuine sacrifice.
12. Wrinkles cost sunsalt, heavily.
13. The Armature is the standoff. He escaped with it, the Fold tears without it, and so they cannot kill him and he cannot come out. Nobody has moved in a lifetime. The heroes are what breaks the deadlock — and if they find him, they lead the hunters to him.
14. Green Fields never attacks the Fold, only the fist around it. The moment its platform becomes *undo the Folding*, it collides with Red Flag, the two rebellions stop being distinguishable, and the reason the establishment tolerates one and hunts the other evaporates.
15. Every faction has two floors. The leadership of the Surveyors and Green Fields knows everything and is the same room of people. Their rank and file know nothing and mean well. Red Flag's leadership knows the crime and not the cost. Never let a faction speak with one voice.

### The Words We Use at the Table

The glossary at the end of this document runs to two dozen entries, and a nine-year-old will not carry two dozen proper nouns. What will break this campaign at the table is not the conspiracy, which is simple enough to state in four sentences, but the vocabulary, which is not.

The rule is therefore that the bible keeps the real words and the table gets plain ones. Say the real word once, in the moment where it sounds best, and then let it go; the children will settle on their own word for everything, and their word will be better than ours.

### Why the Propaganda Holds

The official story is that the Foldwright folded the world to obtain three things: unlimited power, permanent safety, and control. The third is presented as his crime, and it is ongoing rather than historical, since he rules by owning distance itself.

Two of the three claims are true, and the third is a complete and satisfying answer to the question of why he did it. That combination is what makes the lie durable. Nobody is being kept in the dark about the danger, because the danger is advertised — and nobody is being kept in the dark about the losses either, because as far as the world is aware there were none.

### The Two Maps

The best prop available to this campaign is a pair of paper maps.

The first is the folded sheet: a real map, neatly folded, with a seal — a paper clip, a wax dot, a ribbon — on every crease. This is the Surveyor-issue diagram, and it should be handed to the party in Act One like a pamphlet. Unlocking a region means removing that fold's seal while the map stays folded, so that the children watch the checklist shrink and never once need to ask whether the world has unfolded yet. The last seal coming off is the finale. The Unfold Rule can be demonstrated in the same breath by pulling one crease open while the others are still set, and letting the paper tear.

The second map is the Snarl: another map, crumpled into a tight wad and forced into a small box, kept out of sight until the party first goes deep enough to see the truth. It is then placed on the table with a single line — *this is the real one*.

That is the entire "the map is not the territory" revelation, delivered without a word of explanation to a table that has spent ten sessions trusting a diagram. It also justifies the Unfold Rule better than the neat sheet ever did, because a crumpled wad plainly cannot be smoothed out one crease at a time. It comes apart all at once, or not at all.

There is a third prop, and it costs nothing: a plain tallow candle, produced the first time the party spends a night in a dark house.

### Tone and Guardrails

The register is family-friendly with a light edge, leaning family. Spooky villains, real stakes, and gentle dark themes — betrayal, loss, a comfortable lie — are all welcome, as is cheeky humor that winks at the adults.

There is no permanent character death. Heroes are knocked out, captured, or rescued, and never killed off. There is no explicit gore, and nothing genuinely inappropriate for a nine-year-old.

There is no long exposition. The children get hooked quickly and the story reveals itself through doing rather than through telling.

And it is not merely a series of battles. Every leg of the journey wants a fight, a puzzle — the Compass riddles carry most of that load — and one moment in which somebody has to decide something uncomfortable.

### Every Faction Is Partly Right

This is worth protecting, because it is the difference between a morally interesting campaign and a series of fights.

The townsfolk love the folded world and are not fools; it genuinely is safer and warmer than what came before. The rank-and-file Surveyors save real lives at every breach and arrest people who genuinely are trying to loosen the world's only defense. The rank and file of Green Fields want nothing more radical than to walk to the next village without asking permission, and they are hunted for it by their own employer. The Surveyor leadership is correct about the cost of unfolding and wrong about very nearly everything else. Red Flag is correct about the gray, correct about the conspiracy, correct that the seams end only when the Fold does — and wrong about the method in a way that would kill thousands, which they will learn from the heroes and not from us. The Foldwright saved the world and then let grief nearly make him unsave it, which means his jailers were not entirely wrong to stop him. And the heroes are right that the Fold must come down, but the cost is real and it will be paid by people with names.

| 1 | **The players are not in this document.** Four heroes, no stakes, no hooks, no wounds. | The highest priority item by a wide margin. One kid has already chosen *"to find a lost family member"* and *"to free those who are captured"* in the builder, which is a bullseye into the zones and should be used. | **the creases** | The hidden interior, where the Foldwright hides and his family is lost. |
