# Campaign One — *The Folded World*

*The canon bible. What is true, what the players are told, and what they find out — in order.*

*Foundation locked 2026-07-11. Stress-tested and hardened the same day (see "The load-bearing rules" — several of them exist to stop the Act 3 reveal leaking, and breaking one quietly breaks the campaign).*

---

## The pitch, in four sentences

The villain already won. Long ago the greatest mapmaker who ever lived **folded the world** like a paper map — crushing cities together, erasing whole towns into the creases, and killing people who were never found. The world that survived is *smaller, closer, and richer than it has ever been*, because the folding pressed a glowing substance called **starsalt** up out of the land, and starsalt lights every lamp and warms every home. The heroes are going to find out what starsalt actually is, and then they are going to have to decide whether to give all of it back.

---

## The load-bearing rules

Four rules hold the whole thing up. Everything else is flavor and can be changed freely.

1. **Nobody says the word "monster" about the Fold.** The Fold did not fold away dangers. It folded away *distance*. If anyone at the table believes the Fold is keeping something out, the Act 3 reveal is dead — a ten-year-old will get there in one hop. (Monsters still exist in the world, everywhere, as normal. They just have nothing to do with the Fold.)
2. **Starsalt is destroyed by *burning* it, not by the Fold.** See below. This is what makes the final choice a real choice.
3. **The Devouring is unknown to everyone.** Not the public, not the Stillguard, not the Surveyors. Only the Foldwright, and he will not say it out loud.
4. **The Foldwright never explains himself.** Not until Act 3. He appears, he acts, he goes. He is *not allowed* to voice his dread, because his dread is the reveal.

---

## The Fold

The world is folded. Regions once a season's journey apart now sit a day's walk from each other. What got folded away was **distance** — the long empty leagues, the killing roads, the months of travel, the getting-lost-and-never-coming-home.

Everyday life is genuinely good. Your grandmother is close. Trade is cheap. Nobody starves on the road. The lamps are warm.

**At the table:** use a real folded paper map, and put a **seal** (a paper clip, a wax dot, a ribbon) on every crease. This is the best prop in the campaign — do not skip it.

## Starsalt

The glowing stuff. It lights, warms, mends, drives mills. It appeared *after* the Fold and exists *because* of the Fold.

**What it actually is:** starsalt is the world's own living essence, and the Fold **pressed it up to the surface** where it can be gathered. Lying there in the ground, it is still *part of the world* — bruised, exposed, but not lost.

**Burning it is what destroys it.** Every lamp, every forge, every warm house is spending a piece of the world *permanently*. It is slow — a lifetime, not a season — but it is certain, and it is irreversible.

**Which means the world can be given back.** Unburned starsalt, still in the ground when the world unfolds, is reabsorbed. That is what makes the finale a genuine choice and not just a switch: *unfold, and everyone gives up the lamps.*

The dying shows first at the **seams** — the fenced-off edges where the ground has gone grey.

> *This distinction is load-bearing. If the Fold itself did the bleeding, then burning starsalt would be harmless and giving it back would accomplish nothing. The Fold opened the wound. Burning is the bleeding.*

## The Compass Spirit

A shy little creature — a piece of the world's living connective tissue, the thing that used to hold distant places together. It curls up small, like a firefly-fox. When fed, it unfurls into a **glowing compass rose** projected into the air or on the ground, pointing the way to the next portal.

- **It drinks starsalt**, because starsalt is its own substance.
- **The cost is local, visible, and permanent.** The party can't afford refined Surveyor starsalt — they cut it **raw from a seam**, with their hands, and *the ground dies where they cut, in front of them, and stays dead.* Not an abstract share of a global total: a grey handprint they leave behind, every single time. That is the moral weight, and it only works if they *see* it.
- **Its directions are riddles.** Never "go north." A symbol, a fractured image, a rhyme — *"the tower that drinks the sun," "where two rivers forget each other."* The party works it out.
- **The Foldwright made it — and it will not serve him.** It knows what he did. It fled him and slept for a lifetime. It woke for *these heroes*, and chose them, and that is the thing he cannot force and cannot understand.

## The Portals and the Unfold Rule

Every folded region has a hidden **portal**. To *unlock* a region, the party must **open its portal and travel through it.** That loosens the crease.

**No region unfolds until every region is unlocked.** Then the world unfolds *all at once*.

Why: a folded sheet doesn't work any other way. Pull one crease open while the others are still set and the paper tears.

**Make unlocking visible on the prop.** Unlocking a region = **taking the seal off that fold.** The map stays folded. The seals come off one by one, the kids can *see* the checklist shrinking, and nobody ever has to ask "wait, is it unfolded now or not?" When the last seal comes off — that's the finale.

## The two factions (they are the same faction)

### The Surveyors — the establishment
They survey, they fence, they chart what you're allowed to see. They block off regions, hide the portals, and control starsalt.

*Public line:* "The Fold exists. It's dangerous to touch. We manage it, we give you starsalt, we keep you warm. Stay away from the edges."

Not loved. Depended on.

### The Stillguard — the "resistance"
Rallies. Recruiters. Banners. *"The Fold is killing the world! The Foldwright is a monster! Join us — we'll undo it!"*

They never undo anything. The portals are "lost." It's "not the right moment." They're "still gathering strength."

### The truth
**They are one organization.** Same leadership, same purse, same starsalt into the same pockets. The Stillguard is a machine for **absorbing dissent**: anyone who senses something is wrong gets recruited, handed a mission designed to fail, monitored, and neutralized.

Their name is the tell. They don't guard the world. They guard the **still**ness.

**Why this is the cleverest piece:** the establishment does not *suppress* the truth about the decay — secrets leak. It **outsources the alarm.** The Stillguard loudly announce the world is dying, so anyone who notices the grey at the edges already has somewhere to put that worry: *"Yes, terrible — the Stillguard are fighting it. Leave it to them."* The concern is real, publicly acknowledged, and permanently parked.

**What the rot at the top tells itself** (they are not cartoons):
- *"The decay is a lifetime away. We will find another way before then."* They have been saying this for generations and have stopped looking.
- *"Unfolding kills the world we have, today, to save a world nobody alive has ever seen."* People would freeze. Trade would collapse. **That part is true**, and it's the argument the heroes will have to actually answer.
- *"We are not certain it's even true."* They have decided not to look too hard, which is its own kind of choice.

**Not all of them are rotten.** The rank and file are sincere. The rot is at the top. That gives us sympathetic enemies, future allies, and a genuinely upsetting reveal for the kids: *the good guys were the bad guys — but not all of them.*

## The Foldwright

### What everyone believes
A proud mapmaker who wanted to be a god. He folded the world to **remake it in his own design and rule it** — crushing cities, erasing towns, killing people who were never found. The starsalt that came up out of the wound he simply *took*, and hoarded, and it took the Surveyors to wrest it back and share it out. **He still rules** — never seen, but his hand is everywhere, behind the fences. The boogeyman.

*(Note the propaganda blames him for* pride*, not starsalt-greed. It has to: starsalt didn't exist before the Fold, so "he did it for the starsalt" is a story that falls apart the moment a clever kid does the arithmetic — and the crack it opens leads straight to Act 2.)*

### What is true
He folded the world to **shield it from something that was devouring it.** He meant it as a stopgap: buy time, find the way to fight, then unfold.

In the folding he **lost the person he loved inside the creases.** He never recovered. The fight he meant to win never got fought.

Then the people around him saw the starsalt lying in the wound and realized the folded world was an *industry*. They took it, built the Surveyors, hung the crime around his neck, and drove him into hiding. He has been in the **creases** ever since — the unreachable folded interior, not the seams — searching for her.

**He was not publicly driven out — everyone thinks he still rules.** That is precisely what stops anyone noticing who actually does. A hated, absent tyrant is the perfect cover: every ugly thing the Surveyors do is *his* fault, and nobody looks closer.

### Why he needs four children to do it
Two reasons, and both are true:
- **He cannot walk in the world.** His is the most hated face alive. The Surveyors would end him on sight, and they'd be cheered for it.
- **The Compass Spirit will not serve him.** He made it, and it *fled* him, and it slept for a lifetime rather than answer. It woke for the heroes. He can't order it, buy it, or take it — he can only follow.

### Why he is both help and hindrance
**He wants the portals opened** — she is in there, and every opened portal is a place he can finally search.
**He dreads the final Unfold** — *it* is out there.

He genuinely wants both. That is why the party cannot read him for two acts. It isn't a trick; it's a man torn in half.

**How to run him:** brief, silent, gone. He never explains. He appears *in the creases beyond a portal*, never at the seams (where the party harvests). If he must speak, he warns without reasons: *"Not that one. Not yet."* **He never voices his fear.** His fear is the reveal.

## The Devouring — the real threat

The thing the Fold was built to keep out. **Unnamed, undescribed, unseeded — deliberately. It is the last card.**

- **Nobody knows it exists.** Not the public, not the Stillguard, not the Surveyors. When the truth lands, it blindsides *them* too.
- **Seed it invisibly.** A symbol scratched in the creases. A word the Foldwright won't finish. A single moment where the Compass Spirit is *afraid* and won't say of what. Things that only make sense in hindsight.
- **It becomes the final enemy.** The Foldwright chose a slow death over a fast one. The heroes' job is to find the third option: **unfold the world *and* be strong enough to meet what comes through.**

## The three turns

**Act One — our own side is lying to us.**
*The turn is the betrayal, and the mechanism — not "the world is dying."* The Stillguard shout that the world is dying at every rally; the party heard it on day one, so it can't be a revelation. What's new is **what starsalt is** (the world's life, and burning it spends it forever) and **who the Stillguard really are.**
*No monsters. No Devouring. The reveal stays sealed.*

**Act Two — the villain wasn't proud, he was afraid.**
Journals. A locket in every region. A man frantically searching, not ruling. He didn't fold the world to be a god — he folded it to protect something, and lost someone doing it. **What was he protecting us from?**

**Act Three — the Devouring.**
Unfolding lets it back in. The Foldwright becomes an ally. The final choice: **do we dare unfold, knowing what's on the other side?**

## The opening hook (ten minutes, no exposition)

**The heroes are recruited by the Stillguard.** They join the resistance. They're given a mission — find a portal, strike a blow at the monster who broke the world. It's exciting. They're heroes. They're moving before anyone sits through a history lesson.

**The mission was designed to fail.** Like every Stillguard mission. The portal they're sent to is a dead lead, and the plan is that they come back empty, feel useful, and stay in the pen.

**Then the Compass Spirit wakes up.** Nobody planned that. It chooses them, off-script, and suddenly the pointless mission *works*.

And their own side tries to shut them down.

That's the first turn, and it's earned.

## The Act One clock (they need a reason *this season*)

"The world dies in a lifetime" is the weakest possible motivator for a nine-year-old. So make it local, personal, and now:

**A seam is eating a place they love.** Their home village's fields are going grey at the edge. Somebody's grandmother's town is on the wrong side of a Surveyor fence. It is happening *this season*, to *people with names*, and the Surveyors' answer is a fence and a leaflet.

The lifetime-scale truth is what they *learn*. The dying field is what makes them *go*.

## Tone

Family-friendly with a light edge, leaning family. Spooky villains, real stakes, gentle dark themes (betrayal, loss, a comfortable lie). Cheeky humor that winks at the adults.

**No permanent character death.** Heroes get knocked out, captured, or rescued — never killed off.
**No explicit gore.** Nothing genuinely inappropriate for a nine-year-old.

The kids don't sit through exposition. They get hooked and clued in fast, and the story reveals itself through *doing*.

Not just battles: every leg wants a fight, a puzzle (the Compass riddles carry a lot of this), and one moment where somebody has to decide something uncomfortable.

---

## Every faction is partly right — keep it that way

- **The townsfolk** love the folded world, and they are not fools. It *is* nicer.
- **The rank-and-file Stillguard** sincerely want to save the world. They've been used.
- **The Surveyors at the top** are right that unfolding costs real people real warmth, today. They're wrong to have stopped looking for another way.
- **The Foldwright** did a terrible thing for a good reason and has paid for it every day since.
- **The heroes** are right that the world is dying — and *wrong* that unfolding it is simply, safely good.

## Open questions

- **Name the Devouring**, and decide what it is. Deliberately unsettled — it's the last card.
- **Name the Foldwright's lost love**, and decide whether she can be saved. (Given no-permanent-death and *"if there's a loss, build the save"*: probably yes, and it should cost something.)
- **Name the Compass Spirit.** Candidates: *Pip*, *Tesse*.
- **The Foldwright's real name.** He was once just a mapmaker with a name.
- **How many regions?** Sets campaign length. Probably 5–7.
- **What actually happens to the lamps?** The finale asks a whole world to give up warmth. Worth knowing whether there's a hopeful answer (the unfolded world brings back what starsalt replaced) or whether it's a real sacrifice.
