## ⚠️ Law Zero — how to use this without going generic

**Everything below is a net. None of it is a mould.**

**Brainstorm first. Check second.** Chase the idea that excites you, follow the odd thread, let the thing surprise you — **then** run a framework over it and ask *"what did we forget?"* Used that way, this document makes our worlds sharper. Used backwards — working down a list, filling slots — **it will make every town, villain, and dungeon we build feel like the last one.**

**The tells that we're using it backwards:**
- Every town has exactly one landmark, and a feuding mayor and priest.
- Every villain is a well-meaning tyrant who's *right about the problem*.
- Every NPC has all five layers, including the innkeeper who appears once.
- Every faction has precisely one smell, one custom, and one object.
- The content is *fine*, and completely forgettable.

**The dosage rules — these appear throughout, and they're not garnish:**
> *"You don't need all five layers on every NPC."* (§3.3) · *"Pick one and add it to the next game."* (§8.1) · *"Hit a few of these and you're fine."* (§4.4) · *"One or two sentences each is plenty."* (§1.7) · *"You are not failing if you sit at level 2."* (§1.12) · *"Start it, and let the game build the rest."* (§1.0)

**A monster with one great idea beats a monster with five checked boxes.** So does a town. So does a villain.

**The frameworks are strongest where they're *diagnostic*** — the triage table, the seven sins, the seven levels. They tell you *what's broken*. They never tell you *what the thing is*. **That part is still ours.**

## 1 · World & Setting

*Order of this section:* **§1.0 how to start a world** → §1.1–1.8 **towns** → §1.9 **the seven sins** → §1.10 **cities** → §1.11 **the realism question** → §1.12 **the seven levels** → §1.13 **dungeons.**
*The three location types live here: **towns** (§1.1–1.8), **cities** (§1.10), **dungeons** (§1.13). They are built by completely different methods — don't mix them up.*

### 1.0 Step zero: build tension before terrain

**If the first thing you do is draw a map, you've started in the wrong place.**

Think about any fictional world you love. What do you actually *remember* about it? Not the coastlines. You remember **the argument** — Sauron against the free peoples, Stark against Lannister. **Every great fantasy world is built on a fight.** The culture, the history, the geography — all of it emerges from a central tension.

**So your first world-building decision is: *what is this world arguing about?***

Write **one sentence** in this shape:

> **Something is at stake, and the people who depend on it can't agree what to do about it.**

That's the seed. Everything grows from it. *(This is §2.2's Seed Question wearing its world-building hat — the seed question asks what the campaign is about; this asks what the **world** is fighting about. Same engine, different scale.)*

**Worked example — "a magical resource an entire community depends on is failing, and nobody agrees what to do."**
The resource is a river carrying traces of magic from an ancient source up north. Settlements depend on it for farming, medicine, and trade. The source is weakening. And now **the factions build themselves**:
- The people **at the source** control access to the refined water that still works — and they're turning authoritarian, rationing it.
- Rationing means the communities **downstream** get cut off. They're farmers and fishers, they're hungry, and they're looking for alternatives.
- And because there's a shortage, somebody is **stockpiling it for profit** — an alchemists' guild who figured out the refining process.

**Notice what just happened.** One sentence of tension produced three factions with genuinely incompatible goals, and *not one of them is a villain*. That's a world.

#### The ten commandments

**1 · Build tension before terrain.** (Above.) Maps are great — draw maps. Just don't start there.

**2 · Define your world by what it *lacks*.** When we build, we build by *adding* — and you end up with a shopping catalogue of species, kingdoms and magic systems that still somehow feels generic. **Great settings are memorable for what *isn't* in them.** *Limitations are more interesting than powers.* So ask: **what is scarce, forbidden, broken, or gone?**
- *In the example:* healing in this region has always come from the river. The gods here are quiet old nature spirits; clerics **need** refined water to heal. And only the alchemists' guild knows how to refine it. **A healing potion is now a political object**, and the clergy and the economy are welded together.

**3 · Let your magic *break* your world.** D&D hands you magical healing, teleportation, instant communication, mind-reading — and then most settings carry on with medieval feudalism, horse travel, and sealed letters. **The magic and the society are living in two different worlds**, and players feel it. Don't just decide what magic *can do*; decide **what it already did** to the economy, politics, daily life, and war.
- *In the example:* magic water means crops that grow fast, never blight, and taste extraordinary. So the region got rich exporting food — which drew attention. But the whole economy has a **single point of failure**, and it's now failing. The alchemists who solved the refining problem became a pharmaceutical monopoly. The government at the source is passing harsher laws because it's losing its grip. And the farmers have been in economic crisis for a decade, so many of them are turning rebel. **Ask one question — "what does magical water do to a farming economy?" — and an entire political map falls out.**

**4 · Fake the iceberg.** The classic advice is: show 10%, build the other 90% so it *feels* deep. **You do not have time for that, and you don't need to.** Instead, make the 10% you *do* show so hyper-specific that players assume the rest exists. Get a few small details *so right* that they credit you with everything else.
- **The recipe — three details per faction, culture, or place:**
  - **One sensory** — what it smells/sounds/looks like.
  - **One behavioural** — a custom, a greeting, a way of eating, an insult.
  - **One object** — a tool, a coin, a piece of clothing. Something easy to picture.
- *In the example:* the river-source people carry a faint smell of **wet stone** (they live in cave networks near the source). They speak in **water metaphors** — calling someone "still water" is a cutting insult, meaning useless. And they wear **riverstone pendants** that read how much magic is left in water; the custom is to dip yours in your cup before you drink.
- **With zero history and no timeline, an NPC dipping a stone in their drink and frowning makes the world feel a thousand years old.**

**5 · Make your lore *actionable*.** Take any piece of lore and ask: *how can my players interact with this?* If the answer is "they can listen to an NPC explain it" — **it isn't playable yet.** The fix: **attach a need to it.** Someone needs something from it, is threatened by it, or is hiding from it.
- *In the example:* "the river's source" was inert background. So — what if the source isn't natural? What if something **alive** is buried down there, and has been bleeding magic into the water so long that everyone forgot it was ever there? And what if it isn't dying — it's **waking up**, and the magic is stopping because it's being used to do exactly that? Now it's a ticking clock, a dungeon, an investigation, and a moral choice: *do you put it back to sleep to keep the magic flowing, or free it, because it's probably a person?*

**6 · Let the world move without them.** Players get bored when the world is *waiting for them* — the "we haven't been here in three years and nothing has changed" effect. **Give each faction a goal and a progress clock.** Each session, advance one, based on what makes sense. Player actions can speed a clock up or slow it down, **but they cannot pause the world.**
- *In the example, three clocks:* the government's **lockdown** (when it fills, all private trade in the water is banned); the guild's **vault** (when it fills, they have a private reserve big enough to stop supplying anyone and make a play for power); the farmers' **saltrite** (they've sent their bravest out to experiment with sea-based magic — if it works, they don't need the river at all).
- So if the party spends a session escorting a shipment, the government's clock ticks while they're away — and they come back to **new checkpoints on the river.** The world moved, and they have to react.
- *(This is the same tool as §5.1's fix for the five-minute adventuring day: narrative clocks put time back into the world.)*

**7 · Leave room for the unknown.** There are two approaches: build a **scale model** where every question has a logical answer, or **conjure** — use hints and fragments strategically and let imagination do the heavy lifting. Leave things out, and players become **archaeologists**, assembling meaning from fragments. **Treat mystery as a resource.**
- *In the example:* what *is* the thing at the source? I don't know. I have a vibe — ancient, big, maybe conscious. **I'm not writing a stat block, because the moment my players start theorising, their theories will be better than anything I'd have planned.** What's north of the river? Don't know. Maybe nobody goes that far. How does the sea magic work? I'll decide when they're about to see it.

**8 · Honour your promises.** **Every meaningful detail you place is a contract with your players.** Describe a huge rune-covered door sealed with chains and glowing faintly, let them try everything, and then — three sessions later — say *"oh, that was just flavour"*… and you've taught them that **nothing you describe means anything.** Breaking the promise is worse than never making it.
- **The rule of three:** mention something **once** and it's atmosphere. **Twice** and it's a pattern — it should be playable. **Three times and you've made a promise.**
- **And it runs the other way:** when players latch onto a throwaway NPC or a random shop, **they are handing you a blank cheque.** Cash it. Make that detail secretly important. They'll think you're a genius, and — more usefully — they'll start paying close attention to *everything* you show them.

**9 · Build through play.** Beware **world-builder's disease**: the prep becomes the hobby and the game never happens. Months of building, and the campaign doesn't even start. **Your world does not need to be finished before you play.** Start it, play, and let the game build the rest. *(Pre-session-one checklist: §8.5.)*

**10 · Your world exists to challenge characters — not to display your craft.**
This one sits above all the others, and it stings:

> **Your players do not care about your world.** You care about the dynasty and the trade routes. They care about whether the bartender likes them, and about their own backstory. **To them, the world isn't a world — it's the environment around their character.**

**The final filter, run everything through it:** *Can this piece of world-building make a player **character** uncomfortable?* (The character. Not the player — keep the table safe.) Can it force them to betray an ally, or sacrifice something they love? **If yes, your world is doing its job. If no, it may simply not matter at the table.**

- *In the example:* drop an Oath-of-Protection paladin in. The farmers are starving and the government is hoarding — so as someone sworn to protect the weak, the answer looks obvious: help the rebellion. **But then they learn about the thing at the source** — that it may never have given its magic willingly, that it's been *bled* for centuries so the settlements could prosper. And now there's no clean answer.
- **The crucial part: we never sat down to torment a paladin.** We built tension, scarcity, and consequences — **and the dilemma assembled itself.** That's how you know the world is working: **you get drama that constructs itself.**

**→ Our table:** commandment 4 (fake the iceberg) is the single most efficient technique here and it's tailor-made for kids — one smell, one custom, one object per faction, and they'll believe in a place completely. Commandment 8's rule of three is a discipline I should hold to: kids notice *everything* and remember what you promised. Commandment 6's clocks are how we make the world feel alive without more prep. And commandment 10 is the same north star as §1.9's sin 7, arrived at from a different direction: **build for the four people at the table, not for the craft.**

### 1.1 Build only what you need, but build enough that the world *coheres*

The two failure modes are opposite and both fatal:

- **Building forever.** Flesh out the whole world and you never actually play. You don't need the smell of the carnivorous forest four months south of the party.
- **Building nothing.** "Don't over-prepare" is bad advice taken literally. If your world has a strong premise, you must work out the *consequences of that premise* before you drop players into a town, or the town won't make sense.

**The rule:** *build outward from the premise until the world is internally consistent, then stop.* If only one country matters to the story, build only that country — and only the parts of it that are live.

- **Test:** if a player asks "why is it like this?" about your core premise, can you answer? If not, keep building. If yes, stop and play.

### 1.2 Towns: the list *is* the town

A usable town is not a map. It's a **list of ~10 locations, each with one NPC and one sentence** (a hook, an item, or something odd). The map, if you make one, comes *after*. Ten locations is a meaty town; it is also surprisingly a lot of prep.

**Every location entry = Place + Person + One line.**

### 1.3 The three pillars of a town

Every town should be checked against all three:

| Pillar | The question | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Gameplay** | What do players *need* here between adventures? | It's a game first. Rest, resupply, sell loot, get the next job. |
| **Narrative** | What does this place add to the plot and the lore? | Quests, hooks, backdrop. |
| **Verisimilitude** | Why does this feel like a real, living place? | The thing that makes them *care* about it. |

### 1.4 The player-facing questionnaire (answer these and you can run the town)

These are the questions players actually ask on arrival. Answering all seven = the town is playable.

1. **Where's the inn?** — The cornerstone. Non-negotiable, even where history says a village that size wouldn't have one. It's where they rest AND where they gather information AND where the rest of the cast congregates in the evening (the blacksmith is here drinking at 6pm — that's how you meet him without opening the forge). Give it: a keeper (a *family* running it adds instant cast), a sign, and one quirk it's known for. One inn is enough.
2. **Where can I buy equipment?** — Small towns realistically have a blacksmith who makes nails and horseshoes, not longswords. Consider a **market** (people selling wares by day) instead of shops, or a **passing caravan** for the adventuring gear. Don't stat ten shopkeepers: say "the market sells anything on the gear list under 10gp" and move on.
3. **Where can we sell our loot?** — Often the market, but special loot (magic items, art, gemstones) needs a *specific person*: the local wizard, a noble, a collector who covets rare books and pays double.
4. **Who's in charge here?** — Have this NPC ready before play. **What matters is that authority exists and is *contested*, not who holds it.** Influence comes in flavours — **legal, spiritual, economic, military** — and the interesting question is *which flavour dominates here, and who resents it.*
   - ⚠️ **The source offers a stock cast — mayor / noble / priest / guard captain, with two of them feuding. Do not reach for it twice.** It's *one* answer, and if every town we build has it, every town will feel the same. Ask instead: **who actually holds power in a place like this?** The guild that owns the only bridge. A council of grandmothers. The mine foreman, because he controls the water. A child, and the four adults arguing over her. Nobody — and that's the problem.
   - *The durable part of the rule is the friction, not the roster.* **A town without a power struggle is a town without scenes.**
5. **Where do we get information?** — Spread it: shopkeepers know the region, the authority knows the town's current problems, the inn knows gossip. **Telegraph the important one.** If they need the wizard, put his tower on the cliff by the waterfall. Making players interview six NPCs to find the one that matters is tedious.
6. **Who can heal us / do magic?** — Be stingy and be deliberate. See 1.5.
7. **Are there any problems in town?** — See 1.6. This is the engine of the next adventure.

### 1.5 The magic dial (a setting decision, made town by town)

- **Rule of thumb:** a town almost never has a caster above ~3rd-level spells. Spellcasters exist but are *notable* — the local witch, the recluse, the old priest.
- Small towns sell **potions and maybe scrolls**; permanent magic items essentially never (unless a merchant is passing through, or this town is *special* — which is itself a story).
- **Resurrection should be a quest, not a purchase.** If any village can raise the dead, death costs nothing and the world stops making sense. Send them to the far city, or the recluse in the mountain monastery.
- **Give your caster NPCs a reason not to adventure** (§3.4). Otherwise players will ask why the wizard doesn't just come along, or start recruiting an army.

**→ Our table:** the resurrection rule fits our no-perma-death mandate beautifully. Heroes get knocked out, captured, or rescued — so **"resurrection as quest" becomes "rescue as quest"**: the missing hero is being held somewhere, and getting them back *is* the adventure.

### 1.6 Problems in town = the adventure engine

Two tiers, and you want both:

- **The real problem** — bandits on the trade road, a hippogriff in the fields, trolls in the woods, the local druid has gone mad. This is the plot hook.
- **Folksy colour** — the butcher's boys got drunk and started a fight last night. Doesn't tie to anything. Makes the place feel alive. *Sometimes players get obsessed with this and it becomes the adventure anyway.* Let them.

**The Zelda mechanic:** the person whose help you need has a problem of their own. *"I can't help you until someone deals with the owlbears eating my hunters."* This chains the party into meeting the town, doing an investigation, earning goodwill — and *then* the main quest opens up. It's a clean way to make a town's cast matter.

> **⚠️ Once. Not every town.** If every person the party needs has a chore for them first, it stops being a story and becomes a **toll booth** — and players smell it immediately. It's a tool for the town where *earning the town's trust* is the point, not a default gate.
>
> **Other ways the cast can matter:** they *disagree* about whether to help, and the party has to pick a side. They help immediately, and it costs them something the party has to watch. They *want* to help and can't. They already tried, and failed, and are ashamed. **Or they just help — because sometimes a town that simply likes you is the most surprising thing in a campaign.**

### 1.7 The DM-facing questionnaire (answer these and the town feels *real*)

One or two sentences each is plenty.

- **Name** — Names carry lore. Derive from geography, local trade, or a local hero. *Fisher's Brook. Knightsfall. Dragon's End. Glitterstone. Rivercross.* A good name tells the players what this place is before you describe anything. Don't call it Mountaincliff if it's in a field.
- **Population** — Drives everything else. 15 people = no inn, someone puts you up in a barn. 1,000 = a real cast.
  - *Handy ratio:* **max value of any item findable here ≈ the population in gold.** Town of 1,000 → nothing over 1,000gp. Halve it if poor/remote, double it if rich. **Invert it for selling:** no single merchant has more than that in cash, so big loot means bartering or travelling.
  - *Handy ratio:* **~5 people per building.** 1,000 people ≈ 200 buildings. If your map has 300 buildings and 50 people, most of it is abandoned — which is a story, but you'd better know that.
- **Where do people live?** — And they don't have to be houses. Burrows for halflings/dwarves, treehouses for elves, converted boats in a seaside town. Cheap, instant lore.
- **Food & water** — Every town needs a water source; that's why real towns sit on rivers. And the food supply *feeds back into flavour*: a local orchard means the inn is famous for its cider. A large town implies **feeder villages** — the community around a town can be 2–3× the town itself.
- **Defences** — Militia, town guard, or just a sheriff and a deputy. Two constraints:
  - Walls/castles/standing armies **imply a level of wealth and organisation**. A walled village in the middle of nowhere needs an explanation (that explanation is a plot).
  - **Don't over-stat the guard.** Maybe ~1% of a population is dedicated defence, ~2% militia. A town of 1,000 might struggle to muster 25–50 able bodies, and probably can't arm them. Half the population is children or elderly. *This is why they need the heroes.* An old captain and his 18-year-old son who just took the job is a whole plot in one sentence.
- **Culture, religion, disposition** — What do these people *stand for*? Can be the first question you ask or the last; it works in both directions.
- **Major resource / reason to exist** — Every town exists for a reason. Fishing, mining, lumber, fertile land. Or intangible: a fort at a strategic pass, a river crossing, a bridge. Or fantastical: it grew around an old wizard's tower because of what the tower does. Towns naturally cluster about a day's travel apart.
- **One landmark that makes it unforgettable** — *This is the highest-value item on the list.* The skeleton of an ancient dragon embedded in the mountainside above Dragon's End. The legendary bridge at Old Bridge. The ferry that exists because the bridge collapsed. **One** distinctive thing turns a town into a place players want to come back to. Ideally the name, the landmark, the resource, and the history all lock together into one package.

### 1.8 The town-as-adventure-site caveat

Everything above builds a **safe haven** — rest, shop, restock, get the next job. If the town is itself the dungeon (it gets attacked, everyone's lying, the NPCs are steering you into danger), keep these principles but layer dungeon-design on top. Different job.

### 1.9 The seven sins of world-building

Each of these **disguises itself as good practice**, which is why they're so easy to commit.

**Sin 1 · Over-explanation (it looks like thoroughness; it kills curiosity).**
The party spots a strange rune-covered tower and lights up with interest. So the guide explains: built 300 years ago by the wizard so-and-so, the runes channel energy from the negative plane, the pulsing indicates— *"So it's a lich tower. Got it. What else is around?"* **The excitement died the moment you answered completely.** A fully explained world is predictable, not intriguing.
- **The fix — "partial answer + new question."** *"The runes don't match anything in your books. Scholars have been arguing about this thing for decades. There are three competing theories about when it was built, nobody can read the symbols, and the light doesn't follow any known magical pattern."* You've said a lot and revealed nothing. They're now desperate to go in.
- **Replace exposition with discovery.** Don't tell them about the ancient war — let them find a cracked helm with **bite marks that don't match any known creature.**
- *Practical:* before each session, pick **three things you will deliberately leave mysterious.** Ruins, phenomena, a cultural tradition — things that genuinely don't need explaining.

**Sin 2 · The lore dump (it looks like being well-prepared).**
You've built a rich history and you're eager to share it. So you deliver 900 years of dynastic politics and theological schism, and the player says *"Cool. Um, so where do we need to go again?"*
> **Information only becomes interesting when it's connected to something players can touch, investigate, or decide about right now.** Abstract history feels like homework, not adventure.
- **The fix — physical evidence over abstract information.** Not "there are political tensions" but *graffiti arguing about tax policy.* Not a lecture on the Shadow War but *a battlefield where the metal fragments are strangely dark and cold to the touch even in sunlight, and a broken marker you can just barely read: "here fell the—"* — something about betrayal, and **void-touched steel.**
- **The breadcrumb method:** give just enough to create a question, then let them choose whether to dig. *"The marker mentions a betrayal"* beats a complete timeline of who betrayed whom.
- **Before sharing any backstory, ask: how does this connect to what the players are doing *right now*?** If it doesn't, save it.
- > **Your detailed history isn't wasted. It's ammunition — deployed when it has maximum impact.**

**Sin 3 · NPCs as quest dispensers (the "video game effect").**
The party walks into the tavern and the keeper immediately makes a beeline: *"Adventurers! Perfect timing!"* Players instantly smell it — *was he just standing here waiting for us to show up?* Every NPC exists purely for their convenience, and the world feels like a menu.
- **The fix is §3.2** — a **want** (they're pursuing something whether or not the party shows up) and **momentum** (they're already mid-task when the party walks in). *"Can you believe the nerve of that supplier — three weeks late, not a word"* beats *"I have a job for you."* **Nothing more to add here; go build the NPC properly.**
- **The one thing that's specific to a *town*: interconnect their problems into an ecosystem.** The blacksmith's supplier hasn't come. The tavern keeper is short on supply wagons. A merchant in the corner is nervously watching the door, asking whether anyone's seen bandits on the road. **Three separate NPC problems that are secretly one problem** — and the players assemble it themselves. *(This is §8.1's asymmetric-clues principle applied to a whole settlement — and it's the single best use of a town's cast.)*

**Sin 4 · The tourist effect (it looks like creative control).**
You built a magnificent world in isolation. Every piece fits. And the players say: *"So what's my ranger's connection to any of this?" "Yeah — my character feels like a tourist."* They're museum visitors, because they had **no voice in shaping the place.**
- **The fix — bounded creativity.** Ask each player for **one specific contribution.** Not "create whatever you want" (overwhelming, and it dissolves your vision) but *"tell me about your cleric's temple"* or *"what's unique about your hometown?"*
- **Leave deliberate gaps for them to fill.** Let the rogue's player describe how thieves operate in their city. Ask the cleric what ceremonies they grew up with.
- **Then actually build on it.** The sacred grove the ranger mentioned becomes central to a later adventure. The grandfather's cabin turns out to hold clues. **When you use their ideas, ownership becomes real** — and suddenly arriving somewhere isn't visiting, it's *coming home.*

**Sin 5 · Single-purpose locations (it looks like efficient prep).**
Every location serves exactly one story beat. Then the players want to go look at the tower, and you have to say *"...there's nothing there, it's just scenery"* — and they say *"but you made it sound important!"* **You've built a beautiful railroad with illusory choice.**
- **The fix: for each major location, brainstorm three *different types* of conflict that could happen there.** That forest isn't "the bandit encounter." It's bandits *or* refugees hiding from a war *or* poachers hunting magical creatures *or* territorial spirits.
- **Think in layers, not linear paths.** Not "this leads to that," but **"what groups are active here, and what are they trying to accomplish?"** Then emphasise whichever layer serves the current story.
- **Use "yes, and."** When they want to explore somewhere unexpected: *yes — and here's what you find.* Old campfires suggesting someone's been staying here. Strange animal tracks circling the base. Faint chanting from inside. **Three hooks, three directions, one location.**
- > **Preparation should be ammunition, not restraint.**

**Sin 6 · No consequences (it looks like being a forgiving, player-friendly DM).**
You don't want to bog the new adventure down with old complications, so the tavern they burned down last month is just... fine. And the players learn the most destructive lesson available: **nothing we do actually matters.** Why weigh a decision if the world resets between sessions?
- **The fix — a consequence journal.** After each session, write down **three major player decisions and their logical implications.** The caravan they failed to protect — those goods never arrived. The corrupt official they exposed — he remembers.
- **Delay consequences by three to five sessions.** Immediate payback feels like karma; delayed payback feels like a *world*. And it produces the best moment in the game: *"wait — this is because of what we did."*
- **Make consequences opportunities, not punishments.** The burned tavern creates supply problems *and* opens new meeting spaces, shifts social dynamics, and gives the party a reputation. Some people nod gratefully. Others look worried. **Consequences should make the world more interesting, not more punishing.**

**Sin 7 · Ego-driven world-building (the deadliest).**
You've poured hundreds of hours into intricate systems. You're active in world-building communities. And when the players glaze over at the third nested sub-faction and ask *"can we just fight some bandits?"*, you tell them they don't appreciate good world-building. **That's the tell.** You're designing for the approval of other DMs rather than for the enjoyment of your actual table.
- Same conflict, presented well: *"There's tension in town. The lumber baron wants to cut the old growth; the locals say it's sacred. Both sides are hiring muscle."* **Simple, clear stakes, and the ranger's player already has strong feelings.**
- > **Your job isn't to create art. It's to create fun.**
- **Complexity ≠ quality.** The most sophisticated political system ever devised is worthless if it bores your group.
- **Measure success by player engagement, not world-building sophistication.** Excited, asking questions, agonising over choices = working. Confused, on their phones, asking to just fight something = **your beautiful world is failing its only real test.**
- > **The best world-building is invisible.** Players should feel immersed without ever being conscious of the craft. Your creativity should amplify what your players already enjoy — not educate them about what they *should* enjoy.

**→ Our table:** Sin 7 is the one to tattoo on the inside of my eyelids, given our north star. "Would a beginner get this?" and "does this make the game more fun for *these four people*?" are the same test. Sin 4 (bounded creativity) is a gift with kids — *"tell me one thing about your hometown"* is a question a 9-year-old will answer enthusiastically and then defend fiercely for the rest of the campaign. And Sin 6's delayed consequences are how a kid learns their choices are real, which is arguably the whole reason to play.

### 1.10 Cities: build a theme park, then tell them it's a city

**A city is not a big town. Do not build it the way you build a town.**

Cities are the natural showcase for a setting — how different peoples mingle or refuse to, where the powerful live, how magic actually shows up in daily life. A city is your world in miniature: **the greeting card to your universe.** Which is exactly why it's worth not botching.

#### Why cities go wrong

1. **You build for realism instead of for play.** You start with the sewage system, the agrarian economy, and the branches of government — in a campaign where most of the play time is spent fighting monsters. You've multiplied your workload for questions that will never come up, and bought a one-way ticket to burnout.
2. **The endless shopping episode.** A metropolis-sized DMV. If a shopping trip eats a whole four-hour session, something has gone wrong.
3. **Exposition-only places.** All the important NPCs live in cities, so cities become where powerful people *tell the plot at you* — or worse, explain the world at you.
4. **"When do I get to hit something?"** Cities are charisma country. Most parties won't solve city problems with violence, so the bard and the wizard shine and the **barbarian, fighter, ranger and druid get nothing** — except, maybe, some rats in the obligatory sewer mission.
5. **And then you have to do it again.** Countries contain more than one city. Every mistake gets repeated.

#### The method: theme parks

**Build a theme park. Then just tell the players it's a city. They won't notice.** This is **gameplay-first** city design.

**A · Theme parks are *themed*.**
Pick a theme, and fill in the blank: ***[Name], the City of ____.*** *The city of lights. The city of night. The city of doors. The city of fire.*
- **The theme must be visual.** "The city of revenge" doesn't work — you can't build a look out of it. Simple is good: an element, a substance, a phenomenon.
- The theme drives the culture and the look. *City of light?* Maybe it's built around an enormous lighthouse, and whichever district is currently being lit is the district that's currently awake. *City of heat, at the pole of the world?* Warmth is the resource — the rich live near the ever-burning flames and the poor freeze at the edges.
- **Don't over-detail it.** You need room for the important part.

**B · Theme parks have *rides*.**
> **A ride is something in the city that leads to an adventure.**

**Rides are not shops.** Hand the players a sheet of items and prices and let them shop *outside* the session. (Haggling is not the exciting part of being in a city. A shop only becomes a ride when it's an *experience* — the speakeasy shop where you need the password to reach the good stuff.)

**What rides actually look like:**
- Win the coliseum tournament so the city's ruler notices you.
- Stage a play, because the baroness is obsessed with opera and will leave her manor to attend — *while the rogue burgles it.*
- Commit three crimes against the church, because that's the only way the resistance will trust you.

**And rides must be themed to the city.** That's what makes them memorable rather than generic. *In the City of Lights, you need to darken a street long enough for a door made of shadow to form, so you can walk through it.* *In the City of Night, you must dance at the midnight ball to reach the one person who knows what you need — while avoiding catching "night" yourself, which is permanent.*

**C · Every player gets a ride.**
**This is the fix for problem 4.** You are building this from scratch, so *build it for the people at your table*. Rule of thumb: **every player needs at least one ride that appeals to them.** If one player is getting seven and another is getting one, fix it.
- Fighter/barbarian → the coliseum, where combat *is* the correct answer.
- Ranger/druid → the ruins of the undercity, or the king's rose garden and the flower that will cure a friend.
- Rogue → the infiltration.
- Wizard → the puzzles and traps.
- Bard → the ball.

**The best rides serve several players at once.** One raid on a criminal syndicate can be *big dumb combat* for the barbarian, *an infiltration* for the rogue, and *an impersonation* for the bard — simultaneously. Build them that way.

**D · Theme parks are for *fun*.**
The blunt version: **realism is what's burning you out, and it isn't buying you anything.** Nobody needs the city's sewage system, its taxation of neighbouring counties, or its population growth rate. That prep is done "in case someone asks," as though world-building were an exam you could fail.

And the real cost isn't the wasted hours — it's that **the energy you spend on the sewage system is energy you no longer have for the go-kart race through the City of Speed.** So you don't build the race. You don't finish the world either. You cancel the session.

> **You're doing this for fun. It's a game. It's not a test.**

#### Why the theme-park method fixes everything

It makes cities **fast** to build, **repeatable** (you'll need several), **distinct** (different theme each time), and **aimed squarely at your actual players' fun.**

**→ Our table:** this is *extremely* us. "The City of ____" is a format a 9-year-old can not only understand but *invent*, and "everyone gets a ride" is just the spotlight principle (§2.1 step 5) with a concrete build instruction attached. It also solves a real problem we'll hit: our kids are unlikely to enjoy a political intrigue city where the answer is always "talk better." Give them a coliseum.

### 1.11 The realism question (resolving a conflict between sources)

Two of our sources appear to flatly contradict each other, and it's worth settling.

- **§1.3** lists **verisimilitude** as one of the three pillars of a good town — the thing that makes a place feel alive.
- **§1.10** says **delete every piece of advice you've heard about verisimilitude** — it's what's burning you out.

**They're using the word for two different things, and both are right.**

| | **"Feels alive"** (do this) | **"Simulation realism"** (don't) |
|---|---|---|
| What it is | Specific, sensory, human detail | A logically complete model of a functioning society |
| Example | The innkeeper's family bickers; the guild's vials are teardrop-shaped blue glass | The sewage system; the tax base; population growth rates |
| Who it's for | The players | Nobody |
| Cost | Minutes | Weeks |

**The resolution is commandment 4 (§1.0): fake the iceberg.** Hyper-specific detail produces the *feeling* of a deep, coherent world at almost no cost. Simulation produces the same feeling — sometimes — at enormous cost, and usually never reaches the table at all.

**So: be specific, not comprehensive.** And note that §1.7's town constraints (population caps, defence ratios) aren't simulation for its own sake — **they're there because they generate plot.** *That's* the test for any piece of realism: **does it make something happen at the table?** If yes, keep it. If it's there so you can pass an exam nobody is setting, cut it.

### 1.12 The seven levels — a diagnostic ladder

Not a method; a **lens.** Some worlds look like an ocean of ideas and turn out to be ankle-deep. This is a way of asking *how deep is mine, actually* — and, more usefully, *how deep does it need to be?*

**These are tiers of understanding, not steps.** You won't climb them in order, and you'll have pieces of several at once. **You are not failing if you sit at level 2** — plenty of great stories live there.

| | Level | The question it answers | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **A map** | Does the land obey *rules*? | Rivers that split and cross mountains. Cities placed where nobody could eat. |
| **2** | **Rule of cool** | Is there anything here I'm excited about? | **Mistaking it for depth.** |
| **3** | **Surface identity** | Could you tell where you are without a caption? | "Planet of the hats" — a whole culture reduced to one trait. |
| **4** | **Functional power** | What does this power actually *do* to the world? | Magic as decoration. |
| **5** | **Deep culture & history** | How did it *get* this way? | Lore that exists but changes nothing. |
| **6** | **Infrastructure** | Who feeds the city? How does an order reach the front? | Building this when your game will never touch it. |
| **7** | **Generative** | Can I zoom in anywhere and find a story already waiting? | — (this is the goal) |

**Level 1 — the land obeys rules.** Not necessarily *Earth's* rules. Break them freely — floating continents, a flat world on a turtle — **but know why you're breaking them, and commit.** The failure isn't fantasy; it's *arbitrariness*. Rivers should flow downhill and converge. A desert should be dry *because of something*. A city should sit where people could plausibly survive — or you should know why it doesn't.

**Level 2 — the rule of cool.** Dragons, a fallen empire nobody understands, a cursed forest, a city carved into the bones of a god. **This stage is genuinely great** — it's what gives a world identity and it's usually what makes anyone care in the first place. The *only* problem is mistaking it for depth. **Bigger is not deeper.** You can keep stacking factions and gods and ancient wars and end up with something wide and thin.

**Level 3 — surface identity.** Naming conventions, architecture, clothing, symbols. The point is that your world stops being *interchangeable* — someone sees a silhouette and knows instantly where it's from. *(This is the same technique as §1.0's commandment 4, "fake the iceberg," viewed from the outside: consistent surface detail implies a depth you never had to build.)* **The trap is the "planet of hats"** — the warrior culture, the smart culture, the sneaky culture, where everyone from a place dresses and thinks the same. Give a culture a consistent *outer language*, but let individuals inside it be individuals.

**Level 4 — functional power.** **Stop asking "is this cool?" and start asking "what does this actually do?"** If magic is common, what happened to labour? If dragons exist, what happened to warfare? A power that exists but changes nothing isn't a power, it's set dressing.
> **This is exactly §1.0's commandment 3 ("let your magic break your world"), arrived at independently.** Two sources converging this hard is a strong signal — treat it as settled.

The payoff is that **the world stops feeling like things happen because the plot needs them to**, and starts feeling like it would have reacted this way whether or not the heroes existed.

**Level 5 — deep culture & history.** The world stops *feeling* old and starts *being* old. Not what people wear — what they **believe**, what they **argue about**, what they're **embarrassed by**, what they **refuse to let go of even though it stopped making sense.** Traditions that began for practical reasons and became sacred. Taboos nobody questions.
- And history isn't "there was a war 500 years ago." It's **the ripple**: the borders that still exist because of it, the ruins that redirect trade, the religion that fractured into three feuding branches.
- **The best trick at this level: let cultures disagree with themselves.** Different peoples remember the same event differently. Doctrines contradict. Factions reinterpret the past to justify the present. **Real people hold beliefs that don't line up, and act from emotion rather than logic.** A world whose history has *one clean version* feels young. One that's been arguing with its own past for centuries feels ancient.

**Level 6 — infrastructure & institutions.** Banking, trade routes, logistics. If the city has 200,000 people, who feeds them? *(The source is candid that most people never need this — "you don't need agricultural output tables to run a fun D&D campaign.")* **See §1.11 — this is precisely the layer our city source tells you to skip, and they're both right:** build it only if it will *generate constraint at the table.* The good examples all do exactly that — a bank that finances wars and switches sides when you default is a *plot engine*, not a spreadsheet.

**Level 7 — the generative world.** The point where **the world starts producing stories for you.** Zoom in anywhere — any city, any port, any century — and there's already a tension sitting there, waiting. You stop forcing plot into empty space and start *uncovering* the plots that logically exist.
> **This is the same finish line as §1.0's "drama that constructs itself."** Both sources say it independently, and it's the best single test of whether world-building is working.

#### What this means for us

**The ladder is not a to-do list — it's a diagnostic, and the honest answer is that we should deliberately skip a rung.**

- **Levels 1–3** — yes, cheaply. Our map should make sense; the rule of cool is *how we hook kids*; and surface identity is just "fake the iceberg" (§1.0), which is the highest-value-per-minute technique we have.
- **Level 4** — **yes, emphatically.** This is where a world starts to feel real, and it costs one good question.
- **Level 5** — yes, but *lightly*. "This festival started as something practical and is now sacred, and nobody remembers why" is one sentence and a kid will love it.
- **Level 6** — **skip.** This is the exact layer §1.10 identifies as the road to burnout. Build a bank only if the bank is going to *do something to the party*.
- **Level 7** — the goal, and reachable **without** level 6. §1.0's method (central tension → factions → clocks) gets you a generative world *directly*, without the trade logistics.

**The source's own closing line is the right one, and it's the same note as commandment 10:** this isn't about talent, it's about **asking better questions.** *Why is this here? Who benefits from it? What happens if it changes?*

### 1.13 Dungeons

> **A great dungeon isn't a series of rooms. It's a journey through decisions.**

The default failure: players walk in, take the obvious path, and grind through rooms hoping for XP and gold. **It becomes a checklist, not an adventure.**

#### 1.13.0 ⚠️ Read this first: "dungeon" is a *shape*, not a place

**This is the most important note in the section, and it's easy to miss.**

If you spend three seconds actually thinking about what a dungeon *is*, it's a deeply strange object:

> **An enclosed space containing a series of challenges, which must be overcome in sequence, after which those who finish are rewarded — with plot, with loot, or both.**

There is essentially **no real-world equivalent.** (Egyptian tombs come closest, and even they're built with the *opposite* goal — to stop people getting in — and have no bosses and no combat encounters.) **Dungeons are the one part of D&D that never even attempted realism, and that's fine.** Don't try to make them make sense. Make them *cool*.

**But here's why that definition matters: nothing in it says "underground," "stone," or "tomb."**

A "dungeon" is any **bounded space with a sequence of challenges and a goal.** So everything in this section applies to:

| It looks like… | …but it's a dungeon |
|---|---|
| A heist on a bank or a manor | Bounded space, sequence of challenges, a goal |
| A haunted house | ″ |
| A besieged castle — from either side | ″ |
| A carnival, a circus, a festival | ″ |
| A ship, a train, a caravan | ″ |
| A forest that doesn't want you to leave | ″ |
| A magical academy's final exam | ″ |
| A city district under lockdown | ″ |
| **A court, a council, a negotiation with several gatekeepers** | ″ — the "rooms" are people |

**And the crossover runs both ways.** Look at how much this rhymes with things already in this document:
- §1.10's **city "rides"** are dungeon encounters wearing a hat. Same creator, same gameplay-first logic.
- §6.2's **point crawl** is a dungeon's branching structure applied to a map.
- §6.8's **TEST events** (red/yellow/blue) are exactly §1.13.2's encounter types.
- §5's whole apparatus (value shifts, escalation, character moments) is what happens *inside* the rooms.

> **Practical upshot: whenever you're building anything with a bounded space, a sequence, and a goal — reach for this section.** The heist, the ball, the escort, the exam. It is not just for tomb-raiding.

#### 1.13.1 The build method: theme → mechanic → goal → encounters → ending

**Step 1 · Select a THEME.** Not a setting — a *theme*. Is this dungeon about **death**? **Healing**? **Lies**? **Friendship**? This sounds lofty; it isn't. **The setting then falls out of the theme.**
- *Theme: **lies**.* → artifice, illusions → so the setting could be a house of mirrors, or a haunted masquerade, or a carnival.

**Step 2 · Pick a MECHANIC. This is the step everyone skips, and it's the one that matters.**

> **Find a system of rules in the game that expresses your theme — so the players don't just *hear* the theme, they *play* it.**

People do this instinctively with narrative and almost never with mechanics. You can build a dungeon "about death," fill it with zombies and necromancers, have NPCs tell sad stories about people they lost — **and never once let the theme touch the rules.**

- *Theme **sleep*** → mess with **short rests**, **hit dice**, **exhaustion levels**, the **sleep condition**.
- *Theme **lies*** → **illusions**. Tons of spells to draw on, enemies can use them, *the dungeon itself* can use them, and you get to play with perception checks. Cheap and devastating.

**Step 3 · Select a GOAL.** How do they *win*? There are far more options than "kill the thing in the big circular room":
- Reach the end. Defeat the boss. **Find** something hidden. **Escort** someone through in one piece. **Deactivate** something.
- *Nice variation:* the artifact is sitting **in plain sight behind a force field** — and the dungeon is about switching the force field off.
- **Vary the goal between dungeons**, especially if you run them often.

**Step 4 · Build a VARIETY of encounters.** (§1.13.2)

**Step 5 · Give it an ENDING — and make the ending the *collision* of your theme and your mechanic.**

Notice it says *ending*, not *boss*. It's often a boss (players like bosses), but it can equally be a **final puzzle** or a **skill challenge**.

**The rule: take every way you expressed your theme through mechanics during the dungeon, and dump the whole bucket on the ending.**

This is a standard game-design move: **introduce a mechanic somewhere safe so players can learn it (your encounters), then make them use it somewhere dangerous (your ending).**
- *Lies/illusions:* if they've fought in a hall of mirrors, and they've fought an enemy that split into illusory copies — **then the boss does both, at once.**
- **Players love this** because they already know how to engage with it. They're not learning the rules under pressure; they're *proving they learned them*. That's a great feeling, and it's the whole reason a boss fight can be satisfying rather than just long.

#### 1.13.2 Encounter types — and encounter ≠ combat

A dungeon of nothing but combat, with the occasional trap, is deeply boring. **You need hills and valleys.** Pace cannot be maintained by constant fighting.

**Three types** *(and yes — these are exactly the red/yellow/blue of §6.8):*

- **A · Combat.** You know this one.
- **B · Trap / puzzle.** *(Rolled into one deliberately — see §1.13.7.)* A **trap** is something set up for others to *fall into*. A **puzzle** is something set up for others to *solve*.
- **C · Roleplay.** **The forgotten one.** A guardian blocks the way and demands help in exchange for passage. Do you intimidate them? Help them? Talk to them and find out what their deal actually is?

**Then push your mechanic through all three.** Theme *lies*, mechanic *illusions*:
- **Combat:** a fight in a hall of mirrors, where they must use the mirrors to work out which enemies are real — **but while you're looking at a mirror you aren't looking at the enemy, so the enemy has advantage.** The theme is now a *tactical dilemma*.
- **Trap/puzzle:** the key is in a room containing *thousands* of illusory keys. Check them by hand and you'll be here for a lifetime — or find and disable the crystals projecting them.
- **Roleplay:** the NPC is lying. The NPC is not what they appear to be. **The NPC is the one who sent you in here, and had reasons for it.**

> **Players will usually remember the encounters *leading up to* the boss more vividly than the boss itself** — because that's most of the dungeon.

#### 1.13.3 The resource budget (and the metric nobody uses)

**Why do dungeons mechanically exist? To deplete the party's resources before the boss.** Without a run-up, the party strolls to the boss at full strength and **goes nova**, burning everything in the first two rounds and deleting your villain.

**How much depletion?** Balancing it is genuinely tricky, because *you* aren't the one spending the resources.

**The metric: hit dice — not spell slots.**

> **If they reach the boss with more than one hit die left in the pool, you probably went too easy on them.**

**Why spell slots are a bad measure:** not every class has them; casters vary enormously in slots and recovery; and players differ wildly in how cautiously they hoard. Too many variables. **Every class has hit dice, and they scale with level.**

**Starting number: 4–6 encounters per dungeon.** (And a **4–5 session cap** on a single dungeon is a sane default — people need to see the sun.)

#### 1.13.4 The problem: most dungeon "choices" aren't choices

Picture a restaurant menu with 47 items. You panic slightly, and you order the burger. **Was that a choice, or a surrender?**

**Three ways a fork fails:**

1. **One path is obviously better.** There's a golden glow, or friendly NPCs, or visibly less danger. Two paths, but one is glowing — **that's a railroad wearing a costume.**
2. **They have no information at all.** No clue, no context, nothing to reason from. You've asked them to **flip a coin.** They're not choosing, they're playing the lottery.
3. **Nothing is sacrificed.** Both paths lead to roughly the same outcome, loot, or story. **Players have a spider-sense for this** — they detect it instantly and immediately stop caring.

#### 1.13.5 The anatomy of a real choice

All four, or the choice doesn't exist:

- **No dominant option.** Neither path can be obviously correct.
- **Enough information to *reason* with.** Reason is the operative word. Stop making them guess.
- **A genuine trade-off.** Choosing one thing means *losing* another.
- **Outcomes that reflect the investment.**

**What a good fork looks like:**
> *Left: bloodstains on the stone. Dead silence. Cold air bleeding through the doorway.*
> *Right: strange flickering light. Whispers that might be voices — or might be wind. And warmth. Almost too much warmth.*

Neither is obviously right, and **both cost something**: one promises danger you can *see*; the other, danger you can't identify. You're not asking them to solve a puzzle — **you're asking them to commit to an interpretation of thin evidence.** That's a choice.

#### 1.13.6 The fork checklist

1. **Test your forks.** Run twenty imaginary parties through it in your head. **If 80% would pick the same path, you've built a false choice.** Redesign it.
2. **Two to three clues per path.** Zero clues = guessing. *Exhaustive* clues = you've made the answer obvious.
   - *Too few:* two doors, one wood, one iron. Coin flip.
   - *Too many:* claw marks **and** the smell of rot **and** growling **and** dried blood **and** the ranger's senses prickling. You've answered it for them.
   - **The target: let them theorise without letting them be certain.**
3. **Make the trade-off explicit.** *The left path reaches the boss before the ritual completes. The right path is longer but goes through the treasure room.* So the real choice is: **arrive early and unprepared, or late but armed.**

#### 1.13.7 Corridors are not dead space

Once they've chosen, they walk — and typically **nothing happens.** But you have their curiosity *right now*, and doing nothing with it is how you lose it.

**Run corridors on the engine that mysteries run on: the gap.** A good mystery works because you *almost* know something. Your brain treats the gap as an itch it needs to scratch.

**Two questions for every corridor and room:**
1. **"What question does this space put in my players' heads?"** *Why are these torches dead? Why is this stretch of floor different?* **If you cannot name a specific question, the space is dead** — put something in it that makes them wonder.
2. **"Can they answer that question immediately?"** If yes, **pull back**. You've filled the gap. Leave room for theorising.

**Corridors are where you build anticipation** — which is what makes the rooms land. **Tension and release, like music.** They are not the boring bits between the interesting bits.

#### 1.13.8 Run every trap as a puzzle

**Both dungeon sources land on this independently, from different angles. Treat it as settled.**

**Why traps are bad by default.** A hidden pit with no warning is a **gotcha**: there's no engagement before it fires, and mechanically it's just **a random tax on hit points.** And the deeper problem is what the *player* actually does: **they roll a saving throw.** That's it. Dodging darts might look great on film, but at the table it demands no skill from the person sitting in the chair.

> **The rule this points at is bigger than traps: think about what the *player actually does* when they interact with your encounter — not what it looks like in your head. Plenty of things are fun to watch and not fun to play.**

**Two fixes, and they stack:**

**A · Seed it, so it can be theorised about.** *The dust in this hallway is disturbed in an odd pattern, and one flagstone looks newer than the others.* Add a **barely audible click.** (Behind an Investigation roll if you like.) Now they can reason: *is it on a timer? Does the rogue have the right tools? If we blow this, is it "ouch" bad or "oh no" bad?*

**B · Make it an actual puzzle — something to *solve*, not something to fall into.**
- *The room is filling with water and they will drown.* They **can** tear the cover off the valve that's releasing it — but first they have to work out how the console operates. **Now they're solving a pipe puzzle to stop themselves dying**, rather than taking turns rolling against a DC.

**A trap becomes a test of the players, not just of their character sheets.**

#### 1.13.9 Layered reveals — make them *earn* information

Three tiers, and they're the pacing mechanism for the whole dungeon:

| Tier | What it costs | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Free on arrival** | Nothing | The shape of the room, the temperature |
| **Reward for engagement** | Asking a good question, paying attention | Scratches on the wall, the broken torches |
| **Demands investigation** | **Time and resources** | The loose stone, the thing behind it |

> **This makes exploration feel like *earning* information rather than *receiving* it.**

#### 1.13.10 Monsters: design so that holding back *hurts*

Here's the psychology, and it's worth internalising: **humans are loss-averse — losing something hurts about twice as much as gaining the same thing feels good.**

So a player hoarding a potion while their character slides toward death **is not being stupid.** Their brain is ranking *"I no longer have this potion"* above *"I am currently dying."* Same with the wizard guarding a high-level slot three encounters into the day. **It isn't rational — but it is completely predictable. Which means you can design for it.**

> **Encounters are boring when resource management never enters the conversation. They're good when *holding back has a visible cost*.**

**So the monster shouldn't just be dangerous — it should be *doing something*.** Dragging prisoners toward a pit. Working a ritual that gets worse every round it completes.

**The test: "What happens if they hold back?"** If the answer is *"nothing — they just take longer to win,"* the encounter won't force any interesting decisions. **Add a clock: something that gets worse while they play it safe.**

#### 1.13.11 Retreat as a trade-off, not a failure

Most groups never retreat. It's win-and-loot, or TPK. But if you **teach** them retreat is real — with subtle clues to chew on — the whole resource conversation changes.

**Aim for: retreat feels possible, but costly.** The guardian room has a clear exit — but taking it means the ritual completes, the prisoners die, and the treasure is gone for good.

**The test: "What would retreat cost them?"** If there's no answer, invent one. **Never "you can't retreat." Always "if you retreat, you lose X."** Done right, a retreat is a *dramatic beat* — think of the flight from the mines.

#### 1.13.12 Make resting a decision

If resting costs nothing, it isn't a decision — they'll just rest whenever the maths says to. An hour is not, in itself, expensive.

**Unless the dungeon changes while they rest.** The boss relocates. The patrol rotates. A window closes. **Now they're doing calculations**, and the slots and potions they already spent are suddenly *in the balance*.

**The test: "What changes if they take a rest?"** If the dungeon politely waits for them, rest stops being part of the story and becomes a checkbox on a character sheet.

#### 1.13.13 Three caveats

1. **Think about the group, not individuals.** The goal isn't to design fights that stop the warlock using their at-will blast.
2. **A day is meant to hold several encounters.** Don't build every fight to drain the party completely.
3. **This is not DM vs. players.** These tools exist to create tension and interesting situations — **not to counter the party's toolkit.** You are not trying to win.

**→ Our table:**
- **§1.13.0 is the headline.** We should stop reading "dungeon" as *underground* and start reading it as *bounded challenge space*. That reframes half our future content — the haunted mill, the goblin market, the sunken ship, the wizard's exam, the negotiation with four stubborn guild-masters. All dungeons. All buildable with this section.
- **Theme → mechanic (§1.13.1) is the highest-value idea and the easiest to under-use.** Our simplified system actually *helps* here: with fewer moving parts, one twisted mechanic reads loud and clear. A dungeon about **lies** where perception rolls can't be trusted. A dungeon about **exhaustion** where you can't rest. A dungeon about **greed** where every treasure taken makes the way out harder. Kids feel a bent rule immediately, and it lands far harder than any amount of spooky description.
- **The ending = theme + mechanic collision (§1.13.1, step 5)** is the single best structural trick we've found for making a boss satisfying. The kids get to *prove they learned the dungeon.*
- **Traps as puzzles (§1.13.8) matters double for a 9-year-old.** An unavoidable gotcha just feels **unfair**, and unfair is the fastest way to lose a kid. A trap they *spot and beat* is one of the best feelings the game offers.
- **The fork test (§1.13.6)** — *run twenty imaginary parties; if 80% pick the same path, it's fake* — is a check I can genuinely do in thirty seconds.
- **Retreat (§1.13.11) is our pressure valve.** Given no-perma-death, "if you run, the ritual completes and the prisoners are lost" buys us real fear without ever risking a character.
- **The hit-dice metric (§1.13.3)** may need rethinking for our system, but the *principle* — pick one simple resource and watch it — survives the simplification.

## 2 · Story & Campaign Arcs

### 2.1 The five-step campaign launch sequence

Do these **in order** before session one. Most people start at step 2 or 3 and it's why campaigns fizzle.

**1. TONE — decide how it should *feel*.**
Not genre, not plot: mood. Comedic? Horrific? Emotional? Epic? Find it by asking *"which of my favourite stories do I want this to FEEL like?"* — not play like, not resemble plot-wise. **Feel like.**
- *Epic* (Lord of the Rings): grand, earnest, world-ending stakes, the story is the most important thing happening anywhere.
- *Adventurous* (Avatar: The Last Airbender): light and fun, room for jokes, but punctuated by genuinely emotional and occasionally dark beats.
- **The point of naming your tone is that you can then COMMUNICATE it.** The classic failure — running a grim horror game while the players crack jokes — is usually a tone that was never stated out loud.
- **→ Our table:** our tone is already decided and it's essentially **Adventurous**, leaning family with a light edge. Spooky villains, real stakes, gentle dark themes (betrayal, loss), cheeky humour that winks at the adults. This is our north star; check every session against it. See `CAMPAIGN.md`.

**2. CONCEPT — high or low?**
- **High concept**: the premise itself is the hook and takes up a lot of room. *"Spellcasting is regulated because casters risk possession by extraplanar beings, which has led to their systematic oppression."* Easy to pitch, drives the setting.
- **Low concept**: premise is simple, so the *characters* fill the space. *"You work for a guild and take jobs off the notice board."*
- Neither is wrong. High concept = the world is the story. Low concept = the people are the story. Just know which one you're running.

**3. SETTING — derive it from the concept, and only build what's live.** (See §1.1.)
The setting is a *consequence* of the concept. If the air outside cities is poisonous, then: are small towns even viable? Is there a profession for people who can travel outside? Is the protective magic issued by a government — and is that government corrupt? Those questions are bigger than the starting town but you need them answered *before* the starting town.

**4. CONFLICT — the non-negotiable one.**
No conflict, no story. And **start the players ON the conflict** — don't make them spend five sessions in a tavern hunting for the plot while the party stays together out of pure metagame obligation. That's immersion-breaking and boring.
- Campaign is about a tournament? Start as they sign up.
- Campaign is about defeating the evil wizard? Start in the dungeon holding the first artefact that can hurt him.
- **Best version: give players the campaign's conflict during character creation, and ask them to build a character who would *want* to be part of it.** Let them supply their own reason.
- *(Pure sandbox — waffling with no plot — is a legitimate style, but it's a deliberate choice, not a default. If there IS a plot, put them on it.)*

**5. INCLUDE YOUR PLAYERS — the step almost everyone skips.**
> **The DM is not telling a story. The table is.** "Telling" implies an audience that passively listens. Your players are not an audience. This is the one thing that makes D&D different from being read a book.

Concretely, once characters exist:
- **Ask them what they want.** Not their backstory — their *hopes*. Do they want a character arc? Do they want to meet a specific NPC from their past? Do they want a mystery from their backstory answered?
- **Then do the work:** take their backstory and their stated hopes, and *sprinkle them into the campaign*. Player wants an arc → find the beats in their backstory you can challenge. Player wants to find their missing dad → the missing dad is now IN the campaign. There is no excuse for it not to be.
- **Deliberately reserve moments in play** for each player's stuff to surface.
- **Corollary: no DMPCs, no scene-stealing "important" NPCs.** Why would you prop up an NPC when you have 3–6 players who need that spotlight to feel like heroes? Give the big character moments, the hard choices, the great triumphs and great defeats **to them**.
  - **→ Our table:** with a 9- and 10-year-old, this is *the* highest-leverage principle in this whole document. Their character's thing showing up in the story is what hooks a kid.

### 2.2 Premise vs. Idea — the Seed Question

> **A premise is a synopsis. An idea is a premise + a question.**

Most DMs stop at the premise: *"An ancient wizard has grown in power over centuries; now he wants to conquer the world and become a god."* It has a villain, a conflict, and stakes, so it *feels* like a campaign. But "stop the bad guy" is a cliché, and it goes hollow fast. **The best campaigns are *about* something.**

The campaigns that go boring, where sessions feel disconnected and nobody's invested — they never found their question.

#### The six-step diagnosis

**Step 1 — Who / What / Where.**
Sanity check the premise has all three. *Who* is the antagonist or force behind the conflict? *What* do they want? *Where* is it happening? Most DMs have at least this much. This is not the hard part.

**Step 2 — Find the Seed Question.**
Everything else grows out of this, hence the name. **It is not a plot question.** Don't just bolt a question mark onto the premise — *"will the heroes stop the wizard?"* is useless. The seed question is **philosophical**: it's about how the world works, or how people work.

Ask: **what is this campaign actually about? What theme am I exploring with the players?**

Three rules for a good one:
1. **Reasonable people could disagree about the answer.** If everyone at the table would answer identically, it isn't a question.
2. **The villain and the players engage with it from different angles.** It belongs to the whole campaign, not one side.
3. **Every single PC can have a personal relationship with it.** If a PC doesn't care about it, that player will be bored — and a bored player poisons the table.

- *Example:* **"Who gets to decide what the world should look like?"**
- Watch what it does to the villain without changing the premise at all: the wizard isn't a power-mad maniac any more (well — he is, but now there's a floor under him). He's someone who looked closely at the world, saw corruption, famine, and endless war, and concluded that *he* — with genuine vision and centuries of perspective — should be in charge, because he could actually fix it. And with godhood on the table, maybe he could.
- And it changes the *players'* experience: now every faction, every NPC, every choice passes through a *"who gets to decide"* filter. Is the king actually better? Isn't he the latest in a line of corrupt nobles? The party will grow powerful and shape the kingdom's fate themselves — but are *they* legitimate?
- **The seed question turns the campaign into a conversation.**

**Step 3 — Make the villain the most persuasive wrong answer.**
Does the villain represent a *specific answer* to the seed question — and is that answer **compelling**? Even though they're wrong, spend real time turning them into the best available case for the wrong side. **If players can't see why *anyone* — not them, but *anyone* — might agree with the villain even slightly, the villain is just an obstacle.** Obstacles aren't memorable.
- *The wizard's answer:* "the most capable person decides — and that's me." He's centuries old. He has watched elected leaders fail, watched nobles squander power, watched generation after generation suffer. His methods (conquest, domination) are genuinely awful and he is genuinely the villain — but you can *see* how the desperate would follow him. He's credible. Now he's not an NPC, he's a **philosophical position the players must confront.**

**Step 4 — Build the Conflict Web.**
**This is the trap in the method:** if only the villain and the PCs engage with the question, you've built a chess game, not a world. Fix it by making **every major faction and important NPC a different answer to the same question.** No perfect option among them.
- *Crown loyalists:* bloodline decides. Tradition is stability. Yes, the heir is a child — she'll grow.
- *The People's Council:* everyone decides together. Elections, representation. Messy, slow, and — as we all know — vulnerable to charismatic operators who weaponise the system.
- *The Merchant Consortium:* whoever controls the resources decides. They'd never say it that way; they talk about stability and growth, and they sound *extremely* reasonable.
- *The hermit druids:* **nobody** decides. Civilisation is closer to a disease than people admit. Nature is cruel, but it balances — and balance is the best you get.
- Without the seed question these are just "the noble faction, the shady faction, and the weird guys in the forest" — backdrop. **The question gives them reasons, and reasons are what make players care.**

**Step 5 — Give every PC a personal stake.**
Not a quest hook — a stake. Something in their backstory or values that makes the campaign's tension *their* tension.
> **The test: if you could lift a character out of this campaign and drop them into a completely different one without changing anything, their link to the idea is too weak.**

- *Fighter:* served in the old king's army and watched him make terrible decisions. Loyal — but starting to wonder whether loyalty is owed to a ruler or to a people.
- *Warlock:* their patron **is** the villain, and they may not know it yet. They've seen his vision from the inside, and parts of it aren't crazy. *(Only do this if you know the player well enough to be sure they'd find the reveal delicious rather than a betrayal.)*
- *Cleric:* raised on collective rule — their father sits on the People's Council. But corrupt new members have hurt him, and the doubts are creeping in.

**Two conditions for step 5 to work:**
1. **Players must know the meta-tone and be on board.** They need to have agreed to a morally grey political fantasy with hard choices. If they showed up to kill goblins in a dungeon — which is a completely great thing to want — this isn't the campaign for them.
2. **Do NOT tell the players the seed question.** Naming it kills the organic conversation. Give them the vibe, and give them the factual detail their characters would know about the factions — then let them *discover* the question by playing. **The seed question is your compass, not their briefing.**

**Step 6 — Stress-test it against session one.**
> **Can your first session put the seed question on the table within the first hour, without you ever naming it?**

The goal: players walk out of session one **already arguing about the theme without realising it.** If you can do that, the idea is sound. **If session one is any variant of "you meet in a tavern, here's a quest," the question isn't planted deeply enough in the world.**

- *Example opening:* the party arrives in the city. The old king died three days ago. The heir is a child. There's no clear successor and the streets are taut. Then the **trigger** — royal soldiers arrive to escort the child princess to her coronation, the crowd turns hostile, **someone throws a stone**, and a soldier moves on the thrower.
- The party is *in it* instantly. Protect the princess? Shield the crowd? Disarm the soldier? Grab the princess themselves? **It doesn't matter which** — every one of those is the beginning of an answer to *who gets to decide.* The question entered the room the moment the stone was thrown, and nobody had to say it out loud.
- *If you can't get the question to surface in session one,* trace it backwards: usually the conflict web isn't connected tightly enough to the opening situation, or your NPCs' positions aren't being **expressed through actions**.

**→ Our table:** this works for kids — you just pick a question a 9-year-old can feel. *"What do you owe the people who raised you?" "Is it ever okay to break a promise to do the right thing?" "Who deserves to be trusted with something powerful?"* Rule 1 still holds: the adults and the kids should be able to genuinely disagree. And step 3 is the one that makes a villain *scary in a good way* — a bad guy with a point is far more interesting than a monster, and far less nightmare-fuel than one with none.

### 2.3 Theme as foundation, not decoration (the Brennan method)

This is the Seed Question (§2.2) taken all the way — and it explains *why* the seed question works.

The observation that starts it: the DMs people call geniuses are usually credited with **improv** and **voices**. But plenty of DMs have both and don't produce the same effect. Being brilliant at improv doesn't give you the power to make a table cry. So what's the actual difference?

**The difference is that the campaigns are *about* something — structurally, not decoratively.** A campaign that looks like "Game of Thrones with candy people" is actually asking *what legitimises political authority, and when is rebellion justified?* One that looks like a magical high-school comedy is actually about *building meaning after you leave behind the belief system you grew up in.*

#### Mechanism 1 — Theme as foundation, not a coating

Most DMs use a theme as flavour: *"this campaign is about justice."* It's sprinkled on top, it adds a little depth, and the game remains a game. The purpose is **escapism** — and there is genuinely nothing wrong with that.

The alternative approach inverts it: **fantasy as a *map for reality*, not an escape from it.** You're not distancing yourself from real life; you're using the game to see real life in a different light. That gives the campaign a purpose immediately, and a foundation solid enough to build everything else on.

**The Trojan horse.** Here's the clever part. If someone announced *"our next campaign will explore political philosophy,"* half the table would recoil — *I just wanted to kill goblins.* But an absurd premise (literal candy people) **lowers the guard**. Your brain files "candy kingdom" somewhere different from "medieval monarchy," and while it's filed there, the campaign can explore power, legitimacy, oppression, and class — and land it *hard*, because the emotional defences never went up.

> **A silly surface is the best possible delivery system for a serious question.**

#### Mechanism 2 — Mechanics for simulation, humans for meaning

A striking reframe of what the rules are *for*:

> People say that because D&D has so many combat mechanics, you're destined to tell combat stories. That's like looking at a stove and concluding it has nothing to do with food — clearly it's for moving gas around.

**The system should simulate the things humans *don't* intuitively understand** — how an arrow moves through fictional space, how spells interact, movement, physics, damage. That's exactly what rules are good at. **Which frees the DM and players to handle what humans *do* understand intuitively: emotions, relationships, and moral choices.**

And this leads somewhere unexpectedly important — **trust**:

- Run rules as written, roll in the open, announce DCs before players roll.
- **Why it matters for meaning:** if players suspect you're fudging rolls or bending rules, **their moral choices become meaningless.** Why agonise over a decision if the DM will make whatever happen anyway? The dilemma stops being a dilemma and becomes *"how do I game the DM?"*
- **When the mechanics are stable and fair, the link between choice and consequence is crystal clear** — and the choice lands on the player *personally*. It's their values being tested, not a story they're passively riding.

#### Mechanism 3 — Identity built from values, not tactics

In most campaigns, a character's identity accretes from **tactical patterns**: *I'm the one who scouts ahead. I'm the one who charges in.* Those matter, but they're shallow.

The alternative forces identity to be built from **ethical commitments**: *I'm the person who chose mercy over revenge. I'm the person who sacrificed innocents to stop something worse.*

Two things follow:
- **Accumulated choices become a person.** A redemption in session 20 retroactively recolours a failure from session 3. This is how identity works in life, just compressed and intensified.
- **Antagonists who embody philosophies can't be solved by killing them.** You have to understand the ideology underneath and dismantle *that*. Which — usefully — is how real problems actually work; they're systemic. *(Straight back to §4.1: the villain as the most persuasive wrong answer.)*

#### Why it hits so hard

Moral dilemmas activate two systems at once — the fast, emotional, gut-reaction one and the slow, deliberate, calculating one — and they pull in **opposite directions**. That internal conflict is what makes such choices memorable.

And a tabletop game amplifies it beyond what any other medium can. A video game's outcomes are pre-coded; a book gives you no agency at all. **At a table, the uncertainty is genuinely real — the DM doesn't know what the players will choose either.** They aren't picking from a dialogue tree; they're actually working the problem. That unpredictability mirrors real moral life.

**And it's safe.** The stakes feel real, and the moral reasoning *is* real — but nobody gets hurt. **You get to practise being the kind of person you want to be.**

#### How to actually start

> **Replace "what happens next?" with "what does this mean?"**

Start with **one question per arc.** *Do the ends justify the means? When is rebellion justified? What do we owe the people who raised us?* Then build situations that make the players answer it **through action**.

**→ Our table:** this is the most important entry in this document for us, and it's the one that most needs translating. We are *already running the Trojan horse* — "a silly, friendly, kid-safe fantasy game" is a perfect delivery system for real questions, and kids engage with fairness, loyalty, betrayal, and mercy far more seriously than adults expect. The pitch of the question is what changes, not the depth. *"Is it okay to break a promise to do the right thing?"* is a real philosophical question and a 9-year-old will fight you about it for an hour.
Two specific implications:
- **The trust point argues against fudging rolls.** Tempting with kids — you want to save them from a bad outcome. But if they figure out the dice don't matter, then *their choices* don't matter either. Better lever: our no-perma-death rule means a bad roll leads to capture, rescue, or setback — **so we don't need to fudge, because failure was never fatal.** Roll in the open and let it ride.
- **"Identity from values, not tactics"** is the whole reason to run this for the kids at all. *"I'm the one who went back for the scared goblin"* is worth a hundred sessions of *"I'm the one who does the most damage."*

### 2.4 The campaign-launch checklist (short form)

- [ ] Tone named, in one word, and said out loud to the table
- [ ] Concept picked, and I know if it's high or low
- [ ] Setting built *outward from the concept*, only as far as is live
- [ ] Conflict defined, and the players **start on it**
- [ ] **Seed question found** — reasonable people could disagree
- [ ] **Villain is the most persuasive wrong answer** to it
- [ ] **Every faction is a different answer** to it (the conflict web)
- [ ] **Every PC has a personal stake** in it (and would break if moved to another campaign)
- [ ] **Session one puts the question in play inside an hour — without naming it**
- [ ] Every player asked what they want out of this
- [ ] Every player's backstory has at least one thread planted in the world
- [ ] Zero DMPCs

## 3 · NPCs

### 3.1 The core reframe: build from the inside out

Most NPC advice starts on the **outside** — a name, a funny voice, a quirk, maybe a short backstory. This is backwards, and it's why players can't remember anyone's name three sessions later.

> **A novel's character has to be interesting in themselves. An NPC does not — an NPC has to *do something to your players*.**

That's the difference between a **character** and a **force**. A force creates pressure. Pressure creates memorable moments. So build the *engine* first; the voice and the funny hat are the last coat of paint, not the foundation.

The failure mode: a **prop with a job**. "Mevard is a middle-aged cloth merchant who's been in town six weeks." He only reacts when players show up. Even if you make him a quest-giver, he'll hand over the bandit job, they'll do it, get paid, and never think about him again. He's *served his function* and there's nothing left to chew on.

### 3.2 The five layers (build in this order)

**Layer 1 — WANT.** *The thing that puts them in motion.*
A job means they react. A **want** means they *act* — they're pursuing something whether or not the party ever walks in. Without a want, an NPC isn't wrong or unbelievable, just **empty**, and empty is forgettable.
- *Mevard:* his younger brother, a novice in the church of the god of light, wrote a journal of everything he saw in the monastery. The brother died; the journal vanished. Mevard has hunted it for two years, and picked *this town* because he thinks it brings him closer.
- Same merchant, same face — but now every conversation has an undertone, and the bandit quest gets deeper (maybe the bandits have the journal).

**Layer 2 — WOUND.** *The emotional weight under the want.*
A want on its own is a shopping list: it says *what* they're doing, but the *why* stays fragile — and a clean, logical want just gets **solved**. Players hand over the thing and move on.
Stack a wound on it and the NPC becomes **unsolvable**, because the want stops being about the object and starts being about *proving something* or *becoming someone*.
- *Mevard:* he was on the road *with* the journal when bandits attacked. He panicked and fled, leaving it behind. His brother was already dead — he didn't sacrifice anyone — but the journal was the last thing he had and **he didn't fight for it.** Shame and regret.
- Now handing him the journal doesn't close the wound. He needs to *see himself act*. The journal is just a symbol he's decided will prove he's not a coward. Irrational — and therefore human, and therefore playable.
- **Wounds do not have to be visible.** Players may never learn he ran. *You* know, and it colours every response: if they help him, he's a shade *too* grateful, and it lands a little odd — because deep down he knows he needs help precisely because he failed. That's the tell of a good wound: it changes your improv even when it's never revealed.

**Layer 3 — COVER.** *The active lie protecting the want.*
The best NPCs are **never fully legible** — something doesn't quite add up. But most "secrets" are **passive** (a dark past), and passive secrets are hard to bring to the table. What you want is something they are **currently doing** to protect what they want. An active lie makes an NPC feel like a **puzzle** — and, crucially, players can *see the secret in action* before they get to the bottom of it. No lore dump required.
- *Mevard:* the trade is completely real — he genuinely sells the best blankets in the region. But he recently **switched suppliers for no good reason**, purely to justify setting up shop in *this* town, because he knows the bandits are near and have the journal.
- Instantly, the world can leak: suppliers at the tavern grumbling that they don't understand why he ditched better partners; a guard mentioning it was odd that a man from a wealthier region turned up *here*. **The cover writes your hooks for you.**

**Layer 4 — MOMENTUM.** *What they're doing when nobody's watching.*
Most NPCs simply stop existing between player interactions, which is exactly why the world feels like a film set. Fix: **the NPC is already in the middle of something when the party arrives.** Talking to someone, packing crates, scribbling a letter — anything.
The effect is enormous: players don't feel like they're *starting a quest*, they feel like they've *walked into a place that already had a life*.
- *Mevard:* two weeks ago he paid a man to locate the bandit camp. He hasn't heard back. Is the man dead? Or did he find the journal, realise what it's worth, and is right now weighing whether to sell it to the clergy for a bigger payday?
- So when the party walks in, Mevard is **pacing like a maniac**, half-muttering, and stops dead the second he sees them.
- **Bonus:** momentum spawns world. We now have a second NPC — the informant — who could turn out to be a hundred different things.

**Layer 5 — IGNITION POINT.** *Let the players decide what he is.*
The risk of all this layering is that you've built a beautiful railroad. The fix: **leave the final meaning of the NPC undecided, and let the players' first reaction set it.**
Watch how they read that pacing:
- They read it as **scheming** and get aggressive → fine: Mevard wants the journal as *leverage* against the clergy, and blackmail is on the table.
- They read it as **a frightened man putting on a brave face** and show compassion → fine: Mevard is being played by the informant, who intends to betray him — and the informant becomes a low-level villain.
- **Their reaction is intel about where the table's head is at.** It tells you which story this group wants. You planned ahead, and you still get to be surprised.

### 3.3 The point

Mevard is still an awkward middle-aged man who sells blankets. The *shape* never changed. But now: he wants something costly, he's running from something he can't outrun, he's actively lying, he's in motion, and several doors are open — so **whatever the players do, there's a good story waiting.** Prop → force.

**You don't need all five layers on every NPC.** Pick what suits the role. You can even start with just a name because the players did something you didn't plan, and then — between sessions — go back and layer up the one who mattered. That's *more* organic, not less. **What matters is the direction: start with the want and build outward.**

### 3.4 Cheap tricks for the supporting cast

Not everyone gets five layers. For the rest:

- **Families are free characterisation.** An inn run by a family gives you four NPCs and a dynamic for the price of one.
- **Rivalries by default.** The mayor and the priest hate each other. A cast without friction is a cast without scenes.
- **Give powerful NPCs a reason not to solve the problem themselves.** Age, duty, a civic post, fear. *"I'm the town healer — I can't risk my life."* Without this, players correctly ask why they're needed, and start recruiting an army.
- **The inn is your ensemble scene.** Evening at the pub introduces the blacksmith, the priest, and the sheriff without visiting three buildings.

*(Town-prep mechanics — one NPC per location, the ~10-location list — live in §1.2 and §1.4.)*

**→ Our table:** the five layers scale down cleanly for kids — you just make the wound *legible and gentle*. "He's embarrassed because he ran away" is something a 9-year-old understands instantly and, more to the point, *feels*. Keep the wounds in the register of shame, loneliness, broken promises, and letting someone down — not trauma. The ignition point is the real gift here: it lets a kid's instinct ("he looks sad!" / "he's totally lying!") **be right**, which is enormously satisfying.

## 4 · Monsters & Villains

*Two different jobs.* **§4.1–4.3 = villains** (a *position*, an antagonist with a philosophy). **§4.4–4.9 = monsters** (a *thing you build and stat*). A villain is an NPC and follows §3.2. A monster is an engineering problem.

### 4.1 The villain is an answer, not an obstacle

> **Your villain should be the most persuasive possible argument for the wrong side of your seed question.** (Worked through in full at §2.2, step 3.)

The test: if players can't see why *anyone* — not them, but *anyone* — would find the villain even slightly compelling, the villain is a wall with hit points. Walls aren't memorable.

**This does not mean softening them.** One reliable shape: **right about the problem, horrifying about the solution.** The diagnosis is correct; the prescription is monstrous. That's how real people end up following terrible causes — and it's what turns a villain from an NPC into a *position the party has to argue with*.

> **⚠️ But that is one shape, not the only one — and used every time it becomes its own cliché: a parade of well-meaning tyrants.** The requirement is only that the villain **be an answer to the seed question** that *someone* could believe. That answer can be:
> - **Right about the problem, monstrous about the cure** *(the classic)*
> - **Correct, and simply willing to pay a price nobody else will** — the genuinely hardest kind to argue with
> - **Wrong, but for a reason you can feel** — grief, humiliation, a promise kept too long
> - **Entirely selfish, and completely honest about it** — refreshing, and disarming
> - **Not wrong at all.** The villain is only a villain because they're in the party's way. *(Use sparingly. Devastating.)*
>
> **Ask what this villain believes, not which template they fit.**

**The consequence:** an antagonist who embodies an idea **can't be solved by killing them.** You have to understand the ideology and dismantle *that* — which is also how real problems work, since they're systemic. (§2.3)

### 4.2 Villains need the five NPC layers — hardest of all

§3.2 applies, and applies most here. A villain with a **wound** (not just a want) can't be resolved by being defeated. A villain with **momentum** is *doing things while the party is elsewhere* — which is the whole difference between a genuine threat and a boss waiting patiently in a room for the plot to arrive.

### 4.3 Scale down for the first villain

Small, personal, *local* threats make better opening antagonists than world-enders. The town's "problem" (§1.6) is where your first monster comes from — owlbears eating the hunters, not the God-Emperor of Everything. World-ending stakes work better once the players care about a place.

**→ Our table:** §4.1 is exactly how we get "spooky villains and real stakes" without nightmares. A villain who *believes something*, and is wrong, is scary in the *interesting* way. A villain who's just cruel is scary in the way that keeps a 9-year-old awake. Give ours a reason, and let the kids figure out that the reason isn't good enough.

---

### 4.4 What makes a monster good

A checklist to run any monster against — homebrewed or pulled off the shelf. **Hit a few of these and you're fine; you don't need all of them.**

**The gate (ask this first):**
- **Does it inspire *you*?** *"If a monster doesn't excite me, I throw it out."* You're at the table to have fun too. It might be the lore, the picture, one ability, a story hook — doesn't matter. **If you're not jazzed, neither will they be.** *(Same principle as §8.1's Texture layer: the DM is a player.)*

**Its abilities:**
- **It has *cool* abilities.** The anti-example is the classic giant: clubs and rocks. That's it. *(Beware spells specifically — they slow the game and balloon your prep, because now you're cross-referencing spell descriptions mid-fight. **Spell-*like* abilities** written into the stat block are far easier to run.)*
- **⚠️ Its best move is NOT usable every round.** This is the single most-missed rule. A coolest-attack-every-turn monster is **predictable, and predictable drains all the drama.** When players *don't know* when the breath weapon comes back, they're under pressure — take it down fast, or get out of the way.
  > **This is §5.1's autopilot-turn sin, pointed at the monster.** The same disease. Monsters get bored turns too.
- **It's not a one-trick pony.** A mix of abilities means it can *adapt* when players do something insane — which they will — and gives you tools rather than a script.
- **It can do something they've never seen before.**

**Its shape:**
- **Strengths *and* weaknesses** — that make sense for the creature, but which **the players don't know up front.**
  - **Twist the expectation.** An arctic creature is resistant to cold, obviously. Players will reach for fire. So what if it's *also* resistant to fire — and wide open to poison?
  - *Great example:* a troll modified by a hag, so that when the party hit it with fire — because *everyone knows* fire beats trolls — it simply **burst into flames and took no damage.** Now they're fighting a burning troll. It was acid and cold that stopped its regeneration.
  - **A weakness turns a fight into a puzzle** instead of a hit-point slugfest. **Rule: any monster with a stack of resistances or immunities should have a weakness the players can actually find and exploit.**
- **It's something normal, with a twist.** A housefly, but huge, and it drinks spinal fluid. A panther with two extra legs, barbed tentacles, and the ability to appear where it isn't. A spider that steps between planes.
- **It plays on a real emotion** — fear, pity, intrigue. Existing fears do the heavy lifting: spiders, snakes, the dark, the dead.
- **It has a memorable appearance** — and *show them the picture*. Holding up an image does more than a paragraph of description.

**How it behaves:**
- **It has motivations.** The mantra: ***"monsters never attack first."*** Not literally true — sometimes they should — but it's a **reminder that a monster has goals, and attacking is only one way to pursue them.** Which means it can be bargained with, avoided, tricked, or turned. *(This is §3.2's **want**, applied to something with claws.)*
- **It reacts.** It changes tactics. **It flees when the tide turns.**
- **It has tactical depth** — coordinates with allies, changes strategy mid-fight.
- **It uses its environment.** *(= §5.2's three tensions.)*
- **It grows and evolves** — *if it survives*. The one goblin who ran away and came back with friends is worth more than the twelve you killed.

**Its place:**
- **It serves a purpose in the world** (ecology, lore) **and in the story.** A dragon dropped in for no reason is just a pile of hit points that could have been anything.
- **It's rewarding to defeat** — and not only in loot. Story progress, or the sheer satisfaction of having beaten something hard.
- **It's balanced *on purpose*.** Easy, medium, hard, or nearly impossible are all fine — **as long as you chose it, and you vary it.** Too many easy fights and they're bored; too many brutal ones and they're frustrated.
- **It fits your group.** Obviously.

**And above all:**
- **It's easy for YOU to run.** See §4.7 — this deserves its own section, because it's the one people get wrong.

### 4.5 The build process: goal → research → development → play-test

**Step 1 · Goal.** *Be precise.* This step steers everything after it.
- **What is its ROLE?** Minion? Rank-and-file? Lieutenant? Unique boss? **These are completely different design problems.**
- **Where's the inspiration from?** Watching a film, reading a book — **jot down in plain speech what the cool thing *did*, and how it was *beaten*.** *("Giant blob. Probably an ooze. Eats and dissolves everything it touches. Nothing hurts it except cold.")* That's already the blueprint for a monster.
- **Or: what gap am I filling?** *"I've run five swamps. My players have fought the frog people twice, plus the crocodiles and the hags. What ELSE lives in a swamp?"*
- **Write your goal in 3–5 sentences and put it at the top of the page.** *"A monster inspired by X, with roughly this ability, around this power level, whose role is [minion / standard / boss]."* Refer back to it constantly.
- **Don't start with the big bad.** If you've never homebrewed before, **build a non-unique mid-tier monster first** — the kind you can reuse all campaign.
- **Give it a stupid placeholder name.** *"Evil big fly." "Crazy surgery monster." "Sewer thing."* Incoherent but descriptive; it keeps the concept vivid. *(And sometimes the dumb name is so good it survives to print.)*

**Step 2 · Research.**
- **Check it doesn't already exist.** Nothing worse than building a monster from scratch and discovering it's 99% an existing one you could have reskinned in four minutes.
- **But: it's completely fine to do your own take on an existing idea.** Nobody owns dragons, trolls, or goblins. There is always room for another goblin.
- **Open four or five existing stat blocks at your target power level and *compare*.** Get a feel for the numbers. **Don't copy one** — there's real variation within any tier, so look at several. *(A useful trick: the dragon at each tier is a good benchmark archetype — big, high AC, lots of HP, flies, has an area attack.)*
- This is where you find out things like *"I thought 50 hit points was plenty, and everything else at this tier has 200."*

**Step 3 · Development.** → §4.6 (methods) and §4.7 (the complexity budget).

**Step 4 · Play-test.** → §4.8. **Every number you write is theoretical until it sees play.**

### 4.6 The three homebrew methods, easiest to hardest

#### A · Reskin (start here)

**Take an existing stat block and change what it *is*, not what it *does*.** You can start from a concept and go find a matching stat block, or start from an interesting stat block and reimagine it. Either direction works.

**The lightest version:** change the creature type or the damage type. A troll that lives in a volcano — now it's on fire, fire *doesn't* stop its regeneration, and the players have to find another answer. Cheap, fast, and you can do it mid-session.

**To make a reskin genuinely unrecognisable:** identify the creature's **core distinguishing abilities** — the ones that make it *that* monster and not another — then find a **completely different fiction that produces the same mechanics.**
- *Worked example:* a cloaker's core is (1) it attaches to you, bites, and blinds you, and (2) it's very good at hiding. So — what *else* could attach and blind? **A ghost**, which "attaches" by occupying your space, and blinds you the way *it* was blinded: a phantom golden crown, now visible on your head, melting down over your face. The piercing damage becomes fire. It hides by turning transparent rather than by camouflage.
- **Nobody at the table will recognise that as an angry coat.** And mechanically, almost nothing changed.
- Often the whole concept comes from **one fun idea for one feature.** Then you walk the rest of the stat block and see what needs to follow. Usually **just re-describing it is enough.**

**Two warnings:**
1. **Be careful with ability scores — they cascade.** They feed hit points, AC, and attack rolls all at once. *Bumping one stat by three points on a weak creature can effectively double its hit points.* **Safer: change the mental stats, leave the physical ones alone.** Better still, **pick a starting stat block whose strengths already match what you want.**
2. **Damage types are not interchangeable.** Players have abilities keyed to specific types — swapping one can accidentally neuter a character or hand another a free win.

#### B · Frankenstein (combine stat blocks)

1. **Compare the two power levels and pick your target.** Anything you take from the weaker one gets **scaled up**. Pull the ability scores from whichever creature is already at your target level.
2. **Match abilities up by *purpose*** — same function, same action type, same power level. **Pairing them makes the whole thing far less overwhelming.** Where two do the same job, keep one and cut the other.
3. **When two abilities are both complex — pick one.** Don't keep both.
4. **Lazy maths is fine.** If the creature you're building on has ~50% more hit points, scale the borrowed numbers by ~50%. That's genuinely close enough.
5. **For abilities with no counterpart, ask what it's *for* and whether you still need it.** (A charge move for extra mobility is pointless on something that already flies.)

#### C · Power-level conversion (the most useful trick of the three)

**You want a specific monster but it's wildly wrong for the party's level.**

**Don't rebuild it. Take a creature at the RIGHT power level with similar strengths, and apply the concept you want onto *its* mechanics.**

- *Worked example:* you want a three-headed chimera, but the party is far too weak for one. Find a low-tier creature with a big bite. **Split its single bite into three smaller attacks** to recreate the three-pronged multiattack — same total-ish damage, completely different feel. Keep the knock-prone effect. Its existing spray attack becomes the breath weapon; just change the damage type.
- **It works in reverse too** — scale a low-tier creature *up* by modelling it on a higher one with similar strengths.
- **⚠️ Warning:** big jumps break things. **Anything that reduces maximum hit points is dramatically more dangerous at low levels, even if you scale the damage.** Watch for effects whose severity doesn't scale linearly.

### 4.7 ⭐ The complexity budget (the section that matters most)

**The factor almost nobody discusses in monster design: how much mental load does the DM carry to run this thing?**

There are beautifully intricate monsters out there that **would be superb if a computer were running them.** You are not a computer. And it gets worse in groups — four monsters with six abilities each means **you are tracking thirty abilities.**

**The anchoring fact:**

> **Your monster will live for three to five rounds. It will take three to five turns. It gets to do three to five cool things — total.** *Anything you give it beyond that will simply never happen.*

So practise economy.

**The recipe:**

- **One really solid basic attack.** And make it *worth using* — **it should not be the thing the monster does when it has nothing better to do.** Make the default cool.
- **Then one to three of:**
  - **An opening move** — something big, to set the tension at the top.
  - **A combo move** — conditional, and it *escalates*. Something it can only do to a prone or restrained target. This is what makes a monster feel like it's telling a story rather than rolling dice.
  - **A finishing move** — the thing that scares them. *(Or, alternatively, the cornered-animal move: the one trick it pulls when it's losing and needs to turn the fight around.)*

**Hard limits (bosses excepted):**

| | Limit |
|---|---|
| Bonus actions | **One** |
| Reactions | **One** |
| Recharge abilities | **One** |
| Once-per-day powers | **One** |

Multiple bonus actions or multiple reactions is where a monster becomes genuinely painful to run.

**Two rules of thumb for "is this too complicated?":**
- **The proficiency-bonus rule:** *if it has more powers than its proficiency bonus, it's probably too complex for a standard monster.*
- **The word-count rule** *(surprisingly effective)*: **~200 words = a minion. ~400 = a standard monster. 500–800 = you are firmly in boss territory.** A rank-and-file monster with a 1,000-word stat block is a mistake.

**Scale by tier:** a low-tier monster needs a standard attack and *maybe one trait*. That's it. A mid-tier one can carry a bonus action, a reaction, two or three traits, and one or two interesting attacks.

**The minion/boss combo — the best trick in this section:**
> Give your **minions one cool thing** — say, *on a hit, they shove the target five feet.* Then give the **boss an area attack** that the minions are **shoving people into.**
>
> Simple minions. Simple boss. **The interaction between them is what's complex** — and it costs you nothing to run.

*(The framing they use: the minions are the starch; the boss is the gravy. You need both.)*

**On the numbers:** power ratings are famously unreliable — but they're **consistently** unreliable, so they remain useful as a **rough power ranking against other monsters.** Build so that yours sits sensibly alongside others at its tier, and you (and anyone else) can calibrate from experience.

### 4.7b Resolving an apparent contradiction

Two rules in this section look like they fight each other, and it's worth being explicit:

- **§4.4 says:** *don't build a one-trick pony — give it a mix of abilities so it can adapt.*
- **§4.7 says:** *don't give it many abilities — you'll never run them all.*

**They're the same rule, stated from opposite ends of a range.**

| | |
|---|---|
| **1 ability** | The giant with a club. Boring, predictable, nothing to react with. |
| **3–5 abilities** | ✅ **The target.** Enough to adapt and surprise; few enough to actually run. |
| **10+ abilities** | You will forget half of them, the fight will crawl, and it'll play worse than the 3-ability version. |

**The complexity budget IS the variety budget.** One great basic attack plus one to three signature moves gives you a monster that can open strong, escalate, and finish — **which is exactly the variety §4.4 is asking for.** The failure mode isn't "too many abilities" *or* "too few," it's **abilities that never get used** (too many) and **turns that are all identical** (too few). Three to five is the whole window, and it's also — not coincidentally — **how many turns the monster gets** (§4.7).

### 4.8 Play-testing (and the Monster Mash)

**Everything you wrote is a hypothesis until it's played.**

- **Adjust on the fly, shamelessly.** Keep a notepad. Hit someone twice and realise you've nearly dropped them when you didn't mean to? **Quietly lower the damage.** Fight dragging? **Shave hit points off.** They deleted it in round one before it did anything? **Add hit points.** Then write down what you changed.
- **Play-test with plain, vanilla characters — not optimised ones.** If the monster has to survive a min-maxed build, you'll learn nothing: **you'll just watch it die.** And **leave out the hard-control spells** (banishment, stun-locks, and their friends) for the test — if the monster gets removed from the fight on round one, **you didn't test it.** *(That's a perfectly good way for players to win a real fight. It's just not a play-test.)*
- **Don't design in an arms race** against player abilities. If you want a monster that can't be banished, just say it can't be banished. Don't contort the whole design around countering the party's toolkit. *(Same note as §1.13.13: you're not trying to win.)*

**The Monster Mash — the best play-test format, and almost nobody does it:**
1. Get a few DMs together. **Each one builds a monster.**
2. Make a set of plain pre-generated characters.
3. **Pass your monster to the DM on your left — and *they* run it while *you* fight it.**
4. Rotate.

**Why it's so valuable:** when you write a monster, you carry a huge amount of unwritten knowledge in your head — its tactics, how the moves combo, what it's *for*. **None of that is on the page.** Handing it to another DM is the only way to find out whether the stat block **communicates its own tactics at a glance.** If they read it and instantly play it the way you imagined, you've built something genuinely good.

**→ Our table:**
- **§4.7 is the section to actually live by, and our simplified system makes it *easier*.** Three to five rounds, three to five cool things. Our monsters should have **one great basic attack and exactly one signature move** — and that's a complete monster. The complexity budget isn't a compromise for us; it's the whole design brief.
- **The minion/boss combo is a gift for a table with kids.** Simple minions that shove + a boss with an area attack = the *kids* discover the tactic. That's a far better feeling than being told it.
- **"Its best move is not usable every round"** is the monster-side version of the autopilot turn, and it's how we make a fight tense without making it deadly. Not knowing *when the big thing comes back* is real pressure that costs nobody any hit points.
- **Weakness-as-puzzle (§4.4) is the single most kid-friendly idea here.** *"Fire isn't working. Why isn't fire working?"* is a nine-year-old leaning across the table. Twist one expectation per monster and let them crack it.
- **"Monsters never attack first"** is how we keep the non-violent solutions live — a monster with a *want* can be talked to, tricked, fed, or helped. Given our no-perma-death rule, we want those doors open anyway.
- **Reskinning (§4.6A) is our fastest content engine.** Our simplified stat blocks are small, so a new creature is genuinely minutes of work: take the shape, change the fiction, twist one thing.

## 5 · Encounters & Combat

> **"Kill or be killed isn't story — it's just math with dice."**

The central problem: combat is the most action-packed part of the game and it reliably becomes the most **monotonous**. And the usual fixes don't work. Adding more description doesn't fix it. Adding a deadlier monster doesn't fix it. *That's putting a top hat on a goblin — it looks fancy, but it's still a goblin.* The fix is structural.

### 5.0 The one question: what value shifts?

Borrowed from screenwriting, and it's the most useful single idea we have about combat:

> **Every scene must contain a turning point — a shift in values.** Hope → despair. Safety → danger. Shame → triumph.
> **If the emotional state at the end of a scene is the same as it was at the beginning, the scene failed.** Things can have happened. There can have been dialogue and movement and dice. It still failed.

**Most D&D fights don't pass this test, and that's why they feel hollow.** Because the game has rules, we spend our design effort on the rules — action economy, balance, CR. Those matter. But a fight built *only* out of those things **becomes maths rather than a story.**

**And players don't remember damage. They remember the moment everything changed** — the moment they realised the enemy wasn't who they thought.

#### The three steps

Ask, before you design any encounter: ***what is this scene about? What value shifts?***

**1 — The opening value.** What does the party actually *feel* walking in? Confident? Desperate? **Overconfident?** Don't guess — you've been running this story, so you know.

**2 — The closing value.** What should they feel walking out? Shaken. Victorious but at a cost. Terrified. **It must be different from where they started.** *Staying confident is not memorable.*

**3 — The pivot.** **What, inside the fight, makes that transformation happen?** This is the part people leave to chance, and you must not. **Design it.**
- *A worked example:* the lich sizes up the party, identifies the barbarian as the biggest physical threat, and casts **maze** on him — knowing his low Intelligence means he can't escape. The mechanical effect is almost beside the point. What it *says* is: **"I know your weaknesses, and I will exploit them."** That's the pivot, and it sets the mood for the whole fight.
- The pivot can be an enemy's move, the terrain changing, a countdown hitting zero. **Just make sure something triggers the shift.**

**The HP going down is a vehicle. The transformation is the cargo.**

### 5.1 The seven sins (diagnostics — what's going wrong)

**Sin 1 · The 20-minute turn.** The player starts thinking about their turn *the moment it begins*. They read their sheet, ask about distances, do maths aloud, change their mind, open the rulebook. Everyone else is on their phone.
- **Fix A — the on-deck rule.** At the start of each turn, the DM announces **who's up next**. That player now has a whole turn to get ready.
- **Fix B — batch your rolls.** Roll attack *and* damage together. Saves seconds per roll; compounds into whole sessions.
- *Caution:* making a **boring** turn faster just gets you to boredom sooner. Speed is necessary, not sufficient. Which leads directly to:

**Sin 2 · The autopilot turn.** *"I move up. I attack. Hit. Eleven damage."* Round two: same. Round three: same. **This is the root cause of most boring fights — every turn is the same turn.**
- *This isn't about banning a strong option.* If Eldritch Blast is the best move, fine — keep using it.
- **The DM's fix is to create dynamics in the fight:** put things in it players can *interact* with. Levers. A narrow bridge. A hostage. **A ritual with a clock that makes everything worse each round.** Interactive elements force players to *think about the fight*, and turn it into a puzzle rather than a queue.
- **The players' fix is variation in description.** Same spell, but round one you describe the *sound*, round two how it makes your character *feel*, round three how the target *reacts* when hit. Keep them short — pacing matters.

**Sin 3 · The zombie fight.** The outcome is decided. The party has clearly won, they're barely scratched — and the minion still has 40 HP, so everyone just keeps rolling. On, and on, and on. No pressure, no tension, no point.
- **Fix A — the next hit kills.** Just decide it. Describe the monster as bloodied and reeling *first*, so it doesn't look absurd when a 2-damage roll drops it.
- **Fix B — monsters can flee or surrender.** Then pivot into a **chase** or a **roleplay scene**. The goal is to get the table out of "we are now simply rolling forever" mode.

**Sin 4 · The empty room.** *"You enter a 30×30 stone room. There are four cultists. Roll initiative."* No cover, nothing to touch, nothing to describe — so it's just two rows of numbers hitting each other. Location is usually the *last* thing on a DM's priority list, well behind story and balance, and it shows: **when the space is boring, every fight starts to feel like every other fight.**
- **The fix: treat fights as *scenes*, and frame them.** Two halves:
  - **Cosmetics (set design).** **Stop giving measurements.** Replace them with evocative sentences. Use **one visual, one audible, one atmospheric** element. The 30×30 stone room becomes: *a dark library, old books drifting through the air, magic crackling audibly, and a dread that presses on you as you enter.* You can bring the grid out later — but the players must **feel the mood before they think about spell range.**
  - **Narration (why the fight exists).** Give them a **goal beyond "kill the dude."** Glyphs on the walls fill with red light every time the wizard steps on the floor symbols — and nobody knows what happens when they're full. Now the party can try to *occupy the symbols*, and the fight goes tactical.
- **Bonus:** verticality, obstacles, hazards. (Everyone loves shoving people off ledges. Steal it.)

**Sin 5 · The lonely boss.** The BBEG stands alone, gloriously. The party wins initiative, dumps everything on him, and the campaign villain dies in a single round. **This is pure action economy — it's just maths.** However powerful one creature is, four or five players get four or five times the actions.
- **The fix, in one line: stop thinking of a boss as a big monster. A boss is a *complete encounter*.** Minions, an environment that acts, reinforcements, phase changes.
- → **The full toolkit is §5.2, Principle 6.** (And the cheapest fix of all is the **minion/boss combo** at §4.7 — simple minions whose one trick feeds the boss's one trick.)

**Sin 6 · Whack-a-mole healing.** A player is at 6 HP and asks the cleric for a heal. The cleric says: *"Nah — I'll wait until you go down."* And **that is mechanically the correct answer**, because healing costs an action you could have spent on damage, and damage is how you win.
- This is a **values** problem, not a rules problem, so it needs a table conversation. If you care about the fiction, it's genuinely strange for a character to watch a bleeding friend, have the power to help, and decide to wait until they've got about eighteen seconds to live.
- **Fix A (DM-side):** don't punish generosity. If someone spends a turn healing a friend, quietly focus fire elsewhere.
- **Fix B (rules-side):** make **dropping to 0 cost something even if you're healed back up** — a level of exhaustion, or −5 maximum HP until a long rest. Then going down is no longer free, and healing early becomes rational.

**Sin 7 · The five-minute adventuring day.** The party nukes the first fight with every big resource they have, then barricades the door and takes a long rest. Then does it again tomorrow. Nova, rest, repeat.
- **The cause: no clock is running.** In old-school play, torches burned down, rations ran out, wandering monsters showed up. Most tables have dropped all that — and if you also run a purely narrative game, then **resources are paid for in time, and time costs nothing.** So the resources are effectively free and the game's balance evaporates.
- **The fix: put time back into the world.** A ritual that completes in three days. A prisoner executed at sundown. **Narrative clocks, not torch-tracking.**
- **You are not trying to beat the players** by making their abilities unusable. You're aligning the game's mechanical design with the story you're telling together.
- Then: **think in adventuring days, not individual fights** — and build a table culture where the players think that way too.

**The master problem behind all seven:** D&D is a game where you tell a story *and* use rules to do it, so the two things periodically grind against each other. A slow turn stalls the story. An empty room makes the story unmemorable. **Neglect the story and even the mechanics you love start to feel dull.**

### 5.2 Building a great fight (constructive — what to do instead)

> **A note on the three "put something in the room" rules**, which look repetitive and aren't. They're three different jobs, and you want all three:
> - **Sin 4 (the empty room)** — *frame* the space, and give the fight **a goal beyond "kill it."** → the fight has a **reason**.
> - **Sin 2 (the autopilot turn)** — put in things players can **interact with** (a lever, a chandelier, a hostage). → the players have **choices**.
> - **Principle 1 (below)** — make the environment **change over time**. → the fight has **momentum**.
>
> *Reason, choices, momentum. Different problems, different fixes.*

#### Principle 1 — The environment is a participant, not a backdrop

The mistake DMs make when they hear this is to write *more elaborate descriptions* — which players then politely ignore. **The secret isn't describing the environment. It's making the environment impossible to ignore.**

**The Three Tensions framework.** Every dynamic environment needs all three:

| | | Example (a forest clearing) |
|---|---|---|
| **Immediate dangers** | Can hurt or help *right now* | Uneven ground, exposed roots |
| **Developing threats** | Get worse over time | Fire spreading through dry underbrush |
| **Environmental shifts** | Change the whole battlefield | Wind changing direction — affects ranged attacks *and* the fire |

It works anywhere. *City street:* crowds and market stalls (immediate) / the city watch responding to the commotion (developing) / weather turning the cobbles slick and cutting visibility (shift). *Tavern basement:* low ceiling and stacked barrels / a damaged support beam / spilled ale making the floor slippery. **You don't need an exotic location — you need to think about any location deliberately.**

**The cause-and-effect chain.** This is what makes it feel real rather than arbitrary. Start with **one** significant change, then ask three questions:
1. What would this realistically affect **first**?
2. What systems would take **longer** to react?
3. What could go **catastrophically wrong** if it continues?

**Nothing should just "happen."** Each development must follow logically from the last. *A stray bolt breaks a coolant regulator → the temperature drops → frost spreads across the floor → the metal walkways above start to weaken → the glass dome stresses and cracks → escaping magical energy draws a storm → the storm sets off the defensive plants.* One broken thing, and a chain of consequences the players can see coming and try to get ahead of.

**Your action plan for any fight:** identify three physical features that could change → plan how one change would affect the others → work out what would realistically break or be disrupted → create clear but difficult choices → let each choice have logical consequences.

> The goal is not to build elaborate death traps. It's to make the environment feel **alive and responsive to the players' actions.**

#### Principle 2 — Enemies reveal character through action, not backstory

A fantastic dynamic setting full of enemies who might as well be training dummies is *sadder* than an empty room. But you don't fix this with backstory — **you fix it the way real people reveal themselves: through what they do, what they protect, and how they react when things go wrong.**

Four assassins walk in. Instead of "they attack, roll initiative":
- The **leader** moves with practised grace and immediately positions to cover both the party *and* their own companions. Commander's instinct.
- A **younger one** readies a crossbow with visible uncertainty, glancing at the leader for direction.
- An **old researcher** in wire-rimmed spectacles is barely interested in the fight at all — he's hurriedly dragging potted specimens away from the damaged regulator.

No exposition. You already know exactly who these three people are.

**And every player action is an opportunity to reveal more.** The fighter lands a hit on the leader: they step back, shift their stance to favour the uninjured side, and call out *"protect the mission"* — but there's strain in the voice now. And the young one's eyes go wide at the sight of their commander bleeding. **From one sword hit you've learned three things:** the leader is trained enough to adapt, focused enough to keep command, and connected enough to their team that their injury hits morale.

**Let enemies react to pain, to allies falling, to their plans collapsing.** Think about what each one *cares about* and let that drive them:
- A professional soldier pushes grimly through pain, still calling out tactics while bleeding.
- An unseasoned bandit panics after their first serious wound — fear finally beating greed.
- A wild animal gets *more* aggressive when hurt; instinct takes over.
- A wolf pack tells you everything about itself in how the alpha covers the others, how the young ones let eagerness beat caution.
- City guards: veterans automatically cover the exits; rookies bunch together for confidence.

**This may mean playing your enemies slightly sub-optimally.** Do it anyway. The leader abandoning a great tactical position to shield a wounded ally is worth more than the damage they'd have dealt.

#### Principle 3 — Layered stakes

Even great enemies feel hollow if nothing is actually at risk. **The rule is that *something beyond the fight itself* must be at risk.** Three layers is the fullest version — take as many as the fight can carry, and don't force all three onto a scrap with some bandits:

1. **Immediate stakes** — the fight itself, right now. Frost-slick floor means you fight for every step; one bad move slides you into the assassin's reach.
2. **Environmental stakes** — what breaks, and what that costs. The dome is cracking. If it goes, it doesn't just kill everyone inside; it releases decades of contained magical experiments into the city below.
3. **The bigger picture** — what this fight touches beyond itself. The diplomat is carrying the peace terms and must deliver them by sunrise.

**This scales all the way down.** A plain roadside ambush:
- *Immediate:* the bandits have blocked the road with felled trees, and the medicine in your wagon has to reach the village by nightfall.
- *Environmental:* storm clouds are gathering, and that dirt road behind you becomes an impassable mud slick in rain. Clear the blockade soon or be trapped.
- *Bigger picture:* you **recognise** one of the bandits — he's from the village you're heading to. So this isn't random, and something is badly wrong there.

**Give different NPCs different priorities.** The researcher cares about the plants. The diplomat cares about the peace. The assassins care about their cause. Those competing priorities generate drama on their own, and force genuinely interesting choices.

#### Principle 4 — Running it without grinding to a halt

All of the above is worthless if the table stalls. **Do not implement everything at once — that's the fastest route to overwhelm.**

- **Add exactly one narrative element per round.** Baseline: *"That hits. Take 8. Next."* Enriched: *"The blade finds its mark — 8 damage. You see them immediately settle back into a guard stance; the training shows in every movement. Next."* One detail. It adds flavour without breaking flow. Once that's automatic, add a second.
- **Use trigger moments** — pre-decided cues for when you add description, so you're not improvising constantly. Good ones: **initiative count 20** (top of the round), **after a critical hit**, **when someone crosses difficult terrain**, **when an NPC first takes damage.** Pick *one* and start there. Everything else stays standard combat.
- **Handle tactical discussion correctly.** When players are strategising, the action is effectively paused — there's no "make a battle plan" action in the economy. So **don't introduce new developments during the pause.** Instead, *reinforce what's already in play:* "While you plan, here's what you can all see — the crack in the dome above the leader, the frost making the eastern side hard to cross, the researcher still standing by the broken regulator." Keeps the scene vivid without breaking the action economy.
- **Build progressively across the fight.** Round one: just the frost spreading. Round two: how NPCs move on the ice. Round three: how they *react* to the environment. There is no rush.

#### Principle 5 — Every character gets a moment (the heist-movie problem)

> Imagine a heist film where every character has the same job. **Everyone is the getaway driver.** Nobody can crack a safe; nobody is bendy enough to hide in one. You wouldn't watch that movie.

**That is what D&D combat usually is** — every PC has the identical relationship to the fight: *see enemy, attack enemy, wait for next turn, repeat.*

**The fix: know what makes each character feel powerful, and design one moment per fight where that thing matters.** It can be purely mechanical:
- A grappler? **Fight near a cliff.** Let them throw someone off it.
- A paladin? Give them a strong undead commander worth smiting.
- The wizard just hit the level where their big area spell comes online? **Put a cluster of small monsters conveniently close together.**
- A bard? Include a creature that *talks*, so they can try to reason with it mid-combat.

**And the villain version of this is even stronger:** an antagonist who **focuses fire on one specific PC** — ignoring everyone else, using weapons built from that character's own designs — is a fight nobody at the table forgets. (See §5.2 Principle 2: this is character revealed through action, pointed at a *player*.)

> **The move is to turn "know your players" from a warm feeling into something that touches the mechanics.**

*(Note the convergence: this is the same principle as §1.10's "every player gets a ride" and §2.1's spotlight rule. Three unrelated sources, three different scales, one instruction — **make sure each person at the table gets their thing.**)*

#### Principle 6 — Escalate, because tension only falls on its own

Here's the structural problem nobody names. **Round one has real uncertainty** — nobody knows how this goes. Then **round two, the optimising starts.** They work out what's effective, and they repeat it until the expected outcome arrives. **Left alone, tension can only go down.**

So you have to actively fight that. **Steal the video-game move: the boss gets *more* dangerous as it gets *weaker*.** At a damage threshold, it transforms and gains new abilities.

Stop and notice how absurd that is — no real fighter punches harder in the final round as they tire. **And it works anyway**, because it makes the fight a *story*: the party thinks they're winning, victory looks close, and then the cards get reshuffled. **Players read a phase change not as a cheat but as "the monster revealed its true power."**

**Forms escalation can take:**
- **Phase transitions** at an HP threshold.
- **Waves of reinforcements** every few rounds.
- **Environmental shifts** — the tower starts collapsing, the floor becomes lava. *(= §5.2 Principle 1's three tensions.)*
- **Lair actions in ordinary fights.** Take the "initiative count 20" trigger and use it *outside* boss fights. A scrap in a warehouse? **Crates start falling at the top of every round.** This is the cheapest possible upgrade to a routine encounter.

#### End with a complication

A small trick with a big payoff: **finish the fight with a twist that opens the next scene.** The enemy escapes — *with information*. The artifact you recovered is cracked, and now unstable. It works like a **bonus phase** that hands you a clean transition into the next story beat.

#### The atmosphere layer

→ **See §8.1, Layer 2.** Music bound to the fight's turning point, not left running as wallpaper.

### 5.3 If your table just wants to roll dice and kick down doors

**That's completely fine, and the same principles still work — you just change the emphasis.**

A story-focused group worries about the peace treaty. An action-focused group gets: spreading magical frost as a tactical puzzle, a dome about to spectacularly collapse, unstable energies threatening explosive chain reactions. **Same structure, different payoff.** Even a plain bandit ambush: maybe the storm triggers a flash flood that could sweep everyone away; maybe those felled trees become an incredible battlefield *if they catch fire*; maybe the bandits are sitting on gunpowder barrels.

**The goal isn't to force story onto players who want action. It's to give them cooler things to interact with, and real consequences for their choices.**

### 5.4 The warning

If your *random encounters* feature enemies with genuine personality, clever tactics, and real motivation — **your campaign villain now has to clear that bar.** You cannot follow it with a generic overlord who makes obvious mistakes. (See §4.1: that's exactly what the seed question is for.)

### 5.4b The minimum viable good fight

If you take nothing else from this whole section:

> **One terrain feature someone can interact with, and one moment designed for a specific character.**

That's it. That alone lifts a fight out of maths homework, and it's about five minutes of prep.

### 5.5 Two definitions worth holding onto

**① "Encounter" ≠ combat.** *(The canonical definition. Referenced from §1.13.2, §6.2, §6.8.)*

> **An encounter is anything that taxes resources** — hit points, spell slots, exhaustion, time, goodwill, information, trust.

Three separate sources build entire systems on this: dungeons need **combat / puzzle / roleplay** encounters (§1.13.2), point crawls make **every point** an encounter (§6.2), and the TEST travel method colours them **red / yellow / blue** (§6.8) — *which are the same three types.* If you internalise one definition from this document, it's probably this one.

**② Ordinary people are a balance lever.** Don't over-stat the town guard (§1.7). If the militia could plausibly win, the heroes aren't needed — *that's the whole reason there's a quest.*

**Also see §1.13.10–1.13.12** for the resource side of encounter design — designing so that *holding back hurts* (loss aversion), making **retreat a costed trade-off** rather than a failure, and giving **resting** a real price. Those live with dungeons because that's where they bite hardest, but they apply to any fight.

**→ Our table:**
- **The on-deck rule is close to mandatory with kids.** A 9-year-old given a full turn's warning shows up with a plan; a 9-year-old put on the spot freezes and then guesses.
- **The autopilot turn is the kid failure mode.** "I hit it again" forever. The cure isn't nagging — it's **Sin 4's fix**: put a lever, a chandelier, a rickety bridge, or a rising water level in the room, and they'll invent things you'd never have thought of.
- **Sin 6 maps straight onto our no-perma-death rule.** Since heroes get *knocked out, captured, or rescued* rather than killed, going down is already consequence-light — which is exactly the condition that breeds whack-a-mole healing. **So give being knocked out a real cost** (Fix B): you come back groggy and weakened until you rest. Keeps the stakes real without ever killing a kid's character.
- **Layered stakes are how we get "real stakes" without danger of death.** The thing at risk doesn't have to be a hero's life — it can be the village's grain store, an NPC they love, or the bridge home. That's a *better* fit for our table anyway.
- **Enemies revealing character through action** is the kid-friendly version of villain depth: they don't need a monologue to understand that the big goblin went back for the little scared one.

## 6 · Travel & Exploration

### 6.1 The uncomfortable truth: *walking* is not interesting

Start here, because it saves a lot of wasted effort. **Walking is not a game mechanic.** D&D has no good rules about trudging, and no amount of lush description or swelling music turns a journey into gameplay.

**Both of our travel sources agree on the diagnosis, and it isn't "travel is under-simulated."**

> **The problem with D&D travel isn't that it's unrealistic. It's that it's boring.** So every piece of realism-enhancing advice — marching pace, rations, water, exhaustion, encumbrance, the metabolic requirements of a gnome — is answering a question nobody asked. **Most tables don't even track arrows.** There's a reason for that.

And the second villain: **random encounter tables.** A story assembled from random encounters is, definitionally, *a bunch of unrelated things happening for no reason.* Nobody wants a character with a backstory and a goal to die to a wolf pack that has nothing to do with the plot — and if the wolves *can't* kill them, then the fight was a pure waste of table time. **You don't pick your campaign's plot by throwing darts at a wall. Don't run travel that way either.**

**So there are two legitimate moves, and a bad one.**

| | |
|---|---|
| ✅ **Skip it** | Not every journey is a story. Describe it in a few sentences and move on. This is the correct default. |
| ✅ **Make it deliberate** | If the journey *is* the story, author it — as **choices** (§6.2, the point crawl) and as **events** (§6.8, the TEST method). |
| ❌ **Simulate it** | Miles per hour, rations, and a random table. This is what everyone hates, and it's what the books mostly offer. |

**The insight both sources land on:** the cinematic feeling people want from Lord of the Rings **does not come from the helicopter shots of people walking.** It comes from **crossroads** and from **the things that happen on the way.** Over the mountain, or through enemy land, or under it through the mines? Get off the road — take the ferry. *That's* the good part. Not the walking.

### 6.2 The point crawl

**A point crawl is a journey broken into points connected by paths** — essentially a subway map. You don't map terrain; you map *choices*.

- **Each point is an encounter** — and *encounter ≠ combat* (§5.5).
- **The paths are just lines.** All the fun lives in the bubbles.
- **When the players choose a path, they are choosing their encounter.** That's the whole trick. They're not being marched; they're deciding what kind of challenge they want to face.

*The canonical example:* the party is at point A and needs to reach point B, and how those connect isn't known. What *is* known is that three paths lead out — the mountain pass, the road through enemy land, and the mines. And each is a different **type** of challenge:
- the mountain → an extended skill challenge (exhaustion, no rest, damage on failures)
- enemy territory → stealth, and a brutal wave-combat if they blow it
- the mines → a straight dungeon crawl

### 6.3 The rule that makes it work: **informed choice**

This is the part people get wrong, and it's the crux.

> A novelist can spring surprises on their audience — the audience isn't playing the game. **Your audience is your players, so you are on the hook for informing their choices.**

A choice between three things they don't understand isn't a choice; it's a coin flip. So **tell them what kind of challenge each path is.** Not the specifics — you don't say "you'll take a level of exhaustion every day and can't long rest" — but you telegraph it hard: *"You can try the mountain, but people freeze to death up there. Your characters know that."*

**And give the essential information away for free.** If a PC has proficiency in History, they know nobody has heard from the dwarves in years and nobody knows why — **just tell them that, no roll.** That fact is *load-bearing* for the decision. Roll only for the *extra* detail (what specifically went wrong down there). Players will happily infer "abandoned underground complex + mysterious silence = dungeon" and pick with their eyes open.

**Rule of thumb: whatever a player needs in order to make an informed choice should be free. The dice are for bonuses, not for prerequisites.**

### 6.4 When a journey is worth doing at all

Only turn travel into a quest if **all three** are true. Otherwise, time-skip it.

1. **The area is genuinely dangerous.** If they're on a good road through safe country, there's no reason they can't just... arrive.
2. **There is no easy option.** Every route must cost something. If one path is safe and cheap, they'll take it every single time and you haven't written a choice, you've written a formality.
3. **You can imagine several fun encounters for each branch.** If a branch is empty, it isn't a real option.

### 6.5 Point crawls are absurdly versatile

Layer one on top of *any* structure and it becomes an exploration scene:

- **Sewers**, chasing a thief: the tunnel forks. One reeks; the other is cleaner but *louder*. Skill test or combat — you pick.
- **A mega-dungeon you never intended to fully map**: one wing leads to the frictionless hall, the other to the war pits. Two rooms, hours apart. Narratively a dungeon; mechanically, no dungeon-crawl needed.
- **A sea voyage**: north through waters where a hostile fleet is stopping ships, or south past a coast held by something worse.
- **A city** — point crawls are ideal for urban play.
- **Any wilderness**: carve it into named, evocative regions, give each a story, and make passing through them the journey.

**Bottlenecks are a feature.** You're allowed to make certain points *mandatory* — every route from A to B must pass through the Bloodstone Pass. That's where you put your midpoint climax, the beat where all threads converge. Required points are how a point crawl serves **story shape**.

### 6.6 Point crawl vs. hex crawl

| | **Point crawl** | **Hex crawl** |
|---|---|---|
| **Use when** | Exploration isn't the point — you want it out of the way so the *story beats* land | Exploration **is** the campaign; you want emergent, sandbox, open-world discovery |
| **Prep cost** | Very low — **you can build one in your head mid-session** | High — a whole hex map, populated with points of interest |
| **What it is** | An add-on, a plug-in | A **story engine**; a dedicated solution for a whole campaign |
| **Empty space** | Skipped. Every location is a beat. | Part of the point. |

Neither is better. Different tools. *(Note: actual-plays overwhelmingly use point crawls — because empty hexes get ignored in favour of a story beat at every stop.)*

**The improvised version:** you notice mid-session that the party is about to embark on a journey and you want it to matter. Just add one informed choice. *"You can cut through the haunted forest and arrive a week earlier — if you arrive at all."* Done.

### 6.7 Scale notes (from town design)

- Towns cluster roughly **a day's travel apart** (§1.7). Feeder villages sit within an hour to a day of a larger town. That's a usable map scale for laying out points.
- **"You must travel to get this"** is a clean way to gate powerful magic without banning it (§1.5) — and it's a ready-made point crawl.

**→ Our table:** point crawls may be the single most kid-friendly technique in this document. "Here are three roads. The mountain is cold and you'll get worn out. The swamp road is faster but something lives there. The tunnels are dark, and nobody's heard from the diggers in years. Which way?" A 9-year-old can hold that in their head, argue about it, and **own the consequence** — which is exactly the feeling we want. Informed choice matters double for kids: a choice they didn't understand feels like a trap, and feeling tricked is the fastest way to lose them.

### 6.8 The TEST method (Travel Event System) — a complete, usable system

Two years of play-testing, by a DM who *hated* travel and set out to build a version he'd enjoy. **It is radically simple, and it is the closest thing in this entire document to a drop-in system for our table.**

**Three design goals, in order:** it must be **exciting, not realistic**; it must be **simple**; and it must be **deliberate** — never random.

#### Step 1 — Throw away miles

**Distances have three values. That's it.**

| Distance | Events on the way |
|---|---|
| **Close** | **1** |
| **Far** | **2** |
| **Very far** | **3** |

The party experiences that many events, and then **they arrive.** Done.

How many long rests did they take? How many miles was it? Where was the sun? **It does not matter.** You are not converting inches to miles. You need to know one thing: *is it close, far, or very far?*

#### Step 2 — Author events in three colours

**The colours are just D&D's three pillars.** The DM *writes* these — they are never rolled.

- 🔴 **RED = combat.** But a fight **tailored to this place and to what's happened in the story so far.** Because you're authoring it rather than rolling it, it can carry actual plot — and it can show off the strange fauna of a world that isn't ours. It never has to be another pack of wolves.
- 🟡 **YELLOW = exploration.** The rickety bridge over the chasm. The killing desert. The spooky cave. This is where skill challenges live, where the environment takes centre stage, and where the DM gets to flex. **This is the "wow, I'm actually in a fantasy world" beat.**
- 🔵 **BLUE = roleplay.** Meeting someone new. Running into someone you know. Rivals, vendors, nobles in trouble, fellow adventurers — or the villain, taking a break to smell the flowers. **This is where most of the drama lives.**

#### Step 3 — Mix the colours

- 🟣 **PURPLE** = blue + red. The meeting with your rivals turns ugly.
- 🟢 **GREEN** = yellow + blue. Exploring the abandoned mines, you meet — and befriend — a lone kobold.
- ⚪ **WHITE** = *the players decide which pillar it becomes.* This is the best one.

**The white event, worked:** a hag ambushes the party in front of the rival circus troupe she's been hunting for years. It's **blue** if they try to talk her down. It's **red** if they fight her — or the troupe — or both. It's **yellow** if they bolt through the hallucinogenic pollen. **The DM prepared one event and the players choose what kind of scene it is.** *(This is §3.2's ignition point, applied to a whole encounter.)*

#### Step 4 — Bend it freely

The system isn't tied to real distance or real time, so **you can just adjust.** Make "close" two events if you want a meatier leg. **If the players are getting restless and want to *get there*, delete an event** — days give way to nights, and before long the spires of the city break the horizon. You're done. **You are not beholden to a ruler and a mileage conversion.**

**→ Our table: adopt this basically as-is.** It passes the "would a 9-year-old get this?" test on the first try — *close, far, very far* is the entire distance system, and "something happens three times and then you're there" is a structure a kid can hold in their head and even *anticipate*. The colours are a gift too: they let us **guarantee every kid gets their kind of fun on a journey** (the same logic as §1.10's "every player gets a ride"). And the **white event** is our best tool for teaching that talking, sneaking, and fighting are all real options — because the same prepared scene resolves whichever way they choose.

### 6.9 How the two travel systems fit together

They're not rivals — **they're different layers, and they stack.**

| | **Point crawl** (§6.2) | **TEST** (§6.8) |
|---|---|---|
| **Answers** | *Which way do we go?* | *What happens on the way?* |
| **Unit** | Points and paths | Events (red/yellow/blue) |
| **Gives you** | Meaningful, informed **choice** | Pacing and **content** |
| **Its rule** | The choice must be informed | The events must be authored, not rolled |

**Use both:** the point crawl gets them to *choose* the swamp road over the mountain pass, knowing roughly what each costs. Then TEST tells you the swamp road is **far** — so two events happen on it, and you write them. **Choice, then consequence.** That's the whole loop.

And note what they agree on, which is the real lesson: **the walking is never the point. The events and the choices are the point.**

## 7 · Running the Table

*A hub, not a chapter — the table-level rules that don't belong to any single topic. Most point back to where they're argued in full.*

- **Collaborative, not performative.** (§2.1 step 5.) The DM provides a *framework conducive to storytelling*, not a story.
- **Communicate expectations, especially tone.** Most "my players won't take it seriously" problems are unstated-expectation problems.
- **It's a game, not a simulator.** The innkeeper charging adventurers 50gp doesn't need to survive an economic audit. Don't let realism block fun — but *do* use realism when it *creates* fun (see the whole town section: the constraints are what generate plot).
- **Don't make them do tedious things to get to the fun thing.** If an NPC matters, telegraph them. (§1.4 q5.)
- **Let them chase the wrong thing.** The drunk butcher's boys can become the session. That's not a derail; that's the game working.
- **Read the table's first reaction and follow it.** How players instinctively respond to a person or a scene is *free intel* about the story they want. Build the next beat out of it. (§3.2, layer 5.)
- **Sometimes the best move is silence.** With asymmetric clues in play, the party will argue out a theory for twenty minutes while you just listen. Let the game run itself; take notes.
- **Design conditions, don't script outcomes.** Every technique in this document is a condition-setter. None of them tell you what happens.
- **Trust is load-bearing.** Roll in the open; announce the target number before they roll. If players suspect the dice don't really decide, their choices stop mattering to them. (§2.3)
- **"Yes, and" when they go somewhere unplanned.** Your prep is ammunition, not a rail. (§1.9, sin 5)
- **Ask "what does this mean?", not just "what happens next?"** (§2.3)
- **Honour your promises.** Mention a thing once = atmosphere. Twice = it should be playable. **Three times = you've made a promise.** A glowing sealed door that turns out to mean nothing teaches players that *nothing* you describe means anything. (§1.0, commandment 8)
- **Cash the cheque.** When players fixate on a throwaway NPC or a random shop, they're handing you a blank cheque — make it secretly important. They'll think you're a genius, and they'll start paying attention to everything. (§1.0)
- **The world keeps moving.** Advance a faction clock every session. Players can speed it up or slow it down; they cannot pause it. (§1.0, commandment 6)

## 8 · Prep Systems

### 8.0 Prep wider, not longer

The guiding principle. Prep isn't a checklist to survive — it's **a game you play by yourself before the game**. Each layer you add is a toy you get to build, which makes prep *more* fun and the session *more* immersive. And critically:

> **None of these layers control what happens. They design the conditions for something memorable to happen on its own.**

**Start from a SITUATION, not an OUTCOME.** "The party enters an old forest looking for a scout who's been missing three days" is a situation — it can go anywhere. "The party finds the scout's body and learns the cult did it" is an outcome, and you'll spend the session herding them toward it.

### 8.1 The four prep layers

Apply to a session or a scene. You don't need all four every time — **pick one and add it to the next game.**

**Layer 1 — TENSION PAIRS (the session's rhythm).**
Most sessions run at a **single emotional temperature** — three hours of tense dungeon, then a whole session of misty exploration. At the scene level that's fine. Across a whole session it **blurs together**, and blur is what a forgettable session is made of.

The cause: we spend all our prep on *what's in it* and none on the **shape** of it.

The fix: pick opposing pairs and deliberately shift between them.
- *quiet ↔ loud · funny ↔ sad · slow ↔ fast · epic ↔ introspective · safe ↔ exposed*

**Two to three shifts per session is enough.** Work on the *transition* — that's the bit that lands, like a chorus arriving in a song. And note what pairs really buy you: they force you to prep **the players' emotions**, not just the content. You're designing the feeling, which makes it far easier to actually produce that feeling at the table.

- *The forest, with quiet↔tense:* the deeper they go, the more the silence presses in — the birds *should* be singing and they aren't. Then: a branch snaps somewhere they can't see. Or worse, a sound that has no business in a forest at all.

**Layer 2 — SOUNDSCAPE (an accelerator, not wallpaper).**
Most DMs use music as **audio wallpaper**: search "fantasy ambient," press play, forget about it for three hours. (And then the epic battle track is still going during the tavern chat.) The deeper problem isn't the tone mismatch — it's that music is being treated as **decoration** rather than a **layer that does work**.

**The fix: bind the music to your tension pairs. You change the track when the shift happens.** Now the two techniques reinforce each other — the music makes the vibe shift land, and the vibe shift reminds you to change the music, because the music now has a *function*.

- *The forest:* low ambient wind and distant water for the quiet stretch. On the snapping branch, the sound effect — and then bring music in. Dark and heavy telegraphs undead; something ambient and strange telegraphs a puzzle. **What you play is a promise about what's coming.**
- *On workload:* three pairs = six tracks. But because you're working with **general emotions**, you build a small library once and reuse it across sessions. Compose something bespoke only for the scenes that really matter. We're not scoring a film.

*A minimum viable version, for combat specifically:* keep **three** tracks ready — **tense** (anticipation), **dynamic** (active combat), **dramatic** (the pivotal beat) — and shift at the moment things turn. When the dome finally shatters, that's the cue. **The music tells the players something significant has happened before you say a word.** One well-chosen cue at the right moment beats three hours of background noise.

**Layer 3 — ASYMMETRIC CLUES (the roleplay engine).**
When every player learns the same thing at the same time, the party moves forward as a **single block** — nobody needs to talk to anybody, so roleplay quietly dries up.

The fix: **make the information flow asymmetric on purpose. Treat clues as puzzle pieces, not blocks.**
1. Break the discovery into **several small pieces**.
2. Build **several different ways** to find them (so it isn't gated on one roll).
3. Deliberately hand **different pieces to different players.**

Now, if they want the whole picture, they *have to talk to each other*. They'll theorise, disagree, take sides — and this happens **on its own**, while you sit back and listen. What they say tells you exactly where to take things next.

- *The forest clearing:* instead of "you find a journal and a piece of cloth," the ranger finds a dagger wrapped in cloth in a tree stump; the sorcerer finds a journal page in the grass. The ranger knows the scout would *never* leave that dagger — so this is a setup, not a flight. The journal page says he was afraid. Between them they build a theory the DM never wrote: *he's coming back; we should wait here.*
- **You end up looking like a genius either way** — whether their theory is right (it's the scout) or *almost* right (it's four armed bandits, which is better).
- *Practical:* pick **one or two** key moments per session. Write the pieces on **little cards** and hand them over when the check succeeds. You're not writing a mystery novel — you're creating the conditions for a conversation.

**Layer 4 — TEXTURE (prep for the DM, not the players).**
The DM is a player too. Most prep is logistics and mechanics, and that's what drains the joy and ends in burnout. This layer exists **to get you excited**.

Write a handful of **power words or images** for a scene — not a script, an anchor. *One image, one smell, one sound* is a good starting spread; three or four per situation is plenty.

- *The forest:* fog thick enough to cling to the ranger's armour like a veil; a cathedral silence that demands you lower your voice.

The point isn't the prose — make it as plain or as flowery as *you* like. The point is that writing it and re-reading it before the session **pulls you into the world before your players get there.** And when you're already standing in it, everything else — describing, reacting, voices — gets easier. **It's your warm-up, and it turns prep from homework back into a game.**

### 8.2 The Town Sheet (reusable template)

Derived from §1.4 + §1.7.

> **⚠️ This is a checking tool, not a writing tool.** It is the most form-shaped object in this document, and therefore the most dangerous.
>
> **Invent the town first.** Find the one thing that makes it *this* town — the thing you'd tell someone about at a bus stop — and build outward from that. **Then** run the sheet over it and see what's missing.
>
> **If you fill this in from top to bottom, you will produce a town-shaped object with no soul**, and you'll produce a slightly different one every time forever. The failure is easy to spot: *every settlement has exactly one landmark and a feuding mayor and priest, because the form had slots for them.*
>
> **Blank rows are allowed.** A hamlet of forty people has no market, no guard, no wizard, and that *is* the interesting thing about it.

### 8.3 Order of operations

*The index to every framework in this document, in the order you'd actually use them.*

**World level:** What is this world arguing about? (one sentence) → What does it lack? → What did magic already break? → Three specific details per faction → Attach a need to every piece of lore → Give each faction a clock. (§1.0)
**Campaign level:** Tone → Concept → Setting → Conflict → Players. (§2.1)
**Campaign idea:** Premise (who/what/where) → Seed question → Villain as best wrong answer → Conflict web → PC stakes → Session-one stress test. (§2.2)
**Session level:** Situation (not outcome) → Tension pairs → Soundscape → Asymmetric clues → Texture. (§8.1)
**NPC level:** Want → Wound → Cover → Momentum → Ignition point. (§3.2)
**Journey level:** Is it worth running at all? → **Choice layer:** points & paths, informed choice, a bottleneck for the midpoint (§6.2, §6.4) → **Content layer:** close/far/very far → 1/2/3 authored events, coloured red/yellow/blue (§6.8).
**Fight level:** **What value shifts?** (opening feeling → closing feeling → the pivot that causes it) (§5.0) → Why is this fight happening (a goal beyond "kill it")? → Frame the scene (one visual, one audible, one atmospheric) → Three tensions in the environment → Give each enemy something they care about → **Give each PC one moment** → Three layers of stakes → **Build in an escalation** → Pick one trigger moment for description. (§5.2)
  *Short on time? **One terrain feature + one character moment.** (§5.4b)*
**Monster level:** Role first (minion / standard / boss) → check it doesn't already exist → compare 4–5 stat blocks at that tier → **reskin if you can, Frankenstein if you must** → **stay inside the complexity budget: one great basic attack + 1–3 signature moves, and that's all it will ever get to use** → play-test and adjust on the fly. (§4.5–4.8)
**Dungeon level** *(and remember: a "dungeon" is any **bounded space + sequence of challenges + a goal** — a heist, a haunted house, a ship, an exam, a negotiation. §1.13.0):*
Pick a **theme** (lies, death, greed) → pick a **mechanic that expresses it in the rules** → pick a **goal** (not always "kill the boss") → build **4–6 encounters** across combat / puzzle / roleplay, each pushing the mechanic → **end by colliding theme + mechanic**, so the finale tests what they learned. (§1.13.1–1.13.3)
Then, for each piece: forks that pass the 20-parties test → 2–3 clues per path → an explicit trade-off → every corridor poses a question → traps as puzzles, not gotchas → layered reveals → make holding back, retreating, and resting all *cost* something. (§1.13.4–1.13.12)
**Town level:** either start from *player questions* (fastest to playable) or from *DM questions* (better when building a region where towns must relate to each other). Both work; pick per town. (§1.4, §1.7)
**City level:** Pick a visual theme → build rides, not shops → make sure **every player has a ride** → stop before you get to the sewage system. (§1.10)

### 8.4 The "when do I stop prepping" test

Stop when the world **coheres** and the next session is **runnable**. Not when it's finished — it's never finished. "Build until you're comfortable, not until you're coddled."

### 8.5 ⭐ The clock — one tool, three scales

**The single most cross-cutting technique in this document.** It shows up independently in four sources at three different scales, and it's the same idea every time:

> **Something is getting worse, on a schedule, whether or not the party acts.**

| Scale | What ticks | What it fixes | Where |
|---|---|---|---|
| **World** | Each faction has a goal and a progress track. Advance one per session. | The world feels *alive* instead of waiting for the players. They can speed a clock up or slow it down — **they cannot pause it.** | §1.0, cmdt. 6 |
| **Adventure** | A ritual completes in three days. A prisoner hangs at sundown. | The **five-minute adventuring day** (§5.1, sin 7). Resources are paid for in *time* — so if time is free, the resources are free and balance collapses. |§5.1 |
| **Encounter** | The ritual advances every round. The water rises. The prisoners get closer to the pit. | The **autopilot turn** (§5.1, sin 2) and **hoarding** (§1.13.10). If holding back costs nothing, players will hold back — that's loss aversion, and it's rational. | §5.1, §1.13.10 |

**The three tests, one per scale:**
1. *What happens in the world while they're busy elsewhere?*
2. *Why can't they just take a long rest?*
3. *What happens if they hold back?* — **if the answer is "nothing, they just take longer to win," add a clock.**

**And the point of a clock is not to beat the players.** It's to make *time* a resource, so that spending, retreating, resting, and hurrying all become **decisions** rather than defaults.

### 8.6 The pre-session-one checklist (the cure for world-builder's disease)

The failure mode is well documented: **the prep becomes the hobby and the game never happens.** Months of building, and session one never arrives.

**This is genuinely all you need to start playing:**

- [ ] **One central tension** — a single sentence. *Something is at stake and the people who depend on it can't agree what to do about it.* (§1.0)
- [ ] **Two to four factions with incompatible goals** — each one a different answer to the tension. (§1.0, §2.2)
- [ ] **One limitation** that makes this world different from every other. *What's scarce, forbidden, broken, or gone?* (§1.0)
- [ ] **Three sensory details for the starting area** — one thing you can smell/hear/see, one custom, one object. (§1.0)
- [ ] **One mystery you don't know the answer to yet.** Genuinely don't decide. (§1.0)

**That's it. Everything else you build through play.** (§1.0, commandment 9)
